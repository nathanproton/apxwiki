    This article covers the county budget administered by the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors. For the separate municipal budget of the Town of Appomattox, see the Town Council.

    The Appomattox County budget is the annual fiscal plan adopted by the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors governing all county expenditures, revenues, and tax rates. The budget funds county government operations, the public school system, constitutional offices, social services, public safety, and debt service. It is prepared each spring by the county administrator, reviewed by the five-member Board of Supervisors, subjected to at least one public hearing, and adopted before the start of the fiscal year on July 1. Over the two decades from FY2005 to FY2025, the county budget nearly doubled from $27.4 million to $54.0 million, driven by school funding demands, infrastructure investments, state mandates, and inflationary pressures. The budget process has been shaped by contentious debates over real estate tax rates, the perennial tension between school funding and county services, and the challenge of governing a small rural county with a limited commercial tax base.[1]

    Background and budget process

    Appomattox County operates on a fiscal year running from July 1 through June 30, a schedule shared with all Virginia localities. The annual budget process typically begins in January or February, when the county administrator presents preliminary revenue projections and departmental requests to the Board of Supervisors. A series of budget work sessions follows in March and April, during which the five supervisors review line-by-line expenditures and negotiate with constitutional officers and the school board over funding levels. The board must advertise a proposed tax rate and budget, hold at least one public hearing, and adopt the final budget before July 1.[2]

    Virginia law requires that if a locality proposes a tax rate higher than the "equalized" rate — the rate that would produce the same revenue as the prior year after a reassessment — it must hold an additional public hearing specifically on the tax increase. This requirement has repeatedly extended the budget timeline in reassessment years. In 2008, a new law requiring thirty days of advance advertising in reassessment years forced the Board to postpone its budget hearing from March to late April.[3]

    The county budget encompasses all funds appropriated by the Board of Supervisors, including the general fund (which covers county government operations, constitutional offices, and the county's contribution to the school system), debt service, capital projects, and special revenue funds. The single largest expenditure category has consistently been public education, which has consumed between 55 and 65 percent of total county spending throughout the period covered by this article.[4]

    Early history (1990s–2003)

    The earliest budget records available in the Board of Supervisors minute books date to 1992, when the county was governed by a board consisting of Chairman W.E. "Bill" Jamerson, Margaret Beasley, Frank Carwile, Russell Moore, and Johnny Torrence. The county's budget in the early 1990s was modest by later standards; FY1994 budget deliberations, documented in the BOS minutes, reveal that Supervisor Carwile urged the board to adopt several guiding principles: stabilize county taxes, maintain cost consciousness, provide funds for public education, and develop a long-range plan.[5]

    County Administrator Dennis Gragg resigned in late 1993 to become executive director of the Central Virginia Planning District Commission in Lynchburg, beginning a period of administrative turnover that would see multiple administrators serve brief tenures over the next decade.[6] Gordon Rice served as county administrator until his resignation in mid-2003, followed by Darrell "Andy" Carroll, who announced his own resignation in December 2003 after accepting a position with Framatome Technologies in Lynchburg. Assistant County Administrator John Spencer served as interim beginning January 1, 2004.[7]

    The real estate tax rate stood at 66 cents per $100 of assessed value through the early 2000s. The last increase prior to FY2006 had occurred in 2000, meaning the county maintained a stable rate for four to five consecutive years. During this period the board took pride in holding the line on taxes, even as county expenditures grew incrementally with inflation and state mandates.[8]

    The November 2003 elections brought dramatic change to the Board of Supervisors, with three newcomers — Shawn Armbrust, Tom Conrad, and Willie Craft — joining incumbents Sammy Carter and Russell Moore. The turnover left only two experienced members on a five-person board, setting the stage for a new approach to county governance and budgeting.[9]

    Stability and first tax increases (FY2005–FY2008)

    FY2005: fourth year with no tax increase

    The FY2004–05 budget marked the first full cycle prepared under the new board composition and the newly returned County Administrator Aileen Ferguson, who had been re-hired in March 2004 after serving in Prince Edward County during a four-year absence. Ferguson had originally served as county administrator from 1993 until her resignation in June 2000 amid political turmoil.[10]

    Ferguson's proposed budget totaled $27.4 million, more than $1 million less than the previous year's budget. The Board voted 5–0 to maintain the real estate tax rate at 66 cents per $100 of assessed value, marking the fourth consecutive year without an increase. Chairman Sam Carter praised the efforts of Ferguson and the board, noting that state and federal mandates had made it increasingly difficult to avoid raising taxes. Supervisor Armbrust cautioned, however, that continued budget growth would eventually force a rate increase.[1]

    The school system's share of the budget was a point of contention from the outset. The proposed budget allocated $17.3 million to the Appomattox County School Board, but the school board was requesting $18.2 million — a gap of nearly $900,000. School Board member Amy Martin argued that the additional funds were needed to address salary inadequacies and meet students' needs. The school board ultimately adjusted its request downward by $921,769 after receiving increased state and federal allocations, approving a final budget of $18,210,217.[11]

    FY2006: first real estate tax increase in five years

    The FY2005–06 budget brought the county's first real estate tax increase since 2000. The Board proposed a six-cent increase, raising the rate from 66 cents to 72 cents per $100 of assessed value. The increase was projected to generate approximately $400,000 in annual revenue and was necessary to balance a $29.1 million budget — nearly $2 million more than the previous year.[12]

    Carter attributed the increase to the construction of a $600,000 animal shelter and the establishment of an E-911 call center, both of which added significant costs. He expressed hope that the increase might prevent future rate hikes, noting that a general reassessment scheduled for 2007–08 would reset property values and could provide additional revenue capacity.[13]

    At the public hearing on May 16, 2005, several citizens spoke in opposition to the increase, while others acknowledged it as overdue. The board held additional budget workshops before adopting the final budget. The county's general fund reserve at that time stood at $4.3 million, representing 17 percent of the total budget — a healthy cushion by rural Virginia standards.[14]

    FY2007: personal property tax raised for first time since 1974

    The FY2006–07 budget brought a different kind of tax increase. Rather than raising real estate rates, the Board proposed a $1.10 increase in the personal property tax, from $3.50 to $4.60 per $100 of assessed value — the first personal property tax hike in thirty-two years, since 1974. The real estate rate and merchant's capital tax were held steady. The total budget grew to $31,897,468.[15]

    The largest appropriation went to the school system at $20,942,924, approximately $200,000 less than the school board had requested. Chairman Carter defended the personal property increase as the fairest option, noting that it distributed the burden more broadly than a real estate increase would. Commissioner of Revenue Monte Mays confirmed the rate had not been raised since 1974, underscoring the county's long track record of fiscal restraint. The personal property increase was expected to generate more than $700,000 in new revenue.[15]

    At the public hearing on May 22, 2006, only two citizens spoke — School Board Chairman Bobby Waddell and Superintendent Walter Krug, both of whom thanked the supervisors for supporting the schools while noting that Appomattox produced average academic results with well below average per-student spending.[16]

    FY2008: five-cent hike and rising mandates

    The FY2007–08 budget continued the upward trajectory, reaching $33.5 million — a $1 million increase over the prior year and $4.3 million more than two years earlier. The Board proposed a five-cent increase in real estate taxes, raising the rate from 72 cents to 77 cents per $100 of assessed value. Carter cited state mandates and inflationary costs as the primary drivers.[17]

    The supervisors cut the school board's budget request by approximately $250,000 in local funds. The school system still received the minimum necessary to qualify for maximum state funding, but teacher raises became more difficult to budget. The board also did not calculate an impact for the proposed Land Use program, which was under active discussion at the time. A public hearing was held April 16, 2007, at the Appomattox Community Center.[17]

    The 2008 reassessment and its aftermath

    FY2009: post-reassessment budget

    The general reassessment of Appomattox County real estate, conducted by Wingate Appraisal Service and effective January 1, 2008, fundamentally altered the county's fiscal landscape. Property values increased by an average of 78 percent, with home values rising approximately 30 percent and land values more than doubling. Some agricultural parcels saw increases of 200 to 300 percent. The reassessment automatically equalized the tax rate downward from 77 cents to approximately 43 cents per $100 of assessed value — the rate that would produce the same total revenue as before.[18]

    The Board proposed a $37.2 million budget for FY2008–09 and advertised a rate of 49 cents, which represented a five-cent increase above the equalized rate. After a public hearing in which citizens urged restraint and several advocated for a Land Use ordinance to protect farmers, the Board adopted a rate of 44 cents — just one cent above equalization — by a split vote. One supervisor cast a protest vote against any increase. The final budget allocated $22.5 million to the school board, which was $275,000 less than requested but still represented a $1.7 million increase over the prior year.[19]

    The reassessment's impact on individual taxpayers varied dramatically. Many homeowners saw their tax bills remain stable or decrease, because the lower rate offset the higher assessments. But farmers and large landowners faced steeply higher bills. Concord resident Tom Schmutz addressed the Board, noting a 346 percent increase in his property assessment. Two citizens — Robert E. McClenny Jr. and Joseph Terry — filed lawsuits against the county, alleging their land values had been overestimated. County Attorney J.G. Overstreet moved to dismiss the suits, arguing the plaintiffs had failed to exhaust administrative remedies through the Board of Equalization.[20]

    The Land Use ordinance

    The reassessment made the Land Use ordinance an urgent priority. A Land Use program allows qualifying agricultural, forestal, or open-space land to be assessed at its use value rather than fair market value, offering significant tax relief to farmers. Commissioner of Revenue Monte Mays had warned as early as 2005 that land values could triple in the next reassessment without such a program, and urged the Board to adopt the ordinance by the state-mandated deadline of one year before the reassessment.[21]

    The Board initially rejected the Land Use ordinance in 2007, partly due to confusion about the adoption timeline. After the reassessment confirmed the dramatic increases in land values, the Board reconsidered. In June 2008, the supervisors voted 4–1 to approve the Land Use Taxation ordinance, granting tax relief to farmers owning five or more acres that produced at least $1,000 in annual revenue. The decision came after the Board learned it had been misinformed about whether the ordinance could only be adopted in the year before a reassessment. The ordinance took effect for the following tax year, providing retroactive relief to landowners who had faced bills two to three times their prior amounts.[22]

    Recession and austerity (FY2010–FY2014)

    FY2010: ten-cent tax increase and deepening recession

    The Great Recession hit Appomattox County's budget with full force in FY2009–10. Preliminary figures revealed revenues of $38.3 million against projected expenses of $39 million, creating a shortfall of approximately $700,000. County Administrator Ferguson attributed the gap to the economic downturn and the costs of new projects already underway.[23]

    The Board voted 3–2 to recommend a $40.3 million budget with a ten-cent real estate tax increase, raising the rate from 44 cents to 54 cents per $100 of assessed value — the largest single-year increase in the period covered by this article. Supervisors Sam Carter, Gary Tanner, and Russell Moore voted in favor; Tom Conrad and Willie Craft voted against. The increase generated significant public opposition: six citizens spoke at the public hearing, four of whom urged a lower rate. One speaker's remarks were described in the press as racially charged.[24]

    The Board was also navigating cuts from the state. Governor Tim Kaine's budget reductions had forced the county to take a cautious approach to all spending, including delaying $135,627 in funding for the county waterline project. Despite the fiscal constraints, newly elected Board Chairman Gary Tanner insisted the Board remained committed to the waterline.[25]

    FY2011: schools short-funded for first time

    The FY2010–11 budget was adopted at $37.19–$37.3 million, a reduction from the prior year's $40.3 million peak. The Board proposed maintaining current tax rates with no increase. However, the county's allocation to the school system — $3.6 million — fell $521,000 short of the amount needed for the schools to qualify for full state-matching funds. This marked the first time in county history that school funding had been short-funded below the state match threshold. The budget was approved on a 4–1 vote, with some supervisors attributing the shortfall to state funding cuts over which the county had no control.[26]

    The consequences for the school system were severe. The School Board approved an $18.7 million budget on a 3–2 vote, eliminating twenty positions. Programs including the Algebra Readiness initiative and K-3 Class Size Reduction were cut or reduced. The School Board requested the supervisors reconsider and allocate the full $4.3 million originally sought, but the Board of Supervisors declined.[27]

    FY2012–FY2013: rising rates and level funding

    The FY2011–12 budget was set at $37.88–$37.9 million and included a 1.5-cent increase in the real estate tax rate. The increase was sufficient to bring school funding to $4.2 million, allowing the school system to receive full state-matching funds for the first time in two years. The budget was approved 3–0, with two supervisors absent. Some board members expressed the view that the 1.5-cent increase was insufficient to address the county's accumulated needs.[28]

    The Board also adopted a resolution requesting the Virginia General Assembly reverse $60 million in statewide budget cuts that had shifted costs to localities over the prior year, criticizing the state's policy of requiring local governments to subsidize state reductions without corresponding authority to raise revenue.[29]

    The FY2012–13 budget brought a more substantial eight-cent real estate tax increase. The school budget was approved at $19.6 million, including a two-percent cost-of-living raise for employees — the first meaningful raise in several years. The county budget also had to absorb increased contributions to the Virginia Retirement System.[30]

    By contrast, the FY2013–14 budget held the line. The Board unanimously agreed on a $36.4 million budget with no tax increases — the first no-increase year since FY2011. The budget included funding for three school resource officers, a three-percent salary increase for county employees, and level-funding of $4.8 million for the school system. The schools had requested $5.7 million, leaving a $900,000 gap.[31]

    FY2014: the clerical error and structural deficit

    The FY2014–15 budget cycle was one of the most contentious in the county's modern history. The Board confronted a structural deficit exceeding $1 million, driven by three converging factors: an $890,000 increase in the school system's budget, a $600,000 revenue shortfall caused by a clerical error in budget accounting, and approximately $300,000 in reduced revenues from a reassessment that lowered some property values. A six-cent real estate tax increase was proposed to close the gap.[32]

    In May 2014, the Board voted unanimously to set a September deadline for identifying more than $500,000 in budget cuts, with enforcement beginning October 1. The vote triggered alarm among department heads and staff. Just weeks later, three supervisors — Willie Craft, Gary Tanner, and Ronnie Spiggle — requested an agenda item to rescind the vote. The resulting June 2 meeting was one of the most acrimonious in recent memory, with Supervisors Sam Carter and Sara Carter repeatedly questioning why the other three had added the item to the agenda just days before the meeting.[33]

    The Board voted 3–2 to rescind the September deadline, with Craft, Tanner, and Spiggle in the majority. Craft stated he believed the Board had acted in haste, while Spiggle warned against eliminating eighteen to twenty positions in a knee-jerk reaction. By the end of FY2014, preliminary figures showed expenses coming in $700,000 under budget — a welcome reprieve that County Administrator Ferguson reported to the Board in July.[34]

    The events of FY2014 accelerated the transition in county administration. Ferguson, who had served as county administrator since returning to the position in 2004 (and originally from 1993), announced her retirement. The Board hired former Campbell County Administrator David Laurell as a consultant to manage the search for her replacement.[35]

    FY2015: the nine-cent debate

    The FY2015–16 budget brought another difficult tax increase. The Board originally advertised a nine-cent real estate tax increase to close a $540,000 shortfall in a $37.3 million budget. A February 2015 presentation by financial advisor Davenport & Company outlined the county's long-term fiscal position, noting that nearly all county debt would be paid off within ten years, giving the Board future borrowing capacity. Supervisor Sam Carter observed that even $20 million in new borrowing might not be sufficient for the county's accumulated capital needs, including an estimated $30 million in upgrades at Appomattox County High School.[36]

    Ultimately, the Board voted 5–0 to impose a five-cent increase instead of nine, raising the real estate rate to 65 cents. To make up the difference, the Board raised the personal property tax rate and increased the vehicle license tax from $25 to $35 per vehicle. The personal property rate was restructured from $4.60 per $100 at 50 percent of NADA value to $5.00 per $100 based on 100 percent of loan or trade-in value, generating an additional $314,000 annually. The vehicle license increase added $136,000. Together these measures closed most of the gap, leaving the Board roughly $90,000 short of the target — a total that could be offset through further rate adjustments or a draw from the operating reserve.[37]

    The tax increase and restructuring reflected a new era: Susan M. Adams, appointed unanimously as county administrator in February 2015 to replace the retiring Ferguson, brought a fresh management approach that would define the next decade of county finances.[38]

    The Adams era and stabilization (FY2016–FY2020)

    FY2017: debt refinancing averts tax increase

    Under Adams's management, the FY2016–17 budget achieved something the Board had been unable to do in several years: a $39.4 million balanced budget with no tax increases. The Board had originally expected to need a three-cent real estate tax increase, but a debt refinancing plan changed the calculus. On May 4, 2016, the Board voted 5–0 to approve both the budget and a refinancing resolution seeking a tax-exempt loan of $7.8 million to refund a portion of outstanding bonds.[39]

    The refinancing eliminated a $400,000 debt service spike that would have been due in FY2017, enabling the county to maintain its tax rates: real estate at 65 cents, personal property at $5.13 per $100 (50 percent NADA value), machinery and tools at $4.60 per $100 (12.5 percent of initial cost), merchant's capital at $1.00 per $100 (85 percent of original cost), and vehicle license tax at $35 per vehicle. No citizens spoke at the public hearing. Chairman Carter praised the collaborative effort between the Board and county staff.[39]

    The school system received $5.74 million in local funding, a $120,000 increase over the prior year. School Finance Director Bruce McMillan noted it was the smallest additional request he could remember in many years, made possible by higher-than-expected state revenue. The school budget funded a staff raise, new teacher positions, health cost offsets, an assistant principal for the primary school, and three new school buses.[40]

    FY2019: county administrator's raise and fiscal health

    The FY2018–19 budget totaling $40,513,471 was approved unanimously on June 29, 2018, without raising taxes on real estate, mobile homes, personal property, or machinery and tools. It was the latest in a string of no-tax-increase budgets under Adams's stewardship.[41]

    The budget drew public attention primarily because it included a $20,000 raise for County Administrator Adams, increasing her salary from $107,000 to $127,000. Supervisor Watkins M. Abbitt Jr. — a former state delegate who had joined the Board after decades in the General Assembly — championed the raise, noting that Adams had accumulated nearly $10 million in county savings and was performing double duty as both county administrator and de facto economic development director after the previous director's retirement. Even at $127,000, Adams's salary remained lower than all comparable county administrators in the region, and below the salaries of the retiring school superintendent ($140,106) and the Commonwealth's Attorney ($138,925).[42]

    FY2020: no tax increase, rescue squad concerns

    The proposed FY2019–20 budget of $41,053,037 was presented for public hearing in May 2019 with no tax increases — the fourth consecutive no-increase year. Chairman Carter praised the staff for delivering another balanced budget. However, the hearing revealed an emerging tension: Appomattox County Rescue Squad Captain Susan Walton questioned why the squad's allocation had been cut from $100,000 to $56,000, nearly half the prior year's level, despite the squad responding to 2,551 calls in the county the prior year. Walton noted the squad had already spent a quarter-million dollars on a new ambulance and facility repairs and needed to replace aging heart monitors.[43]

    COVID-19 and fiscal expansion (FY2021–FY2025)

    FY2021: pandemic budget

    The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted the FY2020–21 budget process in ways no one anticipated. In March 2020, the projected state revenue for Appomattox County Public Schools alone was $17.3 million, with $5.9 million requested from the county. By April, the economic impact of Governor Ralph Northam's shutdown orders became clear: the state reduced its revenue allocation by $360,000, and in June the Board of Supervisors cut its contribution to the schools by an additional $397,000, for a combined reduction of $758,000. School Finance Director Bruce McMillan called FY2021 one of the "strangest budget years" the division had experienced.[44]

    Despite the disruptions, the Board approved the FY2021 budget at $41,932,881 on June 25, 2020. A public hearing on June 15 drew no public comments. The board also held a separate hearing to amend the FY2020 budget for the purpose of retiring water revenue bond debt from the 2011 series.[45]

    FY2022: recovery and growth

    The FY2021–22 budget marked a return to growth. Finalized on March 29, 2021 after multiple work sessions, the budget was adopted by unanimous vote at $43,850,902 — a $1.9 million increase over FY2021, and the first time the county budget exceeded $43 million. Estimated general fund revenues were $20,876,828. The budget reflected improving economic conditions and increased state funding as pandemic-era federal relief flowed through to localities.[46]

    The school system's operating budget for FY2022–23 reached $29,705,785, with approximately $21.5 million from the state, $6.7 million in local funding, $1.3 million in federal funds, and $125,000 in other school-based revenue. Projected student enrollment (Average Daily Membership) was 2,235, an increase of 12 over the prior year's estimate.[47]

    FY2025: $54 million and the school funding dispute

    The FY2024–25 budget represented a dramatic expansion in county spending. On May 29, 2024, the Board of Supervisors approved a budget of $53,991,082 by a 3–2 vote — the narrowest margin for a budget adoption in recent years. The budget included funding increases for the Sheriff's Office, the Volunteer Fire Department, and the Rescue Squad, and had to absorb a 10 percent increase in county employee health insurance, a 3 percent cost-of-living adjustment, real estate reassessment expenses, and inflationary costs for supplies and services.[48]

    The most contentious element was school funding. Appomattox County Public Schools received $32,607,081, representing approximately 61 percent of total county expenditures. Virginia's Required Local Effort for FY2025 was $5,809,366; the county funded $7,286,908 — some $1,477,542 above the required match, or 34 percent of local tax collections. Nonetheless, the school system had requested an additional $350,465 that the Board declined to fund. Superintendent Annette Bennett warned that losing this amount would cost the district roughly $1 million in state incentive funding, potentially forcing cuts to the Virginia Preschool Initiative, the K-3 Primary Class Size Reduction Program, and Infrastructure and Operations Per Pupil allocations.[49]

    At the June 17 Board meeting, six citizens spoke during public comment, most expressing disappointment in the funding decision. Retired educator Pat Torrence recalled that Appomattox County had been among the first in Virginia to establish a four-year-old preschool program in the mid-1970s, and criticized the Board for failing to support education adequately. Former state delegate and Board member Watkins Abbitt Jr. offered a contrasting view, praising the Board's fiscal responsibility and noting that Appomattox ranked as one of the best-managed counties in the state.[50]

    By late 2024, County Administrator Adams had served ten years in the role, during which time she was credited with generating a balanced budget every year and helping Appomattox County achieve a bond rating superior to most comparable localities. The current-year budget was reported at $54 million.[51]

    School funding as a perennial tension

    No single issue has dominated Appomattox County budget debates more consistently than the level of local funding for the public school system. Virginia's funding formula requires localities to provide a "Required Local Effort" (RLE) to match state funding, and many counties contribute well above the minimum. Appomattox has historically hovered near the lower end of above-RLE contributions, a source of ongoing friction between the Board of Supervisors and the School Board.[52]

    In FY2015, Appomattox ranked last among Region 2000 localities in percentage above the Required Local Effort and in the middle compared to other Region 8 School Divisions. School Board members and superintendents have repeatedly argued that the funding level constrains teacher salaries, limits class sizes, and prevents the district from competing effectively with surrounding counties for qualified staff. In FY2005, Superintendent Walter Krug highlighted that experienced teacher salaries in Appomattox fell behind comparable wages in the area, a refrain that would be echoed by subsequent superintendents for two decades.[53]

    The tension reached its most acute points in FY2011, when the county short-funded the state match for the first time in history, forcing the elimination of twenty school positions; in FY2014, when a $900,000 gap between the school board's request and the county's allocation led to difficult choices; and in FY2025, when the denial of $350,465 in additional funding threatened the loss of $1 million in state incentives. In each case, supervisors pointed to limited revenue, competing demands from other county services, and their obligation to all taxpayers, while school officials emphasized the long-term consequences of underfunding education in a county whose primary competitive asset is its school system.[26][49]

    Revenue sources and structural challenges

    Appomattox County's revenue base presents fundamental challenges for budget makers. As a rural county with approximately 16,000 residents and limited commercial development, the tax base is heavily dependent on real estate and personal property taxes paid by individual homeowners, farmers, and small businesses. The county lacks the large commercial corridors, industrial facilities, or institutional employers that provide diversified revenue streams to more urban localities.[1]

    The arrival of Walmart in 2010 added a significant commercial taxpayer, but supervisors and citizens alike recognized that a single retailer could not transform the county's fiscal position. Letters to the editor and public commentary throughout the 2000s and 2010s repeatedly called for economic development to broaden the tax base and relieve the burden on homeowners. In 2009, citizen Ray Wingfield made an impassioned case for hiring an Economic Development Director, arguing that attracting businesses would shift a portion of the tax burden away from residential property owners.[54]

    The county considered a meals tax in 2015, which could have generated significant revenue. The Board of Supervisors voted 4–1 to place the matter on a voter referendum in the November general election, but voters rejected the measure. Virginia law requires county meals taxes to be approved by referendum, unlike towns, which can impose them by council vote. The Town of Appomattox already levied an 8 percent meals tax — the highest rate in the state as of 2016.[55]

    The announcement of the AVAIO data center project in December 2024, a $3 billion hyperscale campus on 452 acres in the Appomattox Center for Business and Commerce industrial park, represented a potential transformative shift in the county's revenue picture. The project's estimated $17 million annual tax impact, if fully realized, could fundamentally alter the budget dynamics described in this article.[56]

    County administrators and fiscal management

    The county administrator serves as the chief executive officer of county government and is responsible for preparing the annual budget, managing day-to-day operations, and implementing the Board of Supervisors' policy decisions. The position's stability has directly affected budget quality and fiscal management.

    The period from 2000 to 2004 saw rapid turnover: Aileen Ferguson resigned in June 2000 amid political pressure; Gordon Rice served approximately sixteen months before resigning in mid-2003 without stating specific reasons; and Andy Carroll resigned in December 2003 after less than a year. This instability coincided with the Board's ability to maintain a stable tax rate, but left the county without long-term strategic fiscal planning.[7]

    Ferguson's return in March 2004 brought a decade of continuity. She guided the county through the 2008 reassessment, the Great Recession, and the contentious budget cycles of 2010–2014. Her management style emphasized caution: maintaining healthy reserves (the general fund reserve reached $4.3 million, or 17 percent of the budget, by 2005) while accommodating incremental tax increases when necessary. She announced her retirement in late 2014 after a twenty-year career in the position (with a four-year hiatus).[10]

    Susan Adams, appointed in February 2015, brought a focus on fiscal efficiency and debt management. Within three years, the county's savings approached $10 million, and the debt refinancing strategy she helped develop in 2016 averted a tax increase. By 2024, she had delivered a balanced budget every year of her tenure and was credited with helping the county achieve one of the best bond ratings among comparable Virginia localities. Her salary of $127,000 (as of 2018) remained below all comparable county administrators in the region.[42][51]
