    The Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce is a nonprofit business association that promotes commercial, industrial, agricultural, and civic interests in Appomattox County, Virginia. Established around 1967, the Chamber has served for more than half a century as a bridge between local businesses, government agencies, and community organizations, working in partnership with the Town of Appomattox, the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors, and Region 2000 to foster economic development and tourism.[1] The Chamber is best known for organizing the Appomattox Cheerz outdoor concert series, presenting the annual Citizen of the Year award, and awarding a scholarship to a local graduating senior each year. As of 2016, the Chamber had approximately 145 member businesses and operated on an annual budget of roughly $26,000.[2]

    History

    Founding and early decades

    The Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce was established around 1967. A 2006 Times-Virginian article on the Chamber’s relocation noted that the organization had been assisting town and county officials with Region 2000 in promoting business and economic development since that year.[1] The earliest documentary evidence of the Chamber in county financial records appears in 1992 Board of Supervisors minutes, which list a budget line item of $10,000 for the Chamber of Commerce under the county’s contributions to outside agencies.[3] The Chamber predated the more recent organization by several years; a 1965 Town Council minute references the “Appomattox Junior Chamber of Commerce,” a Jaycees chapter that was a separate entity from the county Chamber proper.[4]

    For most of its early history, the Chamber operated out of the Visitors Center in the Train Depot building on Main Street in the Town of Appomattox. The town owned the Depot building, but the Chamber managed and staffed the Visitors Center on the town’s behalf, greeting tourists, distributing information about the county, and coordinating with the Appomattox Court House National Historical Park to direct visitor traffic.[5] During this period, the Chamber was primarily a volunteer-run organization, with an executive secretary handling day-to-day administrative duties and a board of directors providing strategic direction.

    Early 2000s expansion

    The early 2000s were a period of increased activity for the Chamber. Under the leadership of presidents Brett Thompson and his successor Penny Taylor, who was elected in October 2003, the Chamber expanded its event programming significantly.[6] Taylor, who had joined the Chamber’s board of directors only months earlier in February 2003, rapidly became involved in events such as Friday Cheers and the September 2003 Civil War Reenactments before being elected president. The Chamber’s executive secretary during this period was Valarie Garrett, who managed operations, coordinated events, and presented the annual citizenship awards.[7]

    In March 2004, the Chamber hosted its first-ever Trade Show at the Appomattox Community Center, allowing more than twenty local businesses to showcase their products and services to the public.[8] The Chamber also sponsored the Miss Appomattox Festival Pageant, which offered contestants more than $9,500 in prizes, including an $8,000 scholarship to Liberty University.[9] In 2005, the Chamber took on a prominent advocacy role when its board of directors voted by a significant majority to circulate petitions for a voter referendum on “liquor by the drink” in Appomattox County, arguing that allowing mixed-drink sales at local restaurants would promote economic growth.[10] The Board of Supervisors unanimously endorsed the petition effort, although the Town Council rejected a similar resolution on a split vote.[11]

    Jackie Drinkard succeeded Taylor as Chamber president in October 2004 and served through 2006. Drinkard, who had spent a year as vice-president before assuming the top role, emphasized member services and community dedication.[12]

    Loss of the Depot and relocation (2006)

    In January 2006, Chamber officials learned at a Town Council meeting that the town intended to take control of the Visitors Center at the Depot as early as July 1 in order to install a new tourism director. Town Manager David Garrett explained that the tourism director would be responsible for coordinating and operating the Visitors Center, displacing the Chamber from the space it had occupied for decades.[5] Mayor Ronnie Spiggle provided historical context, noting that the town had originally allowed the Chamber to run the Visitors Center simply because the town chose not to take on the responsibility at the time.

    The Chamber relocated to the old Courthouse building at 331 Court Street on October 1, 2006, moving into the former General District Court Clerk’s office. Local businesswoman Jillian Mitchell, who had leased the old courthouse as part of her Courthouse Theatre project, offered the Chamber the space.[1] President Drinkard and other Chamber staff moved the entire office themselves. The Town of Appomattox and its new Director of Tourism, Becky Nix—herself a former president of the Chamber’s board of directors—took over operations of the Visitors Center the same day.[13]

    Financial challenges (2016)

    By 2016, the Chamber was struggling financially. President John Redding appeared before both the Board of Supervisors and the Town Council in May to request financial assistance. Redding explained that the Cheerz concert events, the Chamber’s primary fundraising vehicle, had become more expensive after Blue Ridge Beverages withdrew a $1,000 annual sponsorship and added a $150 labor fee per event. Combined with poor attendance due to bad weather, the Cheerz events were more than $2,000 less profitable than in previous years.[2] The Chamber needed approximately $26,000 annually to operate at a basic level but was bringing in only about $15,000 in membership dues from its 145 members.

    In July 2016, the Town Council voted unanimously not to make a donation to the Chamber. Council members expressed concern that the Chamber had not presented a plan to assist businesses, focusing instead on its own revenue generation. Councilwoman Claudia Puckette stated that she saw no concrete evidence of plans to assist local businesses.[14] Redding indicated the Chamber was exploring a restructuring of its membership dues to close the revenue gap.

    The Redding era and community journalism (2015–2022)

    John Redding served as both president and director of the Chamber from approximately 2015 through the end of 2022. Under his leadership, the Chamber launched several new initiatives. In February 2018, Redding began writing a weekly column in the Times-Virginian called “Words from the Chamber,” which explored topics ranging from the county’s role as a regional shopping hub to ideas for improving the visual appeal of entry corridors into the town.[15] The column became a regular feature and continued under his successor.

    Redding also spearheaded the formation of the Appomattox Tourism Advisory Committee (ATAC) in 2017, a multi-partner initiative branded as “Experience Appomattox” that brought together the Chamber, the 1865 Foundation, the Museum of the Confederacy–Appomattox, local lodging businesses, and the National Historical Park to cooperatively promote tourism.[16] In 2018, the Chamber pursued a downtown business signage project—a $9,000 sign reading “Visit, Shop and Eat in Downtown Appomattox” with ten plaques for participating businesses—though the effort encountered resistance from the town’s Economic Development Authority, which declined to contribute.[17] That same year, Redding announced plans to publish a community-based magazine modeled after the city guides found in hotels, with a dual focus on Appomattox as a destination and a place to live.[18]

    Steven Boyer and the current era (2023–present)

    At the end of 2022, John Redding retired from the director position, and the board of directors approved Steven Boyer as his replacement effective January 1, 2023.[19] Boyer, a 21-year United States Navy veteran who had lived in Appomattox County since 1998, brought a background in municipal government from his career with the City of Lynchburg’s Assessor’s Office, where he had served as a systems analyst and managed the city’s rehabilitation and agricultural land-use programs. Boyer stated his intention to visit every business in the county to understand their needs and to boost Chamber membership.[20]

    Mission and responsibilities

    The Chamber’s stated mission is to improve the quality of life for residents and businesses in Appomattox County by working in partnership with its members and local government. It is committed to the advancement of commercial, industrial, agricultural, and civic interests throughout the county and its trade area.[1] In practice, the Chamber operates as a networking hub for local businesses, a coordinator of community events, an advocate for pro-business policies, and a point of contact for visitors and prospective businesses exploring the county.

    Member benefits have included listing on the Chamber website, ribbon-cutting ceremonies for new businesses, a quarterly newsletter, seminars, discounts on business services such as insurance, and weekly digital “newsblasts.”[2]

    Organization and governance

    The Chamber is governed by a volunteer board of directors that elects a president from among its members. The board president has historically served as the public face of the organization, presiding at events and representing the Chamber before local government bodies. Day-to-day operations are managed by a paid director (historically titled “executive secretary”), supported by a part-time administrator. The Chamber has typically employed only one or two paid staff members at any given time, relying heavily on volunteer labor to organize events and carry out its programs.

    As of 2016, the Chamber office at 276 Court Street was open from 9 a.m. to 2 p.m. on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays.[2] The Board of Supervisors has historically included a budget line item for the Chamber among its contributions to outside agencies, with funding documented as early as 1992.[3]

    Major programs and events

    Friday Cheers / Appomattox Cheerz

    The Chamber’s flagship community event has been its outdoor concert series, which began around 1999 under the name Friday Cheers.[23] The events were held on Friday evenings, typically from April through October, at Watkins M. Abbitt Memorial Park in Downtown Appomattox, with larger acts occasionally relocated to Courtland Field on Court Street. The format featured live music by regional bands, food sold by a rotating lineup of nonprofit organizations, beverages including beer, and a 50/50 raffle. Admission ranged from $2 to $5 per person, with children twelve and under admitted free.[24]

    In September 2006, the event was renamed Appomattox Cheerz while retaining the same format.[25] Each Cheerz event typically benefited a different local nonprofit—among them the Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department, the Appomattox Rescue Squad, the Missionary Food Bank, Appomattox Youth Sports, and the ACHS Booster Club—while a portion of proceeds also supported the Chamber’s scholarship fund. At its peak, the Cheerz series scheduled six to eight events per season and drew crowds of several hundred to hear popular regional acts such as The Worx, the Southland Band, and the Fantastic Shakers.

    The Chamber also organized Emberfest, a fall festival introduced in 2007 at Courtland Field that featured live entertainment, food, and $1 beers, serving as a capstone event after the regular Cheerz season concluded.[26] In 2018, the Cheerz format was briefly replaced by an “Oldies but Goodies Night” event at Abbitt Park.[27] A special Cheerz in June 2016 served as a fundraiser for victims of the February 2016 tornado, with bands Southland and Rare Form donating their performances and net proceeds channeled through Gleaning for the World.[28]

    Citizen of the Year Award

    Each year, the Chamber presents the Citizen of the Year award at its annual fall dinner or banquet, recognizing individuals who have demonstrated outstanding volunteerism and community service in Appomattox County. During some years, separate male and female awards were presented; in other years, a single recipient was honored. The award is nominated by community members and selected by the Chamber’s board of directors, with the recipient typically kept secret until the announcement at the dinner.[7] The fall dinner has been held at various venues over the years, including The Spring House restaurant in Concord, the Appomattox Community Center, and Falling River Country Club.

    Jon B. Montgomery Scholarship

    The Chamber awards the Jon B. Montgomery Scholarship—named for the longtime superintendent of the Appomattox Court House National Historical Park—to a graduating Appomattox County high school senior each year. The $500 scholarship is available to students who have been accepted at an accredited two-year or four-year institution, maintain at least a 2.5 grade-point average, and are an immediate family member of a current Chamber member or a member’s employee.[29] The scholarship fund has been supported throughout its history by proceeds from the Friday Cheers and Cheerz events.

    Other events and initiatives

    Beyond the Cheerz series, the Chamber has organized or co-organized a range of community events and initiatives over the years. The Chamber has been involved in the annual Community of Candles holiday tradition at Courthouse Square, which has been observed since 1987. Chamber member June Chenault was long associated with the event, which was originally created by Shirley Walton of the Virginia Cooperative Extension Service and was featured in Southern Living magazine in 1989.[30] The Chamber has also played a supporting role in the annual Christmas Parade and the Downtown Open House holiday celebrations.[31]

    Other Chamber-sponsored or co-sponsored events have included the Trade Show (first held in 2004), a Benefit Cook-off at Abbitt Park (2004), the Miss Appomattox Festival Pageant, and the Socialize After Five networking events for business owners, which began in 2008 and were held at various member business locations.[8][32][33] The Chamber also hosted civic forums, including a 2009 “Town Hall style” meeting with Delegate Watkins Abbitt and Congressman Tom Perriello, at which community members could pose questions to their elected representatives.[34]

    Business advocacy and community initiatives

    The Chamber has periodically engaged in advocacy on behalf of the local business community. In 2005, the Chamber organized a petition drive for a voter referendum on liquor-by-the-drink sales in the county, a measure the board argued would support economic growth by allowing restaurants to serve mixed drinks.[10] In 2009, President Patrick Richardson launched a “Shop and Buy Appomattox” initiative encouraging residents to patronize local businesses rather than driving to Lynchburg for purchases.[35]

    In 2010, the Chamber unveiled a new business logo designed by Appomattox County High School senior Brandon Hodges, who won a student art contest. Bobby Swanberg of Village Printshop refined the final design, and logos were installed at two community sign locations on U.S. 460.[36] In 2016, Redding wrote a public letter questioning the efficiency of the county having two separate governing bodies—the Board of Supervisors and Town Council—arguing the structure was “confrontational and counter-productive.”[37]

    The Chamber has also served as a vehicle for connecting the community to broader regional and state economic-development efforts, coordinating with the Region 2000 Regional Commission and the Virginia Tourism Corporation.
