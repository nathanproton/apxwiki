    Winfred D. Nash (born c. 1944–1945) is an American nuclear industry executive and civic leader from Appomattox County, Virginia. He served as President of the Nuclear Operations Division of BWX Technologies (BWXT) from 2006 until his retirement in 2011, overseeing consolidated operations across manufacturing facilities in Virginia, Ohio, and Indiana. A lifelong resident of Appomattox County, Nash has been a prominent figure in local economic development, education, and community affairs, serving as a charter member of the Appomattox Industrial Development Authority (now the Economic Development Authority) and as chairman of the Appomattox County School Board.

    Early life and education

    Nash was born and raised in Appomattox County, Virginia. He graduated from Appomattox County High School in 1962.[1] He earned a Bachelor of Science degree in Electrical Engineering Technology from Old Dominion University in 1976, and later received a Master of Business Administration from Duke University's Fuqua School of Business in 1990.[1] He also completed Advanced Executive Management training at Northeastern University.[1]

    Nash has said that he chose engineering as a career because of his interest in automated control systems. "I was always interested in how things work," he told the Times-Virginian in 2007.[1]

    Career at Babcock & Wilcox / BWXT

    Early career (1966–2006)

    Nash began his career in 1966 as an inspector in the Ultrasonic Inspection area of Quality Control within the Nuclear Operations Division of Babcock & Wilcox (B&W) in Lynchburg, Virginia.[1] He subsequently advanced to supervisory positions and later held senior management roles within the company as it was reorganized under BWX Technologies (BWXT), a subsidiary of the former B&W enterprise.[1]

    President of Nuclear Operations Division (2006–2011)

    In July 2006, Nash was named President of the BWXT Nuclear Operations Division, a newly consolidated entity encompassing three BWXT-owned manufacturing facilities in Virginia, Ohio, and Indiana.[1][2] The consolidation brought together the company's nuclear fuel fabrication and naval reactor component operations under a single leadership structure.

    During Nash's tenure as president, the division experienced significant growth. At the time of his retirement, Nash stated that "our business has nearly doubled due to organic growth and vertical integration achieved through strategic acquisitions."[3] The division's portfolio included manufacturing nuclear reactor components for the United States Navy, including work under an $807 million contract for naval nuclear reactor fuel and components.[4] Nash also initiated the Lean Six Sigma quality improvement program at B&W Nuclear Operations Group during this period.[3]

    Retirement

    Nash retired from BWXT on April 1, 2011, at approximately age 66. He was succeeded as president by Peyton S. Baker.[3] During his 45-year career with the company, Nash rose from an entry-level inspection position to lead one of the nation's primary nuclear operations divisions.

    Public service

    Appomattox County School Board

    In June 1988, Nash was appointed to a four-year term on the Appomattox County School Board, representing the Southside District.[5] He went on to serve on the board for eight years, including a term as chairman.[1] Reflecting on his motivation to join the board, Nash said: "I have always been pro-educational. I had two children in school."[1]

    Industrial Development Authority / Economic Development Authority

    Nash is a charter member of the Appomattox Industrial Development Authority (IDA), which was later reorganized as the Economic Development Authority (EDA) of Appomattox County.[1] His membership on the authority spans more than two decades of documented service.

    As early as April 2005, Nash was listed as a member of the IDA alongside Chairman Bill Slagle, Wayne Lewis, Walter Hancock, Cecil Wooldridge, Buddy Conner, and Laurie Harris.[6] The IDA oversaw the Appomattox Center for Business and Commerce (commonly known as the industrial park), a 485-acre county-owned industrial park on State Route 26.[6]

    In June 2006, Nash was present when the IDA formally endorsed a proposal to construct a waterline from Campbell County to Appomattox, a project the authority called essential for the county's economic future and water security.[7]

    Nash was reappointed to the EDA by the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors in December 2010 for a term expiring December 31, 2014,[8] and again in January 2015 for a term expiring December 31, 2018.[9]

    Meeting minutes from 2016 document Nash's active participation in EDA proceedings. At the July 25, 2016, meeting, Nash voted to approve payment of bills including invoices related to the Appomattox Inn and Suites.[10] At an August 2016 meeting, Nash voted in favor of authorizing Phase I utilities engineering for the industrial park at a cost of up to $30,000, as well as $15,000 in costs to provide water and sewer service to the DRT project.[10] He also joined the unanimous consensus approving a resolution of gratitude to departing Director of Economic Development Jeff Taylor.[10] At the September 20, 2016, meeting—where Vice-Chairman Searcy was absent—Nash motioned to nominate Garet Bosiger as temporary chairman.[10] Nash also made motions for the authority to enter closed sessions under Virginia Code §2.2-3711 A.3 and A.5 at multiple EDA meetings throughout 2016–2018, dealing with matters related to prospective business negotiations and real property acquisition.[10][11]

    At the January 2018 annual organizational meeting, Nash seconded the nomination of Gary Tanner as Vice-Chairman of the EDA.[11]

    Central Virginia Community College Advisory Board

    Nash served on the Advisory Board of Central Virginia Community College (CVCC) in Lynchburg, Virginia. He resigned from the board effective December 1, 2018, and was replaced by Jeffrey Garrett, who was appointed by the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors to fill Nash's unexpired term through June 30, 2022.[12]

    Awards and honors

    In 2007, Nash was named a recipient of the Virginia Community College System (VCCS) Chancellor's Award for Leadership in Philanthropy, recognizing his contributions to Central Virginia Community College.[1][2] He was one of 23 recipients honored as "outstanding benefactors" of Virginia's community colleges at a luncheon at the Jefferson Hotel in Richmond on April 25, 2007.[1]

    VCCS Chancellor Dr. Glenn DuBois praised the honorees, stating: "These leaders know the secret to a better tomorrow. By their work with the VCCS, they're improving their communities, the commonwealth and the nation."[1]

    Nash expressed gratitude for the recognition: "I was very pleased. The company that I work for is pro-education, supporting our young people and bringing technology to the industry to be competitive for the future."[1]

    Business investments

    COJANA LLC and the Appomattox Inn and Suites

    Nash is one of four investors in COJANA LLC, a private investment group that developed the Appomattox Inn and Suites, the first new-build hotel in the Appomattox area in more than two decades. The other COJANA partners are Bill Jamerson, Phillip Jamerson, and Buddy Conner.[13]

    Originally envisioned as a Cobblestone-branded hotel, the project was developed independently with financing from Farmers Bank of Appomattox.[13] Ground was broken on Old Courthouse Road in March 2015, and the 56-room, three-story hotel—featuring a 150-seat conference center, fitness center, outdoor swimming pool, and Cannons Café—officially opened on March 1, 2016, under the professional management of Marshall Hotels & Resorts.[13][14]

    Willie Craft, then-chairman of the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors, praised the COJANA investors at the groundbreaking: "We applaud the partners of COJANA who have stepped up to the plate to make this a reality. … These efforts will provide a great opportunity for our citizens and visitors, and will contribute a much-needed boost to our local economy."[13]

    The hotel opened some rooms early in late February 2016 for volunteers and victims of the EF-3 tornado that struck Appomattox County.[14]

    Community involvement

    Central Baptist Church

    Nash is an active member of Central Baptist Church in the Chap community of Appomattox County.[1] When the historic church—formed in 1893—was destroyed by an EF-3 tornado on February 24, 2016, Nash served on the Building Committee alongside Raymond Mullins, Nelson Mann, Arthur Miller, and Billy Moore.[15]

    Nash was subsequently voted as Project Manager for the church rebuilding effort. He worked closely with Jamerson-Lewis Construction on the design of the new building, which is similar in style to the original structure with the addition of partition walls forming classrooms in the fellowship hall.[16] At the dedication ceremony on January 28, 2018, Nash addressed the packed sanctuary of over 120 people, recounting the devastation: "Structural pieces of the church were scattered around the yard. It's humbling to see something you've been associated with your entire life destroyed by a tornado within a matter of seconds."[16]

    Aviation

    Nash is a member of the Airplane Owner's and Pilot's Association (AOPA) and a lifetime member of the National Aviation Hall of Fame Board of Nominations.[1]

    Solar energy advocacy

    In 2024 and 2025, Nash became publicly active in opposing utility-scale solar energy farms in Appomattox County. At an Appomattox County Planning Commission public hearing, Nash spoke in favor of removing utility-scale solar farms from the A-1 agricultural zoning district and in favor of increased buffer and setback requirements.[17]

    Nash also appeared before the Planning Commission in a separate session to present concerns regarding the environmental impact of solar panels, surety and liability issues, and the financial impact on future residents of Appomattox County. He discussed the need to address Energy Storage Systems (battery storage) and was joined by Terry Jamerson, who raised concerns about per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) associated with solar panel manufacturing.[17]

    In January 2024, Nash also presented on solar panel environmental concerns at a public meeting in Fluvanna County, Virginia.[18]
