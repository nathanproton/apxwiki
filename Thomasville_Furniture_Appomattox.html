    The Thomasville Furniture plant in the Town of Appomattox, Virginia, was a furniture manufacturing facility that operated from October 1974 until April 2011. Originally established as Founders Furniture, a division of Thomasville Furniture Industries, the plant was located at 310 Founders Lane on the south side of U.S. Route 460. For most of its 36-year history, it served as the largest private employer in Appomattox County, reaching a peak workforce of more than 1,000 employees before a prolonged decline driven by overseas competition and shifting consumer markets led to its closure. The plant's shutdown eliminated approximately 200 jobs and was widely regarded as one of the most significant economic blows in modern Appomattox County history.[1][2]

    Founding and early years (1972–1990s)

    Grading at the plant site began in 1972, and the facility opened its doors in October 1974 as Founders Furniture, a division of Thomasville Furniture Industries.[3][4] The plant was located in the Town of Appomattox on the south side of U.S. Route 460, adjacent to Business 460 near the Dairy Queen. Bill Slagle, who would later serve as chairman of the Appomattox County Economic Development Authority, played a key role in convincing Thomasville to locate in Appomattox in the early 1970s. As Slagle later recalled during a 2010 EDA meeting, the company presented about a dozen requirements, and local officials worked to meet each one.[5]

    Founders Furniture was created from a vision of manufacturing quality furniture at affordable prices through mass production. The plant initially employed approximately 200 workers and began shipping furniture via freight car shortly after opening.[3] Focusing primarily on bedroom suites, the company grew steadily over the following decades and eventually became the largest employer in Appomattox County, with a workforce exceeding 1,000 at its peak.[1]

    A fire broke out at the plant in 1982, apparently starting in the dust collection system, though the facility continued operating.[6] In 1986, employees Donald Alan Bates of Appomattox and James William Jordan of Prospect received the Thomasville Furniture Industries President's Award, the company's highest honor.[7]

    Divisions and products

    Over its history, the Appomattox plant operated several product divisions under the Thomasville umbrella. The original Founders Furniture division specialized in bedroom suites produced through mass-production methods. In the mid-1980s, Creative Interiors was formed to manufacture ready-to-assemble (RTA) furniture. A contract furniture division followed in the mid-1990s, serving the hospitality and commercial markets. In 2001, the Vignettes brand was established, specializing in imported master and youth bedroom furniture constructed of wood solids and veneers.[3]

    The plant's major retail customers over the years included Walmart, Target, Sam's Club, Value City, and Aaron's. Founders Furniture was also sold through regional chains such as Schewels and Badcock. The company maintained showrooms in High Point, North Carolina, and Tupelo, Mississippi, and employed a product design staff across the East Coast.[3]

    Thomasville Furniture operated two production facilities in Virginia: the Appomattox plant and one in Carysbrook. The parent company, Furniture Brands International (NYSE: FBN), was headquartered in St. Louis, Missouri, and also owned Broyhill, Lane, and several other furniture brands.[8]

    Peak employment and early decline (2000s)

    Thirtieth anniversary and community role

    Founders Furniture celebrated its 30th anniversary in October 2004 with a company-wide event on the plant grounds that included a catered lunch, live music, and giveaways for active and retired employees. By that time, overseas outsourcing had already reduced the payroll from its peak of over 1,000 to approximately 500 workers, yet the plant remained the county's largest employer.[3] Phil Miller, Senior Vice President of Founders, acknowledged the significance of the milestone, noting that companies surviving 30 years in the current manufacturing climate was a rare occurrence. Bill Gilleland, Vice President of Manufacturing, pointed to new contracts with Walmart and Target as reasons for optimism, and the company was projecting a four-percent increase in sales for the following year.[3]

    In 2002, Appomattox officials had honored Thomasville Furniture during Business Appreciation Week for its significant contributions to the community.[9] Despite periodic shutdown weeks when demand slowed, company leaders publicly expressed confidence in the plant's future. The question of what would happen to Appomattox if Thomasville ever closed was, even at the time of the 30th anniversary, a topic of frequent speculation among residents.[3]

    Restructuring (2005–2006)

    In August 2005, Furniture Brands International announced a marketing realignment that shifted two of the Appomattox plant's divisions — Founders and Creative Interiors — from reporting to Thomasville to reporting to its sister brand, Broyhill. Phil Miller described the change as a marketing move rather than an operational one, emphasizing that daily operations and employment would not be affected.[8]

    In January 2006, the company eliminated 30 jobs from its manual labor force, reducing the workforce to approximately 575. Gilleland said the cuts were based on work performance and seniority and were intended to smooth out seasonal production cycles that had resulted in three-to-four-week shutdown periods each year. The company offered affected employees extended benefits and brought in Region 2000's workforce training group to assist with résumés and job placement.[10] Sammy Carter, Chairman of the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors, called the cuts a "heavy blow" to Appomattox.[10]

    In October 2006, Thomasville announced the elimination of its Founders Furniture division from all future operations. Phil Miller, by then president of Thomasville's Virginia operations, said the change would not reduce employment levels. Founders had shrunk to about four percent of the plant's production volume, and the approximately 600-person workforce was expected to continue at its current level, operating six days a week, 24 hours a day. Miller simultaneously announced a major new contract with Walmart — a five-item collection going to 3,000 stores — as well as pending deals with Office Max and another large unnamed retailer.[11]

    CVS rezoning (2007)

    In mid-2007, Thomasville Furniture requested a rezoning of a 10-acre strip of its land from M1 (industrial) to B1 (business) to facilitate the sale of property for a new CVS Pharmacy location. The proposed 12,000-square-foot freestanding CVS would be built across U.S. 460 from the existing CVS location, on land that included the Thomasville ball field. The Appomattox Planning Commission recommended approval in June 2007, but the Town Council delayed the vote several times as Council member Steve Lawson raised concerns about potential unrestricted commercial development on the rezoned property.[12][13] The property was eventually sold to a group that owned CVS for an estimated $1.9 million, with the deed recorded in early 2010.[14]

    Great Recession layoffs (2008–2009)

    The national economic downturn devastated the furniture industry, and the Appomattox plant bore the consequences in two successive rounds of layoffs. In December 2008, Thomasville eliminated 29 percent of its workforce — more than 140 employees — reducing the payroll to approximately 350 workers. The cuts affected both hourly and salaried employees and effectively eliminated the third shift. John Hastings, Vice President of Communications at Furniture Brands International, said the layoffs were part of a company-wide reduction totaling 1,400 positions nationwide, described as an "inevitable response to the recessionary environment." Furniture Brands Chairman and CEO Ralph P. Scozzafava stated the company was "aligning our costs with anticipated lower sales volumes."[15]

    Robert Ayers of Pamplin, who had worked at the plant since its opening in 1974 as a Product Packaging Engineer, was among those laid off. Ayers told the Times-Virginian that the camaraderie among workers was what he would miss most, describing the plant as "one big family."[15] Former Supervisor Nina Beasley subsequently urged the Board of Supervisors to pursue a waterline and hire an Economic Development Director, citing the Thomasville layoffs as evidence of the county's vulnerability.[16]

    A further round of layoffs in September 2009 cut approximately 40 more workers, reducing the workforce to around 300 employees. Furniture Brands spokesman Hastings again declined to provide location-specific numbers, citing company policy, but attributed the cuts to a weak consumer economy. Appomattox County Board of Supervisors Chairman Tom Conrad expressed concern and emphasized ongoing efforts to attract new jobs through waterline development and an Economic Development Director position.[17]

    Closure announcement and final days (2010–2011)

    Closure announcement

    On November 2, 2010, Furniture Brands International announced that the Appomattox facility would close the following spring. Employees were told their jobs would end on March 25, 2011, though a small number of workers would remain beyond that date to manage the official shutdown. The plant employed approximately 200 workers at the time of the announcement.[1]

    Furniture Brands stated it would transfer a portion of production to one or more facilities in North Carolina and that the closure would generate restructuring charges of approximately $7 million, with annual cost savings of approximately $5 million. The company framed the move as part of a strategy to broaden sourcing options, combining domestic manufacturing with contractors in Latin America and Asia.[1]

    Gary Tanner, Chairman of the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors, called the announcement a source of sadness, saying Thomasville had been "a great business partner for the people in this area." The Times-Virginian ranked the closure as the second-biggest news story of 2010, behind only the Snapps Mill Road shooting massacre in January of that year.[1][18]

    Ashley Furniture pursuit

    Within weeks of the closure announcement, local officials launched an aggressive campaign to attract Ashley Furniture Industries to the Thomasville site. Ashley Furniture chairman Ron Wanek reportedly indicated that the Appomattox plant was one of several locations in Virginia and the Carolinas being considered for a new East Coast manufacturing and distribution facility that could employ about 1,000 workers. Ashley officials had toured the Thomasville plant in November 2010.[5]

    The county-appointed Economic Development Authority voted 6–0 in December 2010 to send a delegation to Ashley's headquarters in Arcadia, Wisconsin. EDA member Ronnie Spiggle urged an all-out effort, declaring that the group needed to "wine 'em and dine 'em and do whatever we've got to do to get them here." The Town of Appomattox sent a letter of support to Ashley, and both the Town Council and Board of Supervisors endorsed the effort.[5][19] Despite these efforts, Ashley ultimately did not select the Appomattox facility.

    Final day of operations

    The Thomasville plant officially closed on Friday, April 1, 2011, after 36 years in operation. The final production shipment went out that day. William Gilliland, Vice President and General Production Manager, addressed the remaining workers, telling them, "You care about each other. You have a lot of heart … Sometimes in life there are some special moments and memories that come along … it has been great. I will never forget it."[2]

    Several longtime employees spoke publicly about the closure's personal impact. Faye Thompson, who had worked at the plant for all 36 years, described her coworkers as a second family. Leon Gaines, a warehouse leader with nearly 34 years at the plant, said the job had changed him socially, making him more open as a person. Hettie Adams, a 35-year employee, planned to pursue a career as a certified nursing assistant. Others, including Ellis Shorter (26 years) and Thomas McCoy (26 years), expressed concern about the economic toll on the broader community.[2]

    At the time of closure, the plant employed 135 people in manufacturing, 30 in management, and 30 temporary workers. The company had applied for and received approval under the federal Trade Adjustment Assistance Act, which provided extended unemployment benefits and tuition assistance for eligible displaced workers.[2]

    Auction and shutdown

    An auction was held on August 10, 2011, managed by Loeb Winternitz Industrial Auctioneers. Tens of thousands of pieces of furniture-making equipment were sold, with bidders receiving steep discounts — one saw normally worth $750,000 sold for $1,000, and another bidder purchased approximately $1.5 million worth of equipment for less than $30,000. However, the 831,000-square-foot plant and its 85.5 acres of land did not receive any bids at the minimum price of $6.3 million.[20]

    Gilliland noted that the bargain prices helped small operators get started in the furniture business. A skeleton crew of 12 employees remained through September to manage shutdown procedures and oversee removal of sold items.[20]

    Community involvement and impact

    Throughout its history, Thomasville maintained a visible presence in the Appomattox community beyond its role as an employer. The plant contributed to local charitable causes, including a $1,000 donation from its Public Relations Budget to the Appomattox Elementary School playground project in 2004.[21] The company also supported the annual school supply drive for underprivileged students and provided a $500 scholarship through the Miss Appomattox Festival Pageant.[22][23]

    Thomasville Furniture Industries administered the T. Austin Finch Scholarship program, which awarded scholarships to children of employees across its operations, including recipients from Appomattox County High School.[24] The plant's ball field, located along Business 460, was used by community groups and occasionally served as a landing spot for a helicopter used by Buckingham Virginia Slate Corporation employees.[25] The Thomasville property also served as the traditional starting point for the annual Historic Appomattox Railroad Festival parade; after the plant's closure, the parade was forced to relocate its staging area to Appomattox Elementary School in 2011.[26]

    In 2005, the plant took environmental action by removing copper water piping from its facility after the Town of Appomattox identified it as a contributor to elevated copper levels in the municipal wastewater system.[27] In late 2010, the company and a private citizen donated two decibel meters to the Appomattox County Sheriff's Department for noise ordinance enforcement.[28]

    Numerous obituaries in the Times-Virginian throughout the 2000s, 2010s, and into the 2020s list Thomasville Furniture as a former employer, reflecting the generations of Appomattox County families whose livelihoods were tied to the plant.

    Economic impact of closure

    The plant's closure had cascading effects on the local economy. The loss of approximately 200 jobs directly reduced consumer spending in the area; several former employees and business owners noted the impact on local stores and services. Wanda Doss, a 36-year employee, reported that business owners had told her they were already hurt by the earlier rounds of layoffs.[2]

    The Town of Appomattox faced a sewer rate increase after Thomasville's cessation of operations reduced revenue from the utility system.[29] Appomattox County Supervisor Jerry Small warned that he expected county revenues to decrease in the wake of the closure, complicating an already tight budget for school funding and other services.[30]

    The closure heightened a longstanding push to hire a county Economic Development Director. The county had debated the position for 28 months prior to the closure announcement, but the loss of Thomasville injected new urgency. Town Council member Mary Lou Spiggle proposed a joint meeting of town and county governing bodies specifically to address the hiring, and both bodies eventually moved forward with the position, hiring Jeff Taylor as Appomattox County's first Economic Development Director.[31][32]

    Afterlife of the building

    Lindenburg Industries (2014–2015)

    After the auction failed to sell the property, the building sat largely vacant for three years while various prospects toured the facility. Economic Development Director Jeff Taylor reported interest from two clients in early 2012 and additional inquiries through mid-2012, including manufacturers and speculators.[33] In 2014, Furniture Brands International — which had itself filed for bankruptcy — listed the property through Hilco Real Estate with a bid deadline of June 24, 2014. The 831,000-square-foot building on 85.5 acres had been listed at an estimated value of $4.6 million.[34]

    In November 2014, Virginia Governor Terry McAuliffe announced at a ceremony in the plant parking lot that Lindenburg Industry, LLC, a subsidiary of a Chinese-owned company, would invest $113 million to establish a honeycomb manufacturing operation for industrial air pollution control components at the site, creating up to 349 jobs. The property was purchased for just over $2 million. The announcement was celebrated as transformational for Appomattox, with CEO Stella Li expressing excitement about the community's workforce talent and business environment.[35]

    The venture quickly unraveled. Lindenburg encountered visa problems that delayed the arrival of its Chinese management team, and the factory never opened. By late 2015, the property was placed under liens by the Town of Appomattox, which was pursuing approximately $45,000 in unreimbursed costs for water system upgrades made in anticipation of Lindenburg's operations.[36] A foreclosure auction was held on December 15, 2015, at which businessman George Aznavorian submitted the sole bid of $1 million.[37]

    The Virginia Economic Development Partnership subsequently sued Lindenburg and its associated firm, Development Advisors, to recover $1.4 million in state grant money that had been disbursed. The Virginia State Police also initiated an investigation into the investment. In 2018, the Virginia Supreme Court dismissed Lindenburg's appeal in the lawsuit. The episode became a recurring political issue, cited by critics during Terry McAuliffe's 2021 gubernatorial campaign and by Republican candidate Glenn Youngkin.[38][39][40]

    Aznavorian ownership and Virginia MetalFab

    George Aznavorian, the new owner, developed plans for a mixed-use redevelopment of the property that included ground-floor commercial spaces, apartments intended to create a "main street effect" along U.S. 460, an industrial hub, and senior housing cottages with an associated assisted living facility. In October 2016, the Appomattox Town Council unanimously approved a conditional use permit for the redevelopment plan.[41]

    Progress was slow in the following years, though portions of the property were put to interim use. In 2016, Bays Trash Removal received a conditional use permit to store portable toilets on part of the site, and the parking lot served as a volunteer clean-up staging area after the February 2016 tornado. The old Thomasville building parking lot also hosted the Appomattox Show & Shine Festival in 2017.[42]

    The building found its most significant new occupant in 2022, when Ron Martin relocated his companies Stallworks and Virginia MetalFab to the former Thomasville facility. Governor Glenn Youngkin attended an open house on December 9, 2022, where hundreds of community members celebrated the return of manufacturing activity to the site. Virginia MetalFab announced plans to add 130 jobs to its workforce, which had already grown from 39 to 75 employees.[43][44]
