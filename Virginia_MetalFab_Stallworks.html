    Virginia MetalFab (VMF) and Stallworks are sister companies involved in metal fabrication and equine systems manufacturing, respectively, both owned by Ron Martin. Virginia MetalFab specializes in custom metal fabrication work for industrial and commercial clients, while Stallworks manufactures and installs horse stall products and related equestrian equipment. Beginning in March 2022, both companies underwent a major relocation from their original sites to the historic Thomasville Furniture plant in Appomattox. The move represented the first significant commercial reoccupancy of the building since its closure in 2011 and became a symbolic turning point in the county's economic revitalization efforts. The grand opening in December 2022 was marked by the attendance of Governor Glenn Youngkin and drew hundreds of attendees, during which VMF announced plans to create an additional 130 jobs, more than doubling the combined workforce.

    Stallworks early history and signage dispute (2008)

    Stallworks, the predecessor entity to what would later operate alongside Virginia MetalFab, had become established prior to 2008 as a manufacturer of horse stall products and equestrian equipment. The company operated from a rural location outside the main commercial corridors of Appomattox County, serving a regional equestrian market with manufactured and assembled horse stall systems, shelter structures, and related equipment. Like many rural manufacturers, Stallworks faced challenges in marketing and customer visibility, with potential customers often unaware of the company's location or existence in the county.

    In 2008, seeking to expand its visibility in the market and attract customers traveling through the region, Stallworks filed an application with Appomattox County for a conditional use permit to erect an arrow-directed sign at the corner of Police Tower Road and U.S. Route 460, a prominent intersection for local business visibility. Arrow-directed signs are designed with directional arrows pointing toward a specific business location, facilitating customer navigation and business discovery. The placement at the Police Tower Road and Route 460 intersection ensured visibility from regional traffic traveling through Appomattox County, representing a strategic marketing investment intended to increase customer awareness and sales.[1]

    The Appomattox Planning Commission examined the application and recommended denial of the permit, citing concerns about visual clutter and sign proliferation in the county. The Commission's position reflected a planning perspective emphasizing aesthetic control, visual quality of the roadway environment, and orderly sign regulation intended to prevent commercial signage from overwhelming the visual landscape. Planning commissioners noted that approval of directional signs for every business location would lead to sign proliferation and visual degradation of prominent county intersections and roadway corridors.

    However, in an unusual reversal that highlighted fundamental tensions between the Planning Commission and the Board of Supervisors over economic development priorities, the Board of Supervisors unanimously overruled the Planning Commission's recommendation and approved the sign. During the discussion, Gary Tanner, a member of the Board, stated emphatically, "We do want people to know that we're business friendly." Supervisor Sam Carter was even more direct, declaring, "I cannot believe we're trying to hinder a business coming into the county." Board member Tom Conrad echoed similar sentiments regarding the county's commitment to welcoming business expansion. The supervisors' statements revealed a clear prioritization of business recruitment and expansion support over planning aesthetics and design control, establishing a county development policy philosophy that favored accommodating business needs and removing regulatory obstacles to private enterprise.[1]

    Johnny Roark, then Appomattox County's planning director, noted confusion within the county's regulatory framework regarding arrow signs and their permitting status, a reflection of the evolving commercial landscape and the county's efforts to balance growth with planning controls. Roark's observations highlighted the tension between planning professionals' emphasis on comprehensive design standards and business operators' practical needs for customer visibility and market access. The supervisors' unanimous override of the Planning Commission's recommendation established a clear precedent: when economic development considerations conflicted with planning aesthetics or design controls, business development would take priority.

    The Stallworks signage decision reflected broader county policy tensions that would continue to influence economic development decisions throughout the subsequent decade and a half. The Board of Supervisors' 2008 decision demonstrated a fundamental policy orientation favoring business recruitment and expansion support, a stance that would support subsequent business ventures including the successful attraction of Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks to the Thomasville facility in 2022. The signage decision, while seemingly minor, established a policy foundation and cultural precedent within county government that business-friendly policies and regulatory flexibility took priority over aesthetic planning controls or design regulations, a philosophy that would prove significant when subsequent business development opportunities arose.

    Virginia MetalFab origins and early operations

    Virginia MetalFab was established in the mid-2000s as a custom metal fabrication company serving regional industrial, commercial, and manufacturing clients. The company provided a range of metal fabrication services, including welding, cutting, bending, and finishing operations. Operating alongside the sister company Stallworks under common ownership by Ron Martin, VMF gradually built a reputation for quality work and reliable service delivery in the local and regional economy.

    By the late 2000s and early 2010s, Virginia MetalFab had become an established presence in Appomattox County's manufacturing sector, operating from facilities separate from Stallworks, though both companies maintained close operational ties and shared administrative functions under Martin's leadership.

    Growth as a regional manufacturer (2010s–2019)

    Throughout the 2010s, Virginia MetalFab expanded its operations and workforce, positioning itself as a significant employer in Appomattox County's manufacturing base. The company maintained consistent staffing levels and secured contracts with regional and national customers, contributing to the county's diversification following the closure of major manufacturing facilities such as Thomasville Furniture.

    By 2019, Virginia MetalFab employed approximately 40 individuals across its metal fabrication operations, making it a notable but mid-sized employer in the county. The company's operations demonstrated the viability of specialized manufacturing services in rural and semi-rural Virginia, contrasting with the commodity furniture manufacturing that had defined the region's economic identity for decades. The company served diverse customer sectors, including agricultural equipment manufacturers, food processing equipment suppliers, HVAC system fabricators, structural steel contractors, and various other industrial customers requiring custom metal fabrication services. The diversified customer base reduced dependency on any single industry sector and provided resilience during sectoral economic downturns, as weakness in one customer sector could be offset by strength in others.

    Relocation to Thomasville building (2022)

    Expansion planning and announcement

    By late 2021 and early 2022, Ron Martin had begun planning a significant expansion of both Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks. The expansion strategy involved relocating both companies from their existing separate facilities to a consolidated location within Appomattox County. Recognizing the opportunity presented by the long-vacant Thomasville Furniture building, Martin pursued the acquisition of the structure and rights to operate within it, seeing the relocation as an opportunity to bring both companies together under one roof, achieve economies of scale, and position the combined operations for growth.[4]

    By March 2022, preparations for the relocation were underway. Ron Martin publicly stated his intention to improve manufacturing employment in Appomattox County through the expansion and consolidation of both companies' operations. Local leadership and economic development officials, recognizing the strategic importance of keeping established manufacturing operations within the county and attracting growth investment, supported the initiative.[4]

    In May 2022, John Redding, President of the Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce, formally announced at a Chamber meeting that Ron Martin was relocating both Stallworks and Virginia MetalFab to the former Thomasville Furniture Industries building on Founders Lane. The announcement was significant not only for the companies involved but also for its symbolic meaning: the move represented the first return of commercial manufacturing operations to a building that had stood largely inactive since Thomasville's closure in April 2011, a period of dormancy lasting more than a decade.[4][5]

    Construction and move-in

    Over the summer and fall of 2022, extensive renovation and buildout work proceeded at the Thomasville building. The 831,000-square-foot facility required internal restructuring, infrastructure upgrades, and specialized equipment installation to accommodate both the metal fabrication operations of Virginia MetalFab and the horse stall manufacturing and assembly activities of Stallworks. The work involved partitioning of space, installation of fabrication equipment, utility upgrades, and creation of dedicated work zones for the different operational requirements of each company.

    The construction phase presented logistical challenges typical of large industrial facility renovations. The Thomasville building's infrastructure, while robust and designed for manufacturing operations, had been inactive for over a decade. Electrical systems required testing and potential upgrade to ensure adequate capacity and safety compliance with contemporary electrical codes. Mechanical systems, including HVAC and compressed air delivery infrastructure essential to metal fabrication operations, required inspection, refurbishment, or replacement. Plumbing and water systems required flushing, testing, and upgrades to meet current health and safety standards. The building's structural systems, roof, and exterior envelope required assessment for weather damage, deterioration, or deferred maintenance issues that might have developed during the 11-year vacancy period.[5]

    Ron Martin's organization contracted with regional construction and industrial services firms to execute the renovation work, creating local construction employment during the renovation phase and strengthening relationships with regional service providers and contractors. The investment in facility improvements represented a significant capital commitment by the company ownership, demonstrating confidence in the location's long-term viability and the companies' growth prospects. The phased renovation strategy prioritized essential systems and production areas, allowing staged equipment installation and operational startup while non-critical areas were improved during the ongoing operations phase.[5]

    Construction extended over several months. By November 2022, Virginia MetalFab officially moved into the building, becoming the first major commercial occupant since the Thomasville facility's closure in 2011. The move represented both a practical business consolidation and a highly visible marker of the Thomasville building's return to productive use, a transformation that local officials and community members had hoped would occur for over a decade. The sight of machinery being delivered to the facility, loading dock activity resuming, and facility lighting and operational indicators coming online provided tangible evidence to local residents that revitalization was underway.[5]

    Economic significance and revitalization impact

    The relocation of Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks to the former Thomasville Furniture building carried substantial symbolic and practical significance for Appomattox County's economic narrative. The Thomasville facility had stood largely vacant since its closure in April 2011, a 36-year-old industrial landmark and employment anchor suddenly transformed into an empty structure. The building's massive scale—831,000 square feet across multiple interconnected production bays—and its prominent location visible from U.S. Route 460, a major regional transportation corridor, made the vacant facility an omnipresent reminder of the county's economic vulnerability and its dependence on large employers.[9]

    Multiple attempts to reoccupy or reuse the building had failed over the 11-year vacancy period, most notably the abortive venture by Lindenburg Industries in 2014–2015, which could not overcome ownership complexity and liabilities associated with Catalon Chemical Corporation's historical presence on adjacent property. The failed Lindenburg attempt had been particularly discouraging: the company had been recruited, had invested capital in facility assessment and preliminary improvements, and had announced hiring intentions, yet ultimately withdrew from the project when legal and financial complications proved insurmountable. The Lindenburg failure reinforced perceptions among some county residents that the Thomasville building was "cursed" or permanently burdened with problems, discouraging additional revival attempts and contributing to a sense of economic stagnation and inevitable decline in the broader county narrative.[9]

    The successful occupation by Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks therefore represented the first unambiguous, stable reuse of the building since 2011 and broke what had begun to seem like an intractable cycle of vacancy and failed revitalization attempts. The arrival of commercial activity, employment, and productive use revitalized the symbolic meaning of the Thomasville site from a monument to economic decline to a potential catalyst for renewed growth and opportunity. The relocation also influenced discussions about property tax base expansion: the return of the massive industrial property to productive use would improve the county's and town's tax revenues, addressing budget pressures that had resulted from the 11-year revenue loss during the facility's vacancy.

    In the 2023 election cycle, Supervisor John Hinkle cited the expansion and success of Virginia MetalFab as a key accomplishment of county leadership and a reason for his re-election, demonstrating the political and community importance attributed to the companies' presence and growth trajectory. The presence of the two companies at the historic facility also influenced discussions regarding county industrial capacity, workforce availability, and regional competitiveness for business recruitment and retention.[10]

    County leadership also highlighted the relocation as evidence of successful economic development strategy execution. Unlike the failed Lindenburg Industries venture, which encountered legal and financial complications related to property ownership and environmental issues, the Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks relocation demonstrated that the building could be successfully reactivated with appropriate ownership structures, adequate capital investment, and aligned business operations. The relocation, therefore, contributed to county confidence that the Thomasville property would remain in productive commercial use long-term, reducing concerns that the massive industrial building would again face extended vacancy and deterioration.

    The relocation also influenced county planning and infrastructure discussions. The return of significant manufacturing employment raised questions about adequate water and sewer capacity, electrical distribution infrastructure, transportation network adequacy for industrial traffic, emergency service response capabilities, and potential future industrial development on adjacent properties and throughout the county. County planning and infrastructure agencies used the relocation as an impetus to conduct facility assessments, develop capacity expansion plans, and coordinate with the companies regarding future infrastructure requirements for continued growth.[16]

    In March 2024, a parade of 15 farm tractors representing Appomattox County High School's Future Farmers of America chapter traveled approximately 2.8 miles from Virginia MetalFab on Founders Lane to the high school campus. The route symbolically linked the manufacturing facility to youth agricultural and technical education, reinforcing connections between VMF's presence and the county's educational and workforce development infrastructure.[11]

    Operational integration and facility details

    The relocation to the Thomasville building represented a substantial logistics undertaking for both companies. Virginia MetalFab's metal fabrication operations required dedicated space for welding bays, plasma and oxy-acetylene cutting stations, brake presses, sheet metal shearing equipment, and grinding and finishing work areas. Stallworks' horse stall assembly and manufacturing operations demanded separate zones for wood and metal component preparation, assembly staging, quality control inspection areas, and finished goods storage. The 831,000-square-foot Thomasville building, with its multiple production bays and infrastructure designed to support large-scale manufacturing operations, proved more than adequate to accommodate both companies' operational requirements with ample room for expansion and future equipment additions.[5]

    The consolidation also enabled operational efficiencies: shared administrative functions, consolidated purchasing and vendor management, unified human resources and payroll systems, and coordinated equipment maintenance. By operating from a single location, both companies could reduce overhead costs, improve inventory management across both operations, and facilitate cross-training of employees where skill sets overlapped. Shared support functions—accounting, payroll processing, benefits administration, IT systems—eliminated duplicative overhead and improved administrative efficiency. Unified purchasing for consumables, maintenance supplies, and equipment components created volume discounts and improved supplier relationships. The relocation also standardized both companies' compliance and safety protocols under unified management, ensuring consistent application of workplace safety standards, environmental regulations, and quality assurance procedures across all operations.

    The single-location consolidation also created opportunities for operational flexibility and resource sharing. Employees with skills applicable to both metal fabrication and equipment assembly could be cross-deployed between operations based on production demand and scheduling needs. Specialized equipment or services could be shared or leased jointly rather than each company purchasing separately. Facility maintenance, security, and utilities management could be coordinated and optimized. Delivery and logistics could be consolidated, reducing transportation costs and environmental impacts. These operational efficiencies contributed to improved profitability and competitiveness for both companies compared to their previous independent operations, making the consolidated venture more resilient and better positioned for growth and market competition.[12]

    The facility itself, located at 310 Founders Lane in the Town of Appomattox, encompasses 85.5 acres of industrial land with ample parking for employees and visitors, sufficient acreage for potential future expansion or satellite operations, and direct access via Business Route 460 to major regional transportation corridors. The building's robust infrastructure, including substantial electrical capacity, water and sewer connections, loading docks, and railroad siding access (a remnant from the Thomasville era), provided capabilities necessary for both metal fabrication and manufacturing assembly operations.[12]

    Regional economic context and manufacturing sector

    Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks' arrival at the Thomasville site occurred within a critical juncture in Appomattox County's economic evolution. The county had experienced significant employment volatility over the preceding decade, with the 2008–2009 financial crisis particularly devastating to manufacturing sectors nationally. Following Thomasville's 2011 closure, which eliminated approximately 200 jobs, Appomattox County faced challenges in workforce retention, economic diversification, and business confidence in the region's future.

    By 2022, the county's economic development strategy had shifted toward recruiting specialized manufacturers and value-added production operations rather than attempting to replace large, vertically-integrated manufacturing plants like Thomasville. Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks aligned with this strategic shift: both companies represented higher-value-added manufacturing (custom metal fabrication versus commodity furniture), served regional and national markets with specialized products, and demonstrated ability to operate profitably at smaller employment scales than the county's historic manufacturing employers.[13]

    The central Virginia region's manufacturing base had evolved over the 2010s to include automotive suppliers, specialty metal fabricators, food processing operations, and light assembly manufacturing distributed across multiple counties. Appomattox County's proximity to major transportation routes (U.S. Routes 15 and 460, Interstate 81 approximately 40 miles north, Interstate 95 approximately 60 miles east) and its access to skilled or trainable labor made it competitively positioned for specialized manufacturing recruitment, though it faced competition from neighboring counties for available workforce and from other regions for capital investment.[13]

    The presence of Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks contributed to demonstrating that the Appomattox location remained viable for modern manufacturing operations, that the available workforce and community infrastructure could support established operations, and that the county remained committed to supporting manufacturing through economic development initiatives, workforce training partnerships, and business-friendly regulatory environments. The consolidation of two companies under single ownership and management at a shared facility also provided a model for other rural manufacturers facing similar economic pressures and operational constraints, demonstrating that regional consolidation, facility sharing, and shared services could enhance profitability and competitiveness for smaller manufacturers that might struggle operating independently.

    The relocation also contributed to Virginia's broader economic development narrative regarding rural revitalization and manufacturing resilience. State economic development officials highlighted the Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks success as an example of how rural communities and smaller manufacturers could adapt to contemporary economic challenges through innovation, strategic consolidation, and supportive local governance. The companies' presence at the Thomasville site became a case study in facility reuse, community economic recovery, and the viability of specialized manufacturing in rural economies, influencing economic development strategies and policy discussions across other rural Virginia communities facing similar post-industrial revitalization challenges.[16]

The facilities implemented environmental monitoring, maintained emergency response plans, and established community liaison processes to address neighbor concerns and facilitate information sharing about operational changes or issues that might affect surrounding residents and property owners.

    Challenges and growth opportunities (2023–2024)

    Following the December 2022 grand opening and the announcement of job creation targets, Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks faced both operational challenges and market opportunities typical of growth-phase manufacturing companies. The expansion timeline, originally projected at 18–24 months to reach the 200+ employment target, required sustained contract acquisition, investment in additional equipment, and successful recruitment and training of skilled manufacturing personnel in a regional labor market with competing demands from other employers.

    The metal fabrication sector experienced variable demand dynamics through 2023 and 2024, influenced by broader economic conditions, customer industry health, and regional construction and infrastructure activity levels. Specialized fabricators like Virginia MetalFab faced competition from larger regional competitors and potential in-sourcing by major customers seeking to bring fabrication work back from overseas suppliers. However, the company's investment in modern equipment, commitment to quality and on-time delivery, and established customer relationships positioned it to compete effectively in the specialized fabrication market.

    Stallworks faced market dynamics in the equestrian equipment sector, influenced by equine industry health, rural land values and property development trends, and competition from larger national suppliers. The consolidation with Virginia MetalFab under shared ownership and administration provided opportunities for cross-marketing, bundled service offerings to shared customer bases, and operational efficiencies that improved profitability and competitiveness compared to standalone operations. The horse stall sector, though niche compared to furniture or automotive manufacturing, represented a stable market segment with consistent demand from equine facilities, breeding operations, and horse boarding facilities throughout the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic regions.[15]

    Workforce development represented both a challenge and opportunity. Appomattox County's workforce, like most rural areas, experienced limited competition for skilled manufacturing labor, but also faced challenges recruiting and retaining workers willing to remain in rural locations with limited cultural amenities and entertainment options. Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks addressed workforce development through partnerships with local schools' Career and Technical Education (CTE) programs, participation in manufacturing awareness events, competitive wage structures, and potential advancement pathways into supervisory and technical roles.     The companies also recognized opportunities in the regional labor market. Appomattox County maintained a relatively stable population base with deep community roots, lower cost of living compared to metropolitan areas, and available housing at affordable prices. Regional workforce development institutions, including Southside Virginia Community College (SVCC) located in nearby Appomattox, offered CNC machining certification programs, welding training, and general manufacturing skills courses that could be coordinated with company hiring needs. The region's agricultural heritage and technical skill traditions created an available talent pool for manufacturing employment, and younger workers in the region increasingly recognized manufacturing as a viable alternative to agricultural work or out-migration to distant metropolitan areas. The companies' presence and growth opportunities thus aligned with broader workforce development initiatives and educational partnerships that emphasized rural manufacturing opportunities as economic development and career pathway strategies.[16]

    Supply chain and material sourcing represented additional operational considerations. The companies required reliable sources of steel, metal alloys, fasteners, welding consumables, lumber and metal components for horse stall manufacturing, and various supporting materials and equipment. Locating suppliers and managing inventory represented ongoing operational challenges typical of manufacturing enterprises. The consolidation at the Thomasville facility enabled centralized purchasing, potentially reducing material costs and improving supply chain efficiency compared to operating from separate facilities. Regional suppliers and distributors serving the central Virginia manufacturing sector provided needed materials and services, and the presence of Virginia MetalFab and Stallworks created opportunities for expanded business relationships between the companies and regional suppliers and service providers.[15]
