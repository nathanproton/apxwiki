    Wilbun's Supermarket Closure refers to the closure on August 1, 2010, of Wilbun's Supermarket, a family-owned grocery store in Appomattox, Virginia, that had served the community for seventy-five years.[1] The sudden closure ended a significant chapter in Appomattox's commercial history and marked the loss of a beloved local institution.[2] The store, located on Confederate Boulevard, had struggled financially for two years due to sluggish sales, reduced consumer spending during the economic recession, and customer losses following the closure of the Thomasville Furniture manufacturing facility.[3] The closure resulted in twenty-one job losses.[4]

    Background

    Wilbun's Supermarket originated in 1935 as a small country store near the community of Red House, Virginia, founded by Robert and Hazel Wilbun.[5] The store operated without running water or electricity in its early years but served as an important community gathering place.[6] Following relocation to the Town of Appomattox, the business moved through three different locations before settling in 1972 at a 1961-built supermarket on Confederate Boulevard that had previously operated as King's Supermarket under Ralph King.[7] The store underwent major remodeling in 1985, modernizing its facilities and expanding service capacity.[8]

    By closure, Wilbun's had become a respected independent grocer known for quality meats and a strong produce section that rivaled national chains.[9] Gerald Wilbun, co-owner with his wife Sandy, was an active community member who planned class reunions for Appomattox High School's Class of 1961 and regularly provided food from the store for local events.[10] Gerald was known for personally assisting customers by carrying grocery bags to their vehicles, embodying values his father Robert had taught: "Turn a lot of hot nickels instead of trying for a cold quarter."[11] The store was notably distinguished by Emmitt Tolbert, head meat-cutter for sixteen years, who became instrumental in revitalizing the Appomattox Lions Club's annual Food Festival.[12] Wilbun's was named Business of the Month by the Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce and maintained affiliation with Great Valu Foods as its primary supplier.[13]

    The Event

    Wilbun's Supermarket Closure occurred suddenly on Sunday, August 1, 2010.[14] Financial struggles had deepened over preceding years. Only three years before, the store employed fifty-four workers; by closure, staffing had been reduced to thirty-three employees, reflecting deteriorating financial performance.[15] Multiple economic factors precipitated the closure. The 2008–2010 financial crisis had reduced regional consumer spending, while the shutdown of nearby Thomasville Furniture—historically a major source of customer traffic—dealt a particularly heavy blow.[16] Gerald Wilbun noted: "We got a lot of customers from Thomasville, and when they started cutting back, we felt it."[17] The May 2010 opening of a Walmart Supercenter in Appomattox may have contributed marginally; the family estimated impact at approximately five percent, primarily attributable to broader economic conditions.[18]

    The decision to close reflected not only struggling financials but also Gerald's personal health circumstances. At sixty-seven, Gerald had experienced recent health challenges including hip and knee surgeries and pneumonia, limiting his ability to maintain demanding grocery business operations.[19] The entire Wilbun family—Gerald, Sandy, and son and store manager David—determined closure was necessary. Sandy recalled the emotional weight: "This is a baby we've nurtured, and it's really tough. My heart just can't cut the umbilical cord to our baby."[20] Gerald emphasized the gravity: "This was truly a hard decision."[21] David, while deeply affected, focused on business realities: "Looking at the numbers, I know we made the right decision."[22]

    Immediate Response

    The sudden closure shocked the Appomattox community and prompted immediate emotional response from longtime customers and employees. The loss was particularly acute given the store's seventy-five-year history and central role in local community life.

    On the evening of closure, Gerald Wilbun remained in the parking lot to speak with customers who arrived unaware the store had closed permanently. He reminisced with one couple for several hours, sharing memories and friendship. The couple's subsequent prayer with Gerald provided what he described as a deeply meaningful moment: "I am so blessed to have so many friends."[23] This impromptu gathering epitomized the personal relationships that had defined store operations for decades.

    Emmitt Tolbert, the iconic meat-cutter of sixteen years, was profoundly affected. Tolbert expressed sorrow about the closure and lamented his inability to formally say goodbye: "I miss my customers. I wish I had the chance to tell my customers good-bye, but things happened so fast, we didn't realize it was all over with."[24] His legacy at the store made him known throughout Central Virginia; his meat department quality and warm interactions with customers had been central to store appeal and had drawn shoppers across the region.[25]

    Aftermath and Impact

    The closure created a significant void in local grocery shopping options and contributed to ongoing economic challenges in Appomattox. The closure was part of broader patterns of retail consolidation and local business decline affecting small Virginia towns in the early twenty-first century. The 22,000-square-foot building, assessed at more than one million dollars, remained vacant as the Wilbun family considered retail use offers.[26]

    Loss of Wilbun's meant Appomattox residents had fewer independent grocery options, increasingly relying on larger chain stores and supercenters. The Town of Appomattox felt direct economic impact, losing the personal property tax revenues and meals tax revenue previously provided by Wilbun's.[27] For paycheck-to-paycheck families, closure meant reduced access to personalized customer service and competitive pricing. Emmitt Tolbert expressed ongoing concern about local families' food security, continuing to worry how residents would afford adequate nutrition following closure.[28]

    The Wilbun's parking lot eventually found new use when Iceland's, a frozen dessert vendor, opened in 2020 to serve Appomattox residents seeking ice cream and frozen treats.[29] This repurposing demonstrated the community's ability to adapt and create new commercial opportunities, though it represented significant change from the general grocery service Wilbun's had provided for three generations.

    Community Response

    Letters to the editor and community statements reflected widespread sadness about losing an important local institution. One longtime customer expressed dismay: "I was saddened to read that Wilbun's has closed. Even though I was not there very often, I always made a trip to Wilbun's. Their meat was excellent and the produce fairly priced."[30] Another reader from the Class of 1961 recalled store significance: "For as long as I can remember Wilbun's Supermarket was the place my family shopped for groceries because, 'You can't beat Mr. Wilbun's prices,' my mother would always say."[31] Upon learning of closure, this reader noted with emotion: "I had tears in my eyes as I left Appomattox. It was the end of an era and a special part of my hometown no longer existed."[32]

    A couple living in Florida who frequented Wilbun's on visits home expressed concern: "This event will create a void in the community that will long time be missed. Their efforts in providing service to the public in not only the quality products they provided but community contributions as well."[33] They wished Gerald and Sandy "a very happy and enjoyable retirement."[34]

    The Appomattox Lions Club formally recognized the store's contributions during October 2010 dedication of a new Community Park pavilion. The pavilion had been funded through proceeds from the Lions Club's annual Food Festival, an event Emmitt Tolbert and the Wilbun family had been instrumental in developing. Lions Club member Darrell Puckett stated: "If it hadn't been for Gerald and Emmitt and Sandy, we don't know what we would have done."[35]

    Gerald Wilbun issued a final public statement thanking employees, parents, and community: "I was to thank all my wonderful employees for all our years in business. … I want to give a special 'thank you' for my parents Robert and Hazel Wilbun, who raised me up in the grocery store. … My mom and dad taught me a lot about the grocery business and the value of a dollar, or back then, a nickel."[36] He concluded: "All things cannot last forever. If I may bow out now and go smell the roses and enjoy this time left. Thank you my fine and wonderful friends. May God bless you and keep you forever more."[37]

    Legacy

    The 2010 closure of Wilbun's Supermarket remains a watershed moment in Appomattox County history, symbolizing challenges faced by family-owned, independent retail businesses in an era of economic consolidation and changing consumer patterns. The store's seventy-five-year history—from modest 1935 Red House country store origins to respected regional grocer—represented achievement of multiple Wilbun family generations.

    Seven years later, October 2017 closure of a Food Lion supermarket was described as "the second grocery chain in two years to shut down locally," explicitly referencing Wilbun's closure as significant prior event in commercial history.[38] This second closure highlighted ongoing difficulty for Appomattox to maintain diverse grocery retail options.

    The Wilbun family's post-closure retirement reflected relief at stepping back from decades of intensive labor. Gerald and Sandy pursued long-deferred vacations and quality family time, including trips to their Smith Mountain Lake home.[39] Sandy devoted more time to elderly out-of-state parents and pursued bridge hobby interests, while Gerald spent time with granddaughters.[40] Son David moved forward with optimism about future opportunities, later finding meaningful employment as Appomattox Tractor Supply manager, where he organized community events and pet adoption programs.[41]

    Emmitt Tolbert retired but continued community service, participating in Appomattox Railroad Festival booths and providing affordable catered food for local events, continuing the charitable mission defining his sixteen Wilbun years.[42] His work exemplified the quality and community commitment the store represented.

    Wilbun's Supermarket Closure exemplifies broader economic and retail transformations affecting small American towns in the early twenty-first century, where local, family-owned businesses struggle to compete with larger chain retailers and big-box stores. The store's story remains important to Appomattox residents as reminder of community interdependence, local business significance, and profound impact business closures have on small town life and social cohesion.
