    Zoning and land use in Appomattox County encompasses the regulatory frameworks, comprehensive plans, and land-use policies that have governed development across Appomattox County, Virginia, and its incorporated towns since the late 1960s. The Town of Appomattox adopted its first zoning ordinance effective January 1, 1968, while the county followed two decades later with its own ordinance in 1988.[1][2] Over the subsequent decades, the zoning landscape has been shaped by recurring tensions between agricultural preservation and residential growth, by disputes over commercial siting, and by the evolving relationship between county and town governance structures. Major milestones include a three-year rewrite of the county zoning ordinance completed in 2009, the Town of Appomattox's creation of its own independent planning commission in 2013, the adoption of separate comprehensive plans by both the town and county, and ongoing amendments to address emerging land-use categories such as utility-scale solar energy farms and short-term rentals.

    Origins of zoning

    Town of Appomattox zoning ordinance (1967–1968)

    The earliest recorded discussions of land-use regulation in the Appomattox area occurred in the Town of Appomattox during the late 1950s and 1960s. In June 1959, Mrs. Tulloch of the League of Virginia Municipalities appeared before the Town Council to discuss the possibility of zoning within the town.[3] Over the following years, the town engaged the Appomattox County Planning Commission—which at that time served as the joint planning body for both the county and its towns—in a series of discussions about establishing zoning regulations.

    On August 13, 1969, a joint public hearing was held by the Appomattox County Planning Commission and the Appomattox Town Council at the Municipal Building to consider zoning matters. The Planning Commission at that time was chaired by H. B. Pack, who also served as Mayor of Appomattox.[4] However, the town's zoning ordinance had actually been adopted slightly earlier: it was duly considered following a public hearing on November 15, 1967, and became effective on January 1, 1968.[5] The 1968 ordinance established basic zoning districts within the town limits and defined terms for land-use purposes. It would remain in effect, with only minor amendments, for over four decades.

    The adoption was not without controversy. At the 1969 joint hearing, Mrs. J. H. Wilson questioned why a building permit had been issued before the zoning ordinance was adopted and expressed opposition to zoning changes. Mrs. Beulah Jamerson spoke on behalf of her mother against the prospect of apartments on Sunnydale Avenue, arguing that property owners had agreed to sell lots only for single-family dwellings. Frank H. Carwile, who owned four houses on Sunnydale, warned that his investment would be jeopardized by multi-family construction.[6]

    County zoning ordinance (1988)

    While the Town of Appomattox regulated land use within its corporate limits from 1968, unincorporated Appomattox County did not adopt its own zoning ordinance until 1988.[2] The ordinance established the basic zoning districts that would govern rural land use for the next two decades, including the A-1 Agricultural District, R-1 and R-2 Residential Districts, B-1 General Commercial District, M-1 Industrial District, and the IP Planned Industrial District. It also created a Board of Zoning Appeals (BZA) and codified the role of the joint Appomattox County Planning Commission.[7]

    The 1988 ordinance designated much of the county as A-1 Agricultural. At the time, the one-acre minimum lot size in the agricultural district was considered adequate for preserving rural character. That assumption would be revisited multiple times in the decades that followed as residential subdivisions gradually consumed farmland along the county's primary routes.

    Early governance structures

    Under the original 1988 framework, the county's zoning apparatus consisted of three bodies. The Appomattox County Joint Planning Commission served as an advisory body for both the county and its incorporated towns; it comprised ten members, with five appointed by the Board of Supervisors (one per voting district), three by the Town of Appomattox, and two by the Town of Pamplin. The Board of Zoning Appeals originally included one representative from the Town of Pamplin, one from the Town of Appomattox, and three from the county at large. The Board of Supervisors retained final authority over rezoning petitions, conditional use permits, and ordinance amendments.[7][8]

    The county's Building Inspector, Bill Caldwell, served concurrently as clerk to the Planning Commission throughout the 1990s and early 2000s, reading petitions into the record at public hearings and managing the administrative flow of zoning applications.[9]

    Early rezoning activity and the Environmental Hazard District (1992–2002)

    The earliest Board of Supervisors meeting minutes available in the digital archive date from July 1992 and reveal an active zoning calendar even in the county's early years under the 1988 ordinance. At the July 7, 1992, public hearing, with Chairman W. E. "Bill" Jamerson presiding, the Board heard petitions for conditional use permits ranging from a home auto repair shop (petition 92-164 from Richard James Purcell, Jr.) to requests for mobile home placement on rural lots and variances to setback requirements for livestock structures.[9] These early cases established a pattern that would recur for decades: rural landowners seeking flexibility within the A-1 district for non-traditional uses, often involving mobile homes or agricultural accessory structures.

    A notable early controversy involved the Environmental Hazard (EH) Zoning District. In a special meeting on an unrecorded date in late 1992 or early 1993, the Board of Supervisors convened to discuss the implications of the EH district designation and the possibility of amending and reenacting portions of the zoning ordinance. County Attorney Thomas W. Lawson and County Administrator Gordon L. Rice were present. The Board had obtained specialized outside counsel from Richmond for advice on technical zoning matters and was advised that the EH district, as applied, imposed restrictions that raised legal concerns.[10] Among the affected properties was the Mabel Moore Lime Plant. In May 1993, following a joint public hearing on March 11, the Board adopted Rezoning Ordinance Z93-3, rezoning Tax Map Parcel 17(A)29 from EH Environmental Hazard District to A-1 Agricultural District.[11] The Board also directed the County Administrator to prepare rezoning applications for all remaining properties zoned Environmental Hazard District, effectively phasing out the designation.[10]

    Another significant development during this period was the adoption of a subdivision ordinance. In July 1992, the Board authorized a citizen review committee to examine the existing Subdivision Ordinance, with a target of completing recommendations by September 1992. The committee included representatives from VDOT, local surveyors, a developer, the Building Inspector, county attorneys, and other community figures.[12]

    The late 1990s also saw the county grapple with a contentious biosolids ordinance. In 2001, the Board of Supervisors held a public hearing on regulations governing the land application of treated sewage sludge. County Planner Brandon Stidham presided over testimony from both biosolid contractors (Synagro and NutriBlend) and concerned citizens. The contractors raised objections to signage requirements and notification provisions, while residents expressed concerns about odor and water contamination.[13] In December 2003, a federal judge—U.S. District Judge Norman K. Moon—declared the county's biosolids ordinance illegal, striking it down on the grounds that the county's restrictions were inconsistent with Virginia state regulations. The ruling was a significant victory for eleven local farmers who had challenged the ordinance.[14]

    Growth pressures and new controversies (2003–2005)

    The retirement village rezoning

    One of the first major rezoning controversies of the newspaper era involved a proposed retirement community in late 2003. The Appomattox County Board of Supervisors considered rezoning land to R-2 Residential to accommodate the development, but county officials expressed concerns that if the retirement project failed, the R-2 zoning could allow the property to become a trailer park. Legal doubts also arose regarding the enforceability of an age-restriction clause in the rezoning conditions. A reader writing to the Times-Virginian in June 2004 argued that the age restriction could be nullified in federal court, calling the clause legally questionable.[15][16]

    The Town of Appomattox entered the controversy in November 2003, when the Town Council said it would reconsider providing water and sewer service to the proposed retirement village, contingent on the developer securing a preliminary engineering report from the Virginia Department of Health. The Council had previously rejected the proposal due to concerns about infrastructure capacity.[17]

    The concrete plant dispute

    In March 2005, Lynchburg Ready Mix presented a proposal to construct a $250,000 concrete batch facility on two-and-a-half acres on Business 460, adjacent to the Town Ballpark and Appomattox Community Center. The company sought a conditional use permit for the Park Lane property.[18] The proposal immediately drew fierce opposition from parents and residents who cited health and safety concerns about concrete dust near recreational fields where children played. One resident wrote an open letter urging parents to attend the March 21 public hearing, warning of the dangers of dust inhalation.[19]

    At the hearing, approximately sixty citizens packed the meeting room. The Appomattox County Planning Commission deadlocked 4–4 and could not make a recommendation, sending the issue to the Appomattox Town Council for final determination.[20] Letters to the editor reflected the intensity of feeling; one resident writing from overseas expressed a sense of helplessness that officials were considering the permit.[21] Facing overwhelming public opposition, Lynchburg Ready Mix withdrew its permit application in April 2005 and announced it would seek an alternative location near the Appomattox County community of Evergreen, on a three-acre tract on State Route 630.[22]

    The biosolids ordinance

    The federal court's 2003 ruling striking down the county's biosolids ordinance left the county without local regulatory authority over sludge application. In October 2004, the Board of Supervisors approved the rezoning of a 12-acre tract near Concord for a solid-waste transfer station on a 5–0 vote, with sixteen restrictions placed on the facility.[23] The transfer station rezoning itself would become a recurring point of contention; in 2019, a citizen accused the Board of engaging in questionable "spot zoning" in approving the site, arguing that the decision disregarded the county's comprehensive plan.[24]

    The Scenic Corridor Overlay District debate (2006–2008)

    The most contentious zoning proposal of the mid-2000s originated not in the county but in the Town of Appomattox. In November 2006, the Town Council proposed creating a Scenic Corridor Overlay District that would require all future businesses—and existing businesses undertaking exterior renovations—to obtain a conditional use permit. The overlay would encompass most areas near the town's main roadways, including Court Street, Confederate Boulevard, and the Route 460 corridor.[25]

    The proposal drew immediate and sharp criticism. The Appomattox County Board of Supervisors publicly condemned the plan at its own meeting, with Sammy Carter, chairman of the Board, encouraging the Planning Commission and citizens to oppose it. Supervisors objected primarily because the ordinance contained no specifics about what standards businesses would have to meet.[26] At a packed public hearing on November 13, 2006, nearly all citizens who spoke were opposed. The Appomattox County Planning Commission subsequently voted 6–3 to reject the overlay proposal, though as an advisory body it had no binding authority.[27]

    Town Council pressed for more information rather than calling an immediate vote, and discussions continued into 2007. On March 5, a meeting was held to discuss the meaning, purpose, and pros and cons of an overlay district, with County Planner Johnnie Roark and the entire council invited.[28] The Council subsequently invited Lynchburg City Planner Tom Martin to make presentations on landscape and sign ordinances, seeking a model for more targeted regulations.[29]

    The overlay concept was eventually abandoned in favor of a narrower approach. In April 2008, the Town Council voted unanimously to adopt a new landscaping ordinance that included regulations for trees, parking, and zoning for new business developments—despite the Planning Commission having voted 7–2 against it.[30] The landscaping ordinance represented a modest version of the growth-management goals that the overlay district had attempted to achieve.

    County zoning ordinance rewrite (2006–2009)

    In February 2006, the county hired Johnnie Roark as its new County Planner. Roark, a 35-year-old Blackstone native who had been in the planning field for about eight years, lived in Appomattox County and viewed the position as an opportunity to serve his own community.[31] He would soon undertake the most significant revision of the county's land-use regulations since their original adoption.

    By October 2009, the Appomattox County Planning Commission had spent three years revising the zoning ordinance and map, which had not been substantively updated since the 1988 adoption.[32] Roark described the effort as a complete rewrite, calling it "very significant because it will change the development regulations throughout the county." Among the major proposed changes were a new three-acre minimum lot size for all agriculture-zoned land (up from one acre), a minimum 150-foot lot frontage requirement, the creation of a Corridor Overlay Zoning District along all primary routes within the county, and the establishment of additional zoning classifications.[33]

    A public hearing was held on October 19, 2009, at the Appomattox Community Center. Nearly 100 citizens attended, and all ten who spoke expressed opposition to various parts of the proposed ordinance. The three-acre minimum was the primary point of contention; landowners feared it would restrict their ability to subdivide property for family members. There was also widespread confusion about how the new restrictions would affect existing landowners. Board Chairman Tom Conrad stated that the Board would send the ordinance back to the Planning Commission to address the concerns raised.[34]

    In December 2009, the Board of Supervisors formally asked the Planning Commission to modify the proposed ordinance. Roark explained that the three-acre minimum was intended to preserve farmland, noting that the Planning Commission believed the one-acre minimum had been allowing residential development to consume agricultural land too rapidly. He stressed that all current uses would be grandfathered under the new proposals. The Board, however, felt the restrictions as proposed went too far and requested revisions before it would schedule a vote.[35]

    The Land Use Ordinance (2008)

    Concurrent with the zoning rewrite, the county adopted a separate but related piece of land-use policy: the Appomattox County Land Use Ordinance, which took effect in 2008 (with some sources citing 2009 for full implementation). The ordinance was designed to provide tax relief to farmers by assessing qualifying agricultural land at a lower rate than its fair market value. To qualify, a landowner needed at least five acres of open land and a minimum of $1,000 in gross agricultural income.[36]

    The program's adoption was driven by a significant increase in land values during the previous year's reassessment, which had caused property tax bills to spike for farmers whose land had appreciated in market value but remained in agricultural use.[37] Approximately 200 citizens packed the Appomattox Community Center ten years earlier to show support for the proposed ordinance.[38] In its first year, 552 applications were submitted, generating $9,647 in application fees. Each acre of affected land was assessed at $1,200 per acre, resulting in $218,000 in savings for county farmers and a minimal increase of 1.5 cents in real estate taxes for non-participating landowners.[39]

    The Land Use Ordinance became a recurring point of political debate. Supervisor Gary Tanner cited its implementation as one of his key accomplishments.[40] Commissioner of Revenue Sara Henderson, who implemented the ordinance in 2009, also counted it among her achievements.[41] However, critics argued that the program cost the county approximately $300,000 annually in foregone real estate tax revenue, effectively subsidizing farmers at the expense of other taxpayers. In 2012, a proposal was floated to repeal the program, arguing it would generate approximately one million dollars over four years.[42] By 2015, a citizen writing to the Times-Virginian highlighted the significant annual cost—over $320,000 per year, or nearly $1 million over three years—and advocated for its repeal.[43]

    Dollar General and Pamplin rezoning (2010)

    The 2010 rezoning for a Dollar General store in the Town of Pamplin illustrated the multi-jurisdictional complexity of zoning in Appomattox County. The proposed site—a 1.1-acre vacant lot beside the old Town Office on Pamplin Road (Business 460)—was split-zoned between town and county jurisdiction. In September 2010, the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors voted 5–0 to approve the county-side zoning change, and the Appomattox County Planning Commission recommended approval. In October, the Pamplin Town Council voted unanimously to change its portion of the zoning from Village Center District (V-1) to business use. Mayor Robert Mitchell stated that the Dollar General would financially benefit the town.[44][45]

    Town of Appomattox zoning overhaul (2011–2013)

    Creation of the Town Planning Commission

    On August 15, 2012, the Appomattox Town Council took a step that fundamentally altered the governance of land use in the community: it voted to create an independent Town of Appomattox Planning Commission, effective January 1, 2013. Previously, the county's joint Planning Commission had served all jurisdictions. The new five-member town commission would be responsible for recommendations on zoning ordinances, maintaining a land-use inventory, drafting subdivision regulations, and comprehensive planning.[46]

    In December 2012, the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors formally accommodated the town's request by removing the three Town of Appomattox representatives from the county Planning Commission. The reconstituted County Planning Commission would now have seven members: five appointed by supervisors (one per voting district) and two appointed by the Town of Pamplin (one of which could be a council member). Several supervisors had long criticized the old makeup because six of the ten members lived in one of the two towns, giving town residents disproportionate influence over county zoning decisions.[47]

    Town ordinance rewrite

    Simultaneous with the planning commission restructuring, the Town of Appomattox undertook a comprehensive overhaul of its zoning ordinance—the first major revision since the original 1968 adoption, a span of 43 years. Interim Town Manager Bill Gillespie noted that the community had changed dramatically since 1969 and that the existing ordinance mixed residential areas with business areas in ways that no longer reflected the town's needs.[48]

    The overhaul introduced several significant changes. Publicly owned land was identified and zoned as a distinct category for the first time. Business zoning classifications were expanded to separate different types of commercial activity. Residential and business areas were more clearly delineated to prevent incompatible uses from developing adjacent to each other. By the end of 2012, approximately ninety percent of the revision work was complete, and the town planned to send letters to affected property owners informing them of proposed changes, followed by a public hearing in February 2013.[48][49]

    County ordinance re-codification (2014)

    Following the Town's restructuring, the county undertook its own modernization effort. In 2014, the Appomattox County Planning Commission and Board of Supervisors re-codified the zoning ordinance—previously known as Chapter 170—as Chapter 19.6 of the county code. The entire ordinance was renumbered, reorganized, and updated using current state code references and contemporary zoning language.[50] As summarized in later ordinance amendments, "the original Zoning Ordinance was adopted in 1988 and was overhauled in 2014 with input from community leaders."[51]

    The re-codification preserved the basic district structure (A-1, R-1, R-2, B-1, M-1, IP) but incorporated the changes that had been debated during the 2006–2009 rewrite process, including updated setback requirements, conditional use provisions, and new land-use classifications. Notably, the 2014 version reclassified religious assemblies (churches) from permitted use to conditional use in residential districts—a change that would itself become controversial in 2018.[52]

    Comprehensive plans

    Town of Appomattox Comprehensive Plan (2015, 2020)

    With its own planning commission now in place, the Town of Appomattox began developing its first independent comprehensive plan in 2014. The effort was mandated by Virginia state law following the establishment of the separate planning commission. The town incorporated elements from its existing master plan and solicited citizen input through letters, surveys, a project website, public notices, meetings, and interviews.[53][54]

    The Appomattox Planning Commission collaborated with Region 2000 (the Central Virginia Planning District Commission) to develop the plan, which outlined action items for coordinated community development spanning demographics, land use, education, transportation, and housing. In September 2015, the Planning Commission recommended the completed plan to the Town Council, which voted to adopt it during its September 14 meeting.[55][56]

    An update cycle began in 2019, when the town launched a citizen survey as the first step in refreshing the comprehensive plan. The survey, available online and mailed with water bills, sought citizen opinions on the town's future and challenges. Results revealed widespread appreciation for the town's small-town character and historic feel, alongside a desire for more employment opportunities, a movie theater, and additional retail options.[57][58]

    The Town Planning Commission reviewed the update with Philip Gabathuler of the Central Virginia Planning District Commission, covering goals across housing, transportation, education, community development, and tourism.[59] On August 25, 2020, a joint public hearing was held for the Town of Appomattox 2040 Comprehensive Plan, which served as a guide for future land-use policy and decision-making. The Town Council adopted the plan on September 14, 2020.[60]

    Appomattox County Comprehensive Plan (2015–2021)

    At the county level, the comprehensive planning process proceeded on a parallel but separate track. In September 2015, the county launched a citizen survey to develop goals and incentives for the next two decades, covering public services, parks, schools, and economic development.[61] In December 2014, Planning Commission member Kevin O'Brien presented 14 community action goals to the Board of Supervisors, to be implemented over two to five years and incorporated into the county's Comprehensive Plan.[62]

    In August 2016, a joint meeting was held between the Appomattox Town Council and the County Board of Supervisors at the Museum of the Confederacy conference room to discuss overlapping goals and objectives from their respective comprehensive plans and identify areas for future collaborative projects.[63]

    The county's Comprehensive Plan was reviewed and updated through a formal process in 2021. A public hearing was held in October 2021, during which no members of the public spoke. Supervisor Watkins Abbitt Jr. expressed discomfort voting on the update without reviewing all proposed changes and requested the matter be tabled. The Board ultimately approved the 2021 Comprehensive Plan Update in November 2021.[64][65] In May 2024, the plan was further amended to add the Pamplin Main Street Improvement Project to the Transportation Element at the request of the Pamplin City Town Council, a step intended to enhance the town's eligibility for external funding.[66]

    Future Land Use Map

    The Future Land Use Map is a component of the county's Comprehensive Plan that designates preferred land-use categories for different areas of the county. The map has been revised at several key junctures. In October 2011, the Planning Commission discussed potential amendments to both the zoning ordinance and the Future Land Use Map.[67] At its organizational meeting on January 10, 2012, the Appomattox Joint Planning Commission placed the Future Land Use Map update on its agenda as a priority discussion item.[68]

    A significant revision accompanied the 2021 Comprehensive Plan Update. The county's Department of Community Development prepared a report detailing proposed changes to the Future Land Use Map, including a list of specific area amendments, draft and adopted maps for comparison, and updated goals and objectives.[69] The process of maintaining the Future Land Use Map as a living document—rather than a static plan adopted once and rarely revisited—reflected a growing sophistication in the county's approach to land-use governance.

    Zoning amendments and disputes (2015–2024)

    Intensive farming ordinance (2015)

    In June 2015, a public hearing was held on a proposed ordinance to define and regulate intensive farming operations. Supervisor Gary Tanner made a motion to approve the ordinance, but the adoption was complicated by disagreements over the number of animals permitted in certain types of operations. Both Supervisor Tanner and Chairman Willie Craft expressed a desire to reduce the allowed number of pigs in a "pig parlor" from what had been stated in the draft ordinance. Community Development Director Johnnie Roark was tasked with revising the figures to satisfy the Board before final adoption.[70]

    Outdoor theater rezoning (2016)

    In 2016, Dustin Williams sought rezoning and a conditional use permit to establish an outdoor theater venue on property on Country Club Road. The Appomattox County Planning Commission approved the request, and the Board of Supervisors subsequently granted both the rezoning and the conditional use permit.[71]

    Church zoning amendment (2018)

    A proposed 2018 amendment to the zoning ordinance would have changed all land use for religious assemblies from permitted use to conditional use across the county—meaning all new churches, or existing churches planning additions, would have to apply for a permit and undergo a public hearing. Community Development Director Johnnie Roark had brought the proposal forward, noting that the 2014 re-codification had already required conditional use permits for churches in residential districts, and the new amendment would extend that requirement to all zoning districts.[52]

    The Planning Commission held a public discussion on August 8, 2018, at 153A Morton Lane. After hearing concerns from community members, the commission tabled the proposed amendment, effectively shelving it. The decision reflected the political sensitivity of imposing additional regulatory requirements on houses of worship in a rural, church-going community.[72]

    Islamic cemetery rezoning (2018)

    One of the more culturally charged zoning episodes occurred in June 2018, when the Greater Lynchburg Islamic Association proposed rezoning a property at the intersection of Double Bridges Road and Hundley Springs Road for use as a cemetery. The Appomattox Planning Commission heard community concerns at a public meeting on June 13. Residents expressed worries about property values and well water contamination, noting that Muslim burial practices do not typically include a casket or vault. The proposed cemetery would fall under both state and county laws and regulations.[73]

    The Planning Commission announced that it would conduct research to determine whether the concerns about groundwater contamination were warranted and continued the discussion at its July 2018 meeting.[74]

    The pot belly pig case (2019)

    A zoning enforcement case involving a Vietnamese pot belly pig drew public attention in early 2019, illustrating the sometimes unexpected intersections of zoning law and daily life. Jeffrey Moore, Jr., kept a pot belly pig at a Matthews Road residence. Community Development Director Johnnie Roark stated his authority to interpret the zoning definitions of "livestock" and "household pets," and noted that Moore had been given one year to resolve the situation. The case went before Appomattox County General District Court Judge Robert Woodson, Jr., who scheduled a decision for April 3, 2019.[75]

    Utility-scale solar energy zoning (2019–2020)

    As Virginia's renewable energy landscape evolved, Appomattox County moved to regulate utility-scale solar energy installations. In December 2019, the Planning Commission recommended amendments to both the Comprehensive Plan and the zoning ordinance text to address solar energy farms. The commission forwarded a resolution to the Board of Supervisors recommending an amendment to the Comprehensive Plan that would establish guidelines for siting utility-scale solar energy projects, designating preferred zoning districts and planning factors.[76]

    A corresponding Zoning Ordinance Amendment Package was prepared, including the addition of a new Section 19.6-97 (Utility Scale Solar Energy Farm) and text amendments to district land-use categories across the A-1, R-1, R-2, M-1, and IP districts, along with updated definitions and recreational vehicle regulations.[77]

    The Board of Supervisors held a public hearing in February 2020 and approved the Comprehensive Plan amendment. However, it deferred action on the zoning ordinance text changes, seeking additional review time.[78] The solar energy provisions were among the most forward-looking zoning actions taken by the county, anticipating an industry that was rapidly expanding across rural Virginia.

    Short-term rental regulations (2020–2022)

    The emergence of platforms like Airbnb prompted a two-year regulatory debate. In July 2020, the Appomattox County Planning Commission considered a proposed zoning ordinance amendment that would require a $200 conditional use permit for short-term rentals. At its July 8 meeting, seven citizens rose to the podium; all seven opposed the amendment. Community members argued that the permit requirement would impose an unnecessary burden on property owners who occasionally rented their homes.[79]

    After continued discussion at the August 2020 meeting, the Planning Commission voted against requiring a conditional use permit for short-term rentals. The commission had considered creating a new land-use category and definition specific to short-term rentals, but staff requests for clarification on land-use categories and negative public feedback led the commission to decline the proposal.[80]

    In the Town of Appomattox, the trajectory was different. In 2020, the Town Council discussed a proposed ordinance to allow mixed zoning, specifically enabling loft apartments above businesses in B1 and B2 districts. The town attorney recommended inclusion in the zoning code, and the Planning Commission voted to advance the proposal.[81] Then, in March 2022, the Town Council voted 5–1 to permit short-term rentals in residential zones R1 and R3. Interim Town Manager Roxanne Casto noted that two existing short-term rental sites would be affected—one on Highland Avenue and another under renovation on Burke Street.[82]

    Abattoir conditional use (2020)

    In November 2020, the Board of Supervisors held a public hearing and adopted a zoning ordinance amendment adding "abattoir" (slaughterhouse) as a conditional use in the Agricultural Zoning District. At the same meeting, a public hearing was approved for an amendment to the Comprehensive Plan Growth Management section, requested by the Planning Commission to facilitate a citizen's conservation easement. A separate hearing was scheduled for a proposed reduction in setbacks from external property lines and from creeks, streams, ponds, or wetlands.[83]

    Board of Zoning Appeals composition dispute (2024)

    The question of representation on the Board of Zoning Appeals resurfaced in January 2024, when Pamplin Mayor Sarah Blackwell addressed the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors to request that the county reconsider the BZA's composition. Blackwell explained that when the BZA was originally established in 1988, it consisted of one representative from the Town of Pamplin, one from the Town of Appomattox, and three from the county at large. However, around 2002, the composition had been changed to two representatives from the Town of Appomattox and three from the county, eliminating Pamplin's direct representation.[8]

    Blackwell proposed that by the end of 2024, when the second Appomattox Town representative's term expired, the county revert to the original 1988 agreement. She acknowledged uncertainty about what had prompted the 2002 change, speculating that it may have been due to difficulty finding a Pamplin resident willing to serve.[84]

    Cell tower permits

    Wireless communication facilities became a recurring item on zoning agendas from the early 1990s onward. As early as November 1992, GTE Virginia sought a conditional use permit for a remote communication switching facility on Route 460 in an M-1 industrial zone.[85] In the 2019–2020 period, the Planning Commission and Board of Supervisors processed multiple cell tower applications, including permits for US Cellular towers on Piney Mountain Road and Hancock Road, and a Verizon facility on Trinity Road.[86] In September 2024, the Board approved a zoning permit for a 235-foot self-support cell tower at Cole's Farm LLC on Red House Road, with Verizon Wireless as the anchor tenant.[87]
