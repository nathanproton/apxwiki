Overview

Appomattox County, Virginia, is a rural locality in the central Piedmont region administered by a five-member elected Board of Supervisors. The county's primary locally controlled revenue streams are the real estate tax and the personal property tax, both administered through the Commissioner of the Revenue's office and collected by the County Treasurer. Because the county's tax base is largely residential and agricultural—with limited commercial and industrial development—the Board of Supervisors has historically confronted recurring tension between rising expenditure demands (driven largely by school funding, state mandates, and infrastructure) and the capacity of a modest tax base to sustain them.

The period documented here, drawing primarily on the Times-Virginian newspaper archive (2004–2024), Board of Supervisors meeting minutes (1992–2025), and county government PDF records (2004–2023), reveals a pattern of periodic rate increases separated by multi-year freezes—a pattern shaped by reassessment cycles, state reimbursement changes, agricultural tax policy debates, and local political pressures.

It is essential to note that the Town of Appomattox is an incorporated municipality within the county boundaries and levies its own real estate and personal property taxes. Town residents pay both county and town taxes, at very different combined rates than county-only residents. The town's rates are addressed separately in this article and should not be confused with the county rates that are the primary subject.

Tax Administration

Appomattox County's tax system involves several independently elected constitutional officers. The Commissioner of the Revenue assesses property and administers the tax rolls. For much of the period covered in this article, the Commissioner was Monte Mays, who served multiple terms and was a vocal advocate for policy changes including the Land Use ordinance, the elderly/disabled relief program, and the Personal Property Tax Relief Act implementation. Mays was succeeded by Sara Henderson, who served as Commissioner through at least 2018 and was instrumental in the 2008 expansion of the elderly and disabled tax relief thresholds. The County Treasurer, a separately elected officer responsible for collecting taxes and receiving payments, was Janet Belter during much of this period (Virginia income tax returns for 2009 were directed to her office1).

The County Administrator plays an important role in budget preparation and tax rate recommendations. Aileen Ferguson served as County Administrator during the mid-2000s budget discussions, including the 2006 proposal to raise the personal property tax rate. Tax rate decisions require the Board to formally advertise any proposed rate and hold a public hearing before adoption. Virginia law requires thirty days of advance advertising for budgets in reassessment years, a rule that delayed the county's 2008-09 budget hearing.2

The county's County Attorney, J.G. (Johnnie) Overstreet, played a role in several tax-related legal matters, including the defense of the 2008 reassessment against civil lawsuits and the legal analysis of whether a Land Use ordinance could be adopted outside of a reassessment year.

Real Estate Tax — Chronological History

Pre-2004 Background

The pre-2004 real estate tax rate history is partially reconstructed from references in later newspaper articles. A 2018 article in the Times-Virginian referenced a Board of Supervisors vote approximately twenty years prior to approve a property tax increase of nearly 60 percent, generating an additional $120,000 for county funds—placing that event around 1998.3 A 2008 article, summarizing the history of rate changes, explicitly notes that "a previous increase of 25 cents occurred in 2000."4 This appears to have been the most significant single increase in modern county history, moving the rate from the upper-40-cent range to approximately 66 cents per $100 of assessed value. A 2005 article corroborates that the last real estate tax increase before 2005 occurred in 2000, and that the rate had held steady for four consecutive years afterward.5

Period of Stability (FY2001–FY2005)

From the beginning of fiscal year 2001 through fiscal year 2004–05, the Appomattox County real estate tax rate remained fixed at 66 cents per $100 of assessed value. In May 2004, the Board of Supervisors voted 5-0 to maintain this rate for a fourth consecutive year, keeping personal property and merchant's capital taxes unchanged as well.6 A February 2005 article noted that this stability reflected a period of relative budget equilibrium, though clouds were forming on the horizon: the county was facing significant capital expenditures including a new landfill cell, a new animal shelter, and construction of an E-911 Call Center.7

First Increase in Five Years (FY2005–06): 66¢ → 72¢

In spring 2005, County Administrator Aileen Ferguson presented projections showing that the county's upcoming capital projects—the new landfill cell, the animal shelter, and the E-911 Call Center—could require a rate increase of 7 to 10 cents per $100 of assessed value.8 The Board of Supervisors initially proposed a six-cent increase, which would raise the rate from 66 cents to 72 cents. A public hearing was held on May 16, 2005, at the Town Office on Linden Street.9

On May 26, 2005, the Board of Supervisors voted unanimously (5-0) to approve the six-cent increase, raising the rate to 72 cents per $100, effective for fiscal year 2005–06. The increase was projected to generate approximately $400,000 in additional annual revenue, helping balance a $29.1 million budget.10 Personal property and merchant's capital tax rates were held unchanged. Board Chairman Sam Carter suggested at the time that the increase might prevent future tax hikes by positioning the county to meet demands expected before the upcoming reassessment, then scheduled for 2008.11

It is worth noting that Commissioner of Revenue Monte Mays had been urging the Board to consider raising the personal property tax rate as well, arguing that vehicle owners should bear a higher tax burden than farmland owners, particularly in light of discussions about a potential Land Use ordinance.12 The Board declined to act on the personal property tax at this time.

FY2006-07: Personal Property Raised, Real Estate Held at 72¢

For fiscal year 2006–07, the Board proposed a $31.89 million budget and decided to hold the real estate tax rate at 72 cents per $100, instead raising the personal property tax for the first time in thirty-two years. In April 2006, County Administrator Aileen Ferguson proposed raising the personal property tax from $3.50 to $4.50 per $100 to reduce a budget shortfall of over $699,000.13 The Board ultimately proposed a $1.10 increase, bringing the personal property rate to $4.60 per $100—the first such increase since 1974.14 (See the section on Personal Property Tax for the full history of that rate.)

An April 2006 article illustrates the practical implications of county-only vs. combined town-and-county taxation during the boundary line adjustment discussions of that year: residents potentially within adjusted town boundaries would have faced combined real estate taxes of 87 cents per $100 (72¢ county + 15¢ town) and combined personal property taxes of $4.05 per $100 ($3.50 county + $0.55 town).15

FY2007-08: Second Increase in Three Years: 72¢ → 77¢

In April 2007, the Board of Supervisors proposed a five-cent increase in the real estate tax rate to balance its upcoming fiscal budget. The proposal, described as the second increase in three years following five years of no increases, would raise the rate from 72 cents to 77 cents per $100.16 The Board adopted a $33.5 million budget on April 25, 2007, incorporating the five-cent increase projected to generate an additional $345,000 in revenue.17

Board Chairman (identified as Thomas H. Conrad at other points in this record) explained the increase as necessary to meet state mandates and maintain county services and quality of life.18 Personal property and merchant's capital rates were held at $4.60 and $1.00 per $100, respectively.

Amid the rate debate, the Land Use ordinance was also a live issue in spring 2007. Commissioner of Revenue Monte Mays and the Virginia Farm Bureau offered competing estimates of the secondary tax impact of adopting a Land Use ordinance: Farm Bureau projected a 4-cent rate increase for non-farmers if farmland was assessed at $400/acre; Mays estimated 2 cents at $700/acre; other analyses suggested the impact could range from 6 to 11 cents.19 The ordinance was ultimately rejected by the Board in 2007 (though later reconsidered and adopted in 2008—see below).

The 2007–08 General Reassessment and Its Aftermath: 77¢ → 44¢

The most consequential event in Appomattox County tax history during the 2004–2024 period was the general reassessment of 2007–08, which dramatically altered both assessed values and the nominal tax rate. The reassessment—conducted by Wingate Appraisal Service, led by Harold Wingate—was the first full general reassessment since approximately 2001–02, reflecting a six-year cycle then standard for the county.

Scope and process: Field work was substantially complete by mid-October 2007, with Wingate reporting to the Board of Supervisors on October 11 that basic field work was finished.20 Reassessment notices were mailed to all county residents by mid-November 2007.21 An appeals deadline of January 10, 2008 was set; approximately 240 citizens had filed appeals by that date, with Wingate Appraisal Service handling the hearings.22

Results and scale of increases: The reassessment produced sweeping increases. Overall, Appomattox County real estate values increased by approximately 78 percent.23 Home values rose roughly 30 percent, while land values more than doubled—with agricultural and rural land seeing particularly dramatic increases. Commissioner Mays had warned as early as October 2005 that real estate tax burdens could triple for some landowners due to the upcoming reassessment, projecting the average cost per acre to rise from roughly $800 (2002 assessment) to approximately $2,432 (2008 reassessment).24 Individual experiences bore this out: one Concord resident, Tom Schmutz, addressed the Board in January 2008 reporting a 346 percent increase in his property reassessment;25 a Stonewall-area landowner wrote to the Times-Virginian in June 2008 describing a 748 percent increase in their assessed value, from $14,900 to $111,400—noting that half their 10 acres was flood-prone.26

Commissioner Mays's criticism of the prior reassessment: Commissioner Monte Mays publicly stated in March 2007 that he believed the 2001 reassessment had been improperly conducted—estimating land values below market rates—drawing comparisons to lawsuits that had arisen in Buckingham County over similarly underestimated values.27 This view was contested. A Wingate official at a 2008 reassessment hearing reportedly acknowledged being unaware of the county's existing soil survey data, which critics argued should have informed the land valuations.28

Equalization and the new rate: Under Virginia law and standard reassessment practice, a general reassessment is not intended to generate additional revenue; rather, the jurisdiction is expected to reduce its nominal rate proportionally so that total tax collections remain roughly equal. The 78 percent average increase in values caused the rate to automatically equalize at approximately 43 cents per $100—down from 77 cents.29 A January 2008 article reported that the Board was expected to set the rate in the "low 40-cent range."30

The Board initially proposed a six-cent increase above equalization (setting the rate at 49 cents) as part of a $37.2 million budget, then later reduced that proposal to five cents (49 cents).31 On April 30, 2008, the Board voted to approve only a one-cent increase above equalization, setting the real estate tax rate at 44 cents per $100, with one supervisor casting a protest vote against the increase.32 A June 2008 article confirmed the rate at 44 cents.33

Impact on farmers and landowners: Despite the nominal rate reduction from 77 cents to 44 cents, the dramatically higher assessed values meant that farmers and rural landowners saw their actual tax bills double or more. Homeowners in many cases saw modest reductions or no change in their bills. One July 2008 analysis summarized: supervisors had lowered the tax rate from $0.77 to $0.44 per $100, but farmers and landowners faced doubled tax bills while homeowners often saw taxes remain stable or decrease.34

Lawsuits: Two civil lawsuits were filed against Appomattox County in July 2008, challenging the reassessment values. Robert E. McClenny Jr. and Joseph Terry filed suit alleging their land values were overestimated. County Attorney J.G. Overstreet filed a petition to dismiss the suits, arguing the plaintiffs had not first exhausted administrative remedies (an appeal to the Board of Equalization), and requested that the appraisal company re-evaluate the disputed parcels.35

Reassessment cycle recommendation: In the context of the dramatic 2008 reassessment results, Harold Wingate recommended in March 2007 that Appomattox County consider adjusting its property reassessment schedule to occur every four years instead of every six, arguing this would prevent the accumulation of large unrealized value increases and reduce the severity of reassessment shocks to taxpayers.36 The Board did not appear to formally adopt this recommendation at that time, though the suggestion reflected broader criticism of the six-year cycle.

FY2009-10: Post-Reassessment Climb: 44¢ → 48¢

With budgetary pressures increasing in the recession year of 2009, the Board of Supervisors voted 3-2 in late April 2009 to advertise a ten-cent increase in the real estate tax rate, from 44 cents to 54 cents per $100.37 Supervisors Sam Carter, Gary Tanner, and Russell Moore voted in favor; Supervisors Tom Conrad and Willie Craft voted against. A public hearing was held on May 11, 2009, at the Appomattox Community Center, drawing six citizen speakers; four urged a lower increase.38 Former Supervisor Dennis Torrence recommended reducing the rate to 50 cents per $100.39

On May 20, 2009, the Board voted unanimously (5-0) to reduce the proposed increase from ten cents to four cents, setting the final rate at 48 cents per $100 as part of a $40.3 million budget.40 A subsequent analysis noted that 39 percent of this 9-percent effective rate increase was attributable to the newly adopted Land Use ordinance (adopted June 2008), which had reduced the tax base by removing qualifying agricultural land from full assessed-value taxation, creating a $218,000 shortfall in real estate tax revenues that other property owners had to absorb.41

Brief Stability: FY2010-11 — 48¢ Maintained

For fiscal year 2010-11, the Board of Supervisors unanimously voted to leave the real estate tax rate unchanged at 48 cents per $100. Chairman Gary Tanner cited fiscal responsibility during challenging economic times. The board managed to maintain services without a tax hike by careful budget management, and also declined to use a tax increase to bolster the county's $400,000 rainy day fund.42 Concurrently, a citizen letter to the Times-Virginian warned that federally funded teacher raises for the 2010-11 school year—funded by stimulus money—could lead to local real estate tax increases in 2012 when the federal funding ran out.43

FY2011-12 and FY2012-13: Successive Increases

FY2011-12 (1.5-cent increase: 48¢ → 49.5¢): In April 2011, the Board voted 3-2 to advertise a 1.5-cent real estate tax increase to provide nearly $400,000 for the schools.44 A public hearing was held May 2, 2011. On May 4, 2011, the Board voted 4-1 to approve the increase, raising the rate to 49.5 cents per $100. Supervisor Sam Carter was the lone dissenter, voting no on the grounds that the increase was insufficient to meet county and school system needs.45 The increase helped finance the school division's budget and was part of a $37.9 million overall budget.46

In early 2012, citizen Dennis Torrence recommended the Board raise the real estate tax further—from 49.5 cents to 57 cents per $100—to provide increased financial assistance to the school division.47

FY2012-13 (8-cent increase: 49.5¢ → 57.5¢): On May 8, 2012, the Board voted 3-2 to increase the real estate tax rate by eight cents, raising it from 49.5 to 57.5 cents per $100. The increase was designed to fund the school division's $4.8 million budget request, cover employee raises, and meet Virginia Retirement System (VRS) contribution increases.48 The contentious 3-2 vote reflected ongoing division on the Board about the appropriate level of local school funding. One letter to the editor published before the vote called for citizens to vote out supervisors who approved the increase.49

Appomattox County supervisors had also been planning to set aside funds for an upcoming reassessment: a March 2012 article notes that supervisors were considering financing half the cost of a reassessment expected in approximately two years (i.e., around 2014).50

FY2013-14 and FY2014-15: Clerical Error and Revenue Shortfall

FY2013-14: The real estate tax rate was maintained at 57.5 cents per $100. Personal property rates also held at $4.60 and merchant's capital at $1.00.51

FY2014-15 (2.5-cent increase: 57.5¢ → 60¢): The budget process for fiscal year 2014-15 was complicated by a convergence of three factors: an $890,000 increase in the school system's budget request (later revised), a $600,000 deficit resulting from a clerical error in revenue projections, and a $300,000 revenue shortfall caused by lower-than-expected values in the most recent reassessment.52 Together, these factors created a gap that the Board initially proposed to address with a six-cent real estate tax increase to 63.5 cents per $100, advertised in April 2014.53

The Board used approximately $1 million from the county's "rainy day" (reserve) fund to limit the rate increase. Even so, a public hearing was held on April 28, 2014, where citizens expressed opposition to the proposed six-cent hike.54 In the final vote, the Board unanimously reduced the increase to 2.5 cents, setting the rate at 60 cents per $100 as part of a $36.7–36.9 million budget that provided $5.4 million in local funds to the school system.55

The same budget year also saw the Town of Appomattox's Town Council enact a cigarette tax (27 cents per pack), which became a new town revenue source and would later allow the town to reduce its own real estate tax rate.56

FY2015-16: The Nine-Cent Debate — Advertised 9¢, Approved 5¢: 60¢ → 65¢

The budget process for fiscal year 2015-16 generated the most public controversy over Appomattox County tax rates in the period covered by this article. The Board faced a $540,000 budget shortfall and mounting pressure from the school division, which sought a $2 million increase in local funding.

In March 2015, the Board voted 3-2 to advertise a nine-cent real estate tax increase, proposing to raise the rate from 60 cents to 69 cents per $100.57 Supervisors Sam Carter, Willie Craft, and Ronnie Spiggle voted to advertise; Supervisors Gary Tanner and Sara Carter dissented. A higher proposal of 18 cents from Supervisor Sara Carter and a 12-cent proposal from Supervisor Sam Carter were both voted down before the 9-cent advertising motion passed.58

The proposed nine-cent increase—a 15 percent hike—provoked strong public opposition. A public hearing on April 22, 2015, drew over one hundred citizens, most opposing the measure.59 Multiple speakers urged the Board to find alternative revenue sources. Letters to the editor appeared in the Times-Virginian arguing the increase was particularly burdensome for elderly residents on fixed incomes, and that a 15-percent tax hike would consume 60 percent of a recent 1.3 percent Social Security cost-of-living adjustment.60 Former Delegate Watkins M. Abbitt was noted as supporting landowner opposition at the meeting.61

One satirical letter accused the Board of a "Wickedly Brilliant" strategy: advertising an extreme range (9 to 14 cents) to make a lower final increase (5 cents) seem reasonable by comparison, with the decision effectively made before the public hearing.62

On May 4, 2015, the Board voted 5-0 to approve a five-cent increase instead of the advertised nine cents, raising the rate to 65 cents per $100—the maximum permitted under Virginia law (the Board may not adopt a rate higher than the advertised maximum, but may adopt a lower one). Under Virginia practice, once a maximum is advertised, the Board retains the authority to reduce but not increase the approved rate. The increase addressed the $540,000 shortfall and set the school budget at $5.4 million and social services at $1.6 million.63

Separately, within weeks of the real estate increase, the Board also proposed and adopted increases to the personal property tax rate and vehicle license fee—see below.

FY2016-17 and Beyond: Extended Stability at 65¢

For fiscal year 2016-17, the Board of Supervisors proposed a $39.4 million budget with no tax increase, leaving the real estate tax rate at 65 cents per $100. The Board was aided in this decision by a debt refinancing plan developed with the help of Davenport & Company, which outgoing Supervisor Gary Tanner had urged the Board to explore in mid-2015 to prevent a projected three-cent increase.64 The Board had briefly tabled the refinancing discussion in August 2015 but ultimately moved forward, enabling a stable tax rate for fiscal year 2016-17.65

The fiscal 2019 (FY2018-19) budget was approved on June 29, 2018, without raising taxes for real estate, mobile homes, personal property, or machinery and tools.66 The rate of 65 cents per $100 appears to have remained in place through at least the early 2020s.

In March 2022, while the real estate rate remained unchanged, the Board addressed an unusual personal property situation triggered by used car value inflation during the COVID-19 pandemic—see the Personal Property Tax section below.

Personal Property Tax — Chronological History

The Appomattox County personal property tax applies to tangible personal property, which in practice primarily means privately owned vehicles (cars, trucks), boats, business equipment, machinery and tools, and similar taxable items. As with real estate, the rate is expressed per $100 of assessed value, though the assessed value for personal property (particularly vehicles) is determined by standard valuation guides (typically the NADA or similar schedule) rather than a local appraisal. Virginia's Personal Property Tax Relief Act (PPTRA) provides partial exemption for many qualifying personal-use vehicles—see below.

Pre-2006: $3.50 per $100

Through fiscal year 2005-06, the Appomattox County personal property tax rate stood at $3.50 per $100 of assessed value. The rate had remained unchanged since at least 1974—a span of over thirty years with no increase.67 The merchant's capital tax (applicable to business inventory) was set at $1.00 per $100.

FY2006-07: First Increase in 32 Years — $3.50 → $4.60

In April 2006, County Administrator Aileen Ferguson recommended raising the personal property tax rate from $3.50 to $4.50 per $100 to help close a budget shortfall exceeding $699,000. The Board ultimately proposed a larger increase of $1.10, bringing the rate to $4.60 per $100.68 A public hearing was held, and the increase—described in contemporaneous reporting as the first proposed personal property tax hike since 1974—was adopted as part of the FY2006-07 budget.69 The $1.10 increase was projected to generate over $700,000 in additional annual revenue. The merchant's capital tax was held at $1.00 per $100.

This rate of $4.60 per $100 remained in effect for nearly a decade, through fiscal year 2014-15.

FY2015-16: Second Major Increase — $4.60 → $5.13

In May 2015, following approval of the five-cent real estate tax increase, the Board of Supervisors proposed an additional 53-cent increase in the personal property tax rate—from $4.60 to $5.13 per $100—to address the remaining $1 million budget shortfall.70 A public hearing was held on June 1, 2015. Five citizens spoke against the proposal, urging alternative revenue sources.71 Citizens labeled the proposed increases "legalized theft" and expressed concern that the personal property tax was particularly burdensome for individuals on fixed incomes who depended on vehicles for basic transportation.72

On June 3–10, 2015, the Board voted 4-1 to approve the increase, setting the personal property tax rate at $5.13 per $100.73 Separately, the Board also increased the vehicle license fee from $25 to $35 per vehicle, and enacted a meals tax—described as taxes paid by those who could afford them, according to Supervisor Sam Carter.74 The Board had noted it reserved the right to lower or eliminate these increases if alternative revenue streams materialized.

FY2023: Vehicle Rate Reduction — $3.35 → $3.00

In March 2022, the Board of Supervisors unanimously approved a reduction in the personal property tax rate specifically on vehicles for fiscal year 2023, from $3.35 per $100 to $3.00 per $100—a reduction of 35 cents.75 This action reflected a nationwide trend: during 2021-22, used car values had surged dramatically due to COVID-19-related supply chain disruptions and microchip shortages, causing vehicle-assessed values (based on standard guides) to increase substantially even for older vehicles. Many Virginia localities reduced their nominal personal property tax rates on vehicles to offset the inflated assessments and avoid windfall tax increases on residents.

Note on rate discrepancy: The $3.35 vehicle rate in 2022 does not directly correspond to either the $4.60 or $5.13 general personal property rate. Virginia law permits localities to apply different rates to different classes of personal property. The county appears to have maintained a lower, separate rate specifically for vehicles—likely reflecting partial PPTRA relief being baked into the effective rate. The $5.13 rate applies to general tangible personal property (machinery, tools, business equipment, etc.), while vehicles were taxed at a separate rate accounting for PPTRA reimbursements from the state.

Merchant's Capital Tax

The merchant's capital tax, applied to business inventory and capital, was maintained at $1.00 per $100 of assessed value consistently through the period covered in this article. It was held unchanged in the FY2005-06 budget proposal,76 the FY2006-07 budget,77 and the FY2007-08 budget.78 No proposals to change this rate were prominently documented in the newspaper record.

Personal Property Tax Relief Act (PPTRA) Implementation

The Virginia Personal Property Tax Relief Act, originally enacted in the late 1990s, provided state reimbursements to localities for a portion of the personal property tax on qualifying vehicles. Under the original framework, the state reimbursed localities at a percentage of tax relief per vehicle. By 2004-05, the state legislature revised the PPTRA framework, replacing the open-ended percentage reimbursement with a capped block-grant formula—meaning localities received a fixed total amount from the state, regardless of the number of qualifying vehicles or the total relief being provided. This change had the practical effect of reducing the per-vehicle reimbursement rate as the pool of qualifying vehicles grew.

In late December 2005, the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors adopted an ordinance to implement the 2004-05 PPTRA changes, effective January 1, 2006.79 The Town of Appomattox Town Council held a public hearing and adopted a similar ordinance on January 5, 2006, following a workshop meeting.80 Pamplin, another jurisdiction in the area, initially postponed its PPTRA decision in February 2006 pending additional state guidance, then acted in subsequent months.81

A January 2006 article warned that Town of Appomattox residents might face higher personal property tax bills under the new capped system, since the state's reimbursement would cover a smaller fraction of total relief as the number of qualifying vehicles increased and the fixed state allocation was spread more thinly.82

The PPTRA has continued to affect annual tax appropriations. A 2019 article reports the Appomattox Town Council approving an amendment to the Appropriations Resolution regarding personal property tax relief for fiscal year 2020, aligning with state code.83 In June 2023, the Appomattox County budget public hearing included adoption of a "Personal Property Tax Relief Program" resolution as a standard annual item.84

Vehicle Decal Elimination and License Fees

In June 2006, Appomattox County proposed eliminating the requirement for vehicle decals—the annual stickers affixed to license plates as proof of local vehicle licensing. A public hearing was scheduled for July 5, 2006. On July 20, 2006, the Board of Supervisors unanimously approved a vehicle license ordinance eliminating vehicle decals effective with the 2007 billing year.85 The $25 annual decal fee was folded into personal property tax bills rather than collected separately. County Treasurer Janet Belter explained that residents would see a prorated charge of $18 on their personal property tax bill in December 2006 (for the partial year), and the full $25 fee added to their December 2007 bill thereafter.86

County Administrator Aileen Ferguson had been planning this change since at least October 2005, when she indicated 2007 as the target year and noted that the treasurer's office would need to reprogram computer systems to incorporate the fee into personal property bills.87

In May–June 2015, as part of the broader tax increase package, the Board proposed and approved an increase in the vehicle license fee from $25 to $35 per vehicle, effective the following year.88

General Reassessment — Processes, Cycles, and Controversies

Virginia law requires localities to conduct general reassessments of real property at regular intervals to bring assessed values to fair market value. Appomattox County historically followed a six-year reassessment cycle. The major reassessments documented in the record are:

  • ~2001-02 reassessment: Referenced retrospectively in 2008 articles. Property values approximately doubled between this assessment and the 2008 assessment. Commissioner Mays publicly questioned whether this reassessment was properly conducted, noting that values may have been set below true market rates. The 2002 average per-acre cost was approximately $800; by 2008 it reached approximately $2,432.
  • 2007-08 reassessment: The most extensively documented. Conducted by Wingate Appraisal Service. Produced a 78 percent average increase in values; effectively halved the nominal tax rate through equalization; triggered public controversy, appeals, and civil litigation. Described in detail in the real estate tax history above.
  • ~2014 reassessment: Noted in 2012 articles as expected within two years. By 2014, lower-than-expected reassessment values contributed to a $300,000 revenue shortfall that helped necessitate the FY2014-15 tax increase.89
  • 2024 reassessment: Costs associated with a reassessment were identified as a budget challenge for fiscal year 2025.90

In reassessment years, Virginia law requires localities to advertise proposed tax rates at least 30 days before the budget public hearing—a longer lead time than in non-reassessment years. This requirement delayed the county's FY2008-09 budget hearing, postponing it from March 31 to late April 2008.91

The Board of Supervisors stated explicitly that reassessments are not intended as revenue-generating operations: the Board expected to automatically calculate the new revenues into a lower rate to equalize collections.92 However, the practical result of the 2008 reassessment—in which land values more than doubled while the rate was reduced from 77 to 44 cents—meant that farmers and rural landowners (who held large quantities of the most-appreciated land) experienced doubled tax bills, while many homeowners saw modest reductions or no change.

Land Use Tax Ordinance (Agricultural Tax Relief)

The Land Use Assessment program, established by the Virginia General Assembly in 1972, allows localities to tax eligible agricultural, horticultural, forest, or open-space land based on its use value rather than fair market value.93 Approximately 75 percent of Virginia counties had adopted Land Use programs by the mid-2000s; Appomattox County had not.

The push to adopt a Land Use ordinance in Appomattox County accelerated as the 2007-08 reassessment approached and landowners grew alarmed at projected increases. Commissioner of Revenue Monte Mays became the principal advocate for adoption. Several public meetings were held in 2007, with county Farm Bureau members, citizens, and supervisors debating competing estimates of the fiscal impact. Estimates of the consequent tax rate increase for non-participating property owners ranged from 2 cents (Commissioner Mays) to 11 cents (newer data presented in 2007-08) per $100 of assessed value, depending on assumptions about per-acre valuation of qualifying farmland.94

Complications arose over timing: an initial legal understanding held that Land Use ordinances could only be adopted in the year before a reassessment. When the Board declined to adopt the ordinance in 2007, it appeared the opportunity had passed. However, County Attorney Overstreet later clarified that the ordinance could in fact be adopted in any year—it was the Board that had been misinformed about the timing requirement. This reversed the situation: in spring 2008, following the reassessment that had increased some land values by 200-300 percent, the Board reconsidered.95

On June 18, 2008, the Board of Supervisors voted 4-1 to approve a Land Use ordinance, granting tax relief to qualifying farmers owning five or more acres that produce at least $1,000 in annual farm revenue.96 In a surprise move at the same meeting, the Board stripped a provision that would have extended the program to owners of 20 or more acres of timberland.97 Critics questioned the fairness of the ordinance's application to "hobby farmers" with high non-farm incomes who might qualify based on minimal agricultural activity.98

The Land Use ordinance was formally implemented beginning in 2009. Its fiscal impact was significant: a 2009 analysis found it created a $218,000 shortfall in real estate tax revenues, accounting for 39 percent of the county's 9-percent effective tax rate increase that year, as non-participating property owners absorbed the reduction in the tax base.99 A 2010 article summarized the program's origins in the reassessment that had caused some property values to rise as much as 350 percent.100 Commissioner of Revenue Sara Henderson—who succeeded Monte Mays—implemented the program formally in 2009 and received credit for establishing it during her first term.101

Elderly and Disabled Tax Relief Program

Virginia Code authorizes localities to provide property tax relief for elderly and permanently disabled homeowners who meet income and net-worth criteria. Appomattox County maintained such a program throughout the period covered in this article. The program allows qualifying seniors (typically age 65 or older) and totally disabled individuals to receive a full or partial exemption from local real estate taxes on their principal residence. Applications are filed annually with the Commissioner of the Revenue's office.

Pre-2008 Qualification Thresholds

Prior to February 2008, the county's elderly and disabled tax exemption ordinance set qualifying thresholds at:

  • Annual income: not to exceed $15,000
  • Net financial worth: not to exceed $90,000

Commissioner of Revenue Sara Henderson noted a significant drop in the number of qualifying senior recipients between 2006 and 2007, attributing this partly to the restrictiveness of the net-worth threshold—some seniors had seen their net worth rise above $90,000 due to the rising real estate values that would be formally recognized in the 2008 reassessment.102

2008 Expansion: Income and Net-Worth Thresholds Raised

On January 7, 2008, Commissioner Henderson proposed updating the senior and disabled tax exemption ordinance to raise both thresholds. The Board scheduled a public hearing for February 4, 2008, at 6:15 p.m. at the County Administrator's Office on Morton Lane.103

Following the public hearing, on February 4, 2008, the Board of Supervisors voted 5-0 to approve the updated ordinance, raising the qualification thresholds to:

  • Annual income: not to exceed $20,000
  • Net financial worth: not to exceed $100,000

The Board praised Commissioner Henderson for initiating the proposal. The update was described as making it easier for a larger percentage of senior and handicapped citizens to qualify—reversing the trend of declining program participation.104

Commissioner of Revenue Monte Mays had earlier highlighted the original elderly/handicapped tax relief program as an accomplishment of his office, noting he had convinced the Board to implement it.105 At the 2018 senior health fair organized by the Good Neighbor Information and Help Center, Mays—now in a different role—spoke as a tax law expert to seniors about understanding tax regulations.106

Application Deadlines

The annual deadline for filing Tax Relief for the Elderly and Totally Disabled applications was May 1. Forms were available from the Commissioner of the Revenue's office. A 2009 reminder published in the Times-Virginian noted the May 1, 2009 deadline.107

2018 Expansion

In January 2018, the Board of Supervisors again considered an expansion of the elderly and handicapped tax relief program. On February 16, 2018 (approximately), the Board voted to ease qualification requirements for tax breaks for senior citizens and handicapped individuals, once again following a recommendation from Commissioner of Revenue Sara Henderson.108 Specific new thresholds were not reported in the available record, but the pattern of periodic updates reflects ongoing attention to ensuring the program reaches a meaningful share of eligible residents.

State Constitutional Context

Several Virginia constitutional amendments related to property tax relief for seniors and disabled individuals appeared on statewide ballots during this period. In November 2010, voters decided on:

  • A constitutional amendment allowing localities to establish income limitations for property tax relief for homeowners aged 65+ or permanently disabled (Question 1); and
  • An amendment requiring the General Assembly to exempt the principal residence of a veteran with a 100 percent service-connected disability, or their surviving spouse, from local taxation (Question 2).109

In November 2016, voters considered amendments concerning the Right to Work law and property tax exemptions for surviving spouses of emergency service personnel.110 In November 2018, Virginia voters approved a ballot measure expanding veteran property tax exemptions by a margin of 84.3 percent, and a measure for property tax relief for flood abatement by 70.7 percent.111 In November 2020, voters considered exempting one motor vehicle owned by a veteran with a 100 percent service-connected disability from state and local property taxes.112

Property Tax Exemptions for Non-Profit and Special Entities

In February 2007, the Appomattox Town Council reviewed a request for a real estate tax exemption for Walter Gum Manor, a proposed low-income elderly apartment complex in the Town, sponsored by the Virginia United Methodist Housing Development Corporation—a non-profit.113 The Town Council was asked to grant a real estate tax exemption for the planned facility under Virginia Code provisions allowing localities to exempt nonprofit housing for elderly residents.

Special Districts and Overlay District Proposals

No special tax district or service district—a distinct geographic area subject to an additional tax levy for specific public services—was adopted in Appomattox County or the Town of Appomattox during the 2004–2024 period covered by this research. However, several related proposals generated significant public discussion.

Scenic Corridor Overlay District (Town of Appomattox, 2006–2007)

Beginning in 2006, the Town of Appomattox considered establishing a Scenic Corridor Overlay District—a zoning overlay applicable to properties along the US 460 corridor through town. The overlay was not a special tax district; it would impose architectural, landscaping, and signage standards on new construction and business renovations rather than an additional tax levy. However, it was considered alongside discussions about the town's identity and growth management.

A public hearing held in October 2006 was heavily attended, with most speakers opposing the ordinance primarily due to the absence of specific, written guidelines—the standards had not yet been defined, meaning businesses would need a conditional use permit without knowing the criteria.114 In November 2006, the Appomattox County Planning Commission voted 6-3 to reject the proposed overlay district ordinance.115 Town Manager David Garrett indicated written guidelines would be available within approximately two months.116

In December 2006, the Town Council deferred a vote and requested more details.117 Mayor John Wilson made the overlay district one of his three stated priorities for 2007 (alongside financial integrity and tourism), expressing support for protecting the town's entrance corridors from unrestricted development.118 The Town Council continued studying the proposal through early 2007, with a scheduled educational meeting set for March 5, 2007, and the planning commission reviewing overlay details in February 2007.119 A citizen opinion piece in the Times-Virginian urged the Town Council to adopt specific zoning controls including the Scenic Corridor Overlay as a tool for managing growth and maintaining the town's historic character.120 The ultimate fate of the overlay ordinance—whether it was adopted or permanently shelved—is not definitively resolved in the available record.

Commercial Corridor Overlay District (US 460, 2007)

A separate but related concept emerged from a regional study of the US 460 Corridor between Appomattox and Concord, submitted to the Board of Supervisors in May 2007 and overseen by the Region 2000 Local Government Council. The study recommended improvements including deceleration turning lanes at major intersections and the implementation of a Commercial Corridor Overlay District.121 This overlay, like the scenic corridor overlay for the town, was a land-use planning tool rather than a special tax levy.

Boundary Line Adjustment and Proposed Tax Exemptions (2012)

In 2012, negotiations between the Town of Appomattox and the County over a proposed boundary line adjustment—which would have extended town limits to encompass commercial development along the US 460 West corridor—included a proposal for permanent real estate tax exemptions for landowners within the newly included area. Under this county proposal, landowners who did not sell their property for profit and did not connect to the town's sewer system would receive a permanent tax exemption, with the Town administering any tax rebate program.122

Supervisor Sam Carter characterized the arrangement as a "win-win" for town and county, promoting economic development.123 However, the Town Council rejected this and three other county counterproposals—including revenue sharing and the creation of a Public Service Authority for utilities. The Town rejected the permanent tax exemption concept in part because it believed such exemptions would not maximize revenue potential compared to a model in which the town had full taxing capability over annexed properties.124 No boundary extension or special tax arrangement was implemented.

Agricultural Districts and Conservation Easements (2008)

During the 2008 debate over the Land Use ordinance, County Attorney Overstreet investigated the possibility of designating specific areas as Agricultural Districts, which would have qualified landowners within those areas for tax relief similar to a Land Use ordinance without requiring the formal ordinance.125 Separately, conservation easements—voluntary agreements limiting development while protecting natural values—were discussed as another mechanism for providing tax relief to agricultural landowners.126 Neither approach was formally adopted in lieu of the Land Use ordinance, which was ultimately enacted in June 2008.

Town of Appomattox Tax Rates (Separate Jurisdiction)

The Town of Appomattox is an incorporated municipality. Residents living within town limits pay both county taxes and town taxes. The town's rates are significantly lower than the county's, reflecting the smaller scale of town government. These rates should not be confused with the county-only rates that are the primary subject of this article.

The Town of Appomattox levies its own real estate tax, personal property tax, and (beginning in 2014) a cigarette tax. Town budgets are adopted by the Town Council rather than the Board of Supervisors.

Rate changes and dynamics: In July 2006, the Appomattox Town Council voted to maintain the town's real-estate tax rate at 15 cents and personal property at 55 cents per $100.134 In May 2007, the council again proposed maintaining both rates.135 The town's real estate rate dropped to 13 cents (approximately) around the FY2008-09 budget, apparently following a town-level reassessment process that increased assessed values and allowed a rate reduction. Billing Cycles and Collections

Appomattox County real estate and personal property taxes are billed annually, with personal property tax bills historically due in December. The vehicle decal elimination in 2007 consolidated the vehicle license fee into the personal property tax bill (see above). No change to the overall annual billing cycle for real estate or personal property was documented in the materials reviewed.

In November 2011, the Town of Appomattox adopted a new tax billing policy covering collection procedures and late-payment penalties for most tax types, effective November 15, 2011. The water and sewer billing portion of the policy, including penalties for late payments, was delayed until March 1, 2012.139

The county library system periodically used state income tax intercept mechanisms ("debt offset") to recover overdue fines, warning patrons that unresolved 90-day overdue notices could result in offsets against state tax returns and lottery winnings.140

Recent History (2016–2024)

The period from 2016 through 2024 was marked by relative real estate tax rate stability at 65 cents per $100, punctuated by the vehicle personal property tax relief in FY2023 and ongoing budget pressures from healthcare, VRS contributions, and state mandates.

In fiscal year 2016-17, the Board approved a $39.4 million budget without any tax rate increases, aided by debt refinancing.141 The fiscal year 2018-19 budget of $40,513,471 was similarly adopted without raising any local tax rates.142

Outgoing Supervisor Gary Tanner, who did not seek re-election in 2015, cited opposition to unfunded state and federal mandates as his primary concern, arguing they forced tax increases on local taxpayers. He expressed a desire to be remembered as a voice for citizens—especially elderly residents on fixed incomes.143

In March 2022, the Board unanimously reduced the personal property tax rate on vehicles from $3.35 to $3.00 per $100, effective for fiscal year 2023 billing—an action taken to offset the surge in vehicle assessed values caused by used-car market inflation during the COVID-19 pandemic.144 This was the most significant local tax rate action of the 2020-2024 period captured in the available record.

For fiscal year 2025 (FY2024-25), the county identified several funding challenges including a 10 percent increase in county employee health insurance costs, a 3 percent cost-of-living adjustment for employees, real estate reassessment expenses, and inflationary increases in supplies and services.145 The upcoming reassessment cycle and associated costs were a recurring concern as the county approached what would likely be its next general reassessment.

The Virginia state-level tax landscape also evolved during this period. In 2022, the General Assembly (under Governor Glenn Youngkin) enacted a significant tax relief package totaling nearly $4 billion statewide, including an increased standard deduction, a refundable earned income tax credit, the repeal of the state grocery tax, and suspension of gas tax increases.146 While these measures did not directly change local real estate or personal property tax rates, they represented substantial state-level tax changes affecting Appomattox County residents and businesses.
