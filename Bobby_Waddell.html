    Bobby Waddell is an American educator and public servant who represented Appomattox County on the School Board for approximately 15 years, most prominently serving as board chairman for eight years. Honored as the Virginia School Boards Association (VSBA) Regional Member of the Year in 2024, Waddell became known for his unwavering commitment to student achievement, teacher compensation, and bridging communication gaps between educational and governmental leadership.

    Early Life and Career

    Bobby Waddell was born around 1952 and spent much of his early career in education and agricultural extension work. He earned credentials as an agricultural teacher and served in multiple leadership capacities within Appomattox County Schools. His professional positions included agricultural teacher, Administrator in Career and Technology Education, and agricultural extension agent for Appomattox County.[1] Waddell is married to Sue Waddell and has three sons—Jeremy, Christopher, and Nicholas—all of whom are Appomattox County High School graduates.[1]

    In 2002, after 22 years with the Appomattox County School system, Waddell resigned as Director of Career and Technology Education to pursue a similar career at Prince Edward County Public Schools, where he worked as Director of Technology and Assessment.[2]

    Appointment to School Board

    In June 2005, the Appomattox County School Board appointed Bobby Waddell to an interim position representing the Falling River District, filling the vacancy left by Marcie Jones, who had departed to serve as headmistress at Appomattox Christian Academy.[1] At age 53, Waddell expressed his commitment to bringing decades of teaching, administrative, and community experience to the board. "Having been a parent and a teacher and an administrator, I feel like I've seen all sides of the job," he stated at the time.[1]

    Waddell indicated his intention to run in the special election scheduled for November 2005 to secure the seat permanently.[3]

    School Board Service

    Early Tenure (2005–2008)

    On November 9, 2005, Bobby Waddell won the special election for the Falling River District seat, defeating Veronica F. Reed decisively with 584 votes to Reed's 308.[4] Just two months into his tenure, the School Board unanimously elected Waddell as its new Chairman in January 2006, succeeding Amy Martin and making him one of the newest members to assume the chairmanship.[5] This appointment reflected his peers' immediate confidence in his leadership abilities.

    During his early years as chairman, Waddell advocated forcefully for teacher compensation. At a November 2005 board meeting, he highlighted a critical problem: the salary difference between a beginning teacher and one with 20 years of service in Appomattox was only $5,300—far below what he considered competitive with neighboring localities.[6] He took the lead in requesting increased school funding during county budget hearings, working alongside Superintendent Walter Krug to argue for full state-matching funds allocation.[7]

    Waddell also demonstrated pragmatic administrative stewardship. When construction and boiler replacement issues arose at school facilities, he engaged meaningfully with repair and capital improvement planning. In June 2006, he was authorized by the board to execute an agreement with U.S. Cellular for property access for a cell tower project involving FFA Alumni Association property, ensuring proper restoration terms.[8]

    Re-elected to the chairmanship annually through 2008, Waddell was succeeded as chairman by Rev. James "Wes" Williams in January 2008 after serving two consecutive years in the role.[9] He remained an active board member and advocate for the school system's needs.

    Mid-Tenure Leadership (2008–2012)

    Throughout 2008–2011, Waddell continued to shape school policy from his board seat. He championed athletic facility upgrades, working with Superintendent Dr. Aldridge Boone and fellow board members to address deteriorating baseball and football fields that had become safety hazards.[10] He served on the Appomattox Education Foundation Scholarship Committee, reflecting his commitment to expanding educational opportunities.[11]

    In April 2007, Waddell announced his candidacy for a second full term on the School Board, citing pending projects such as Primary School remodeling and his ongoing goals around board-supervisor relations, teacher compensation, and community engagement. He was re-elected in November 2007.[12]

    Waddell again served as vice-chairman from 2011–2012, and was renamed chairman in January 2012 for a third term in the role.[13] His leadership during the fiscal crisis of the early 2010s proved crucial; as schools faced budget cuts, he publicly acknowledged the difficulty of trimming expenditures when approximately 80 percent of the school budget was allocated to salaries—a reality that constrained flexibility.[14]

    Extended Chairmanship (2012–2019)

    Waddell's third term as chairman, beginning in 2012, coincided with renewed infrastructure projects. In October 2012, he wrote formally to the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors detailing serious traffic safety issues at Appomattox Elementary School, where parental pickup congestion created hazardous conditions on Lee-Grant Avenue and Pumping Station Road. His advocacy prompted the county supervisors to request a Virginia Department of Transportation (VDOT) traffic study, completed at no cost to the county.[15]

    The board re-appointed Waddell as chairman with near-unanimous votes in subsequent years. In January 2015, the board voted 5-0 to re-appoint him for his fourth consecutive year.[16] He was also appointed to the Appomattox County High School Master Plan Committee, positioning him to guide major facility renovation initiatives.[16]

    When seeking his third full term on the board in March 2015, Waddell articulated his vision for continued educational excellence. He emphasized the school system's critical role in economic development, noting that prospective businesses evaluate school quality and community support when considering relocation.[17] He aimed to complete high school renovations and middle school upgrades while ensuring teachers received adequate resources.[17] Running uncontested in the Falling River District in 2015, he received 401 votes.[18]

    Waddell was reappointed chairman for his fifth consecutive year in January 2016 via a 5-0 vote in an interim meeting.[19] He addressed new hires at a School Board convocation in August 2016, expressing appreciation alongside Superintendent Dorinda Grasty.[20] In July 2018, he made headlines when discussing the new superintendent's compensation, asserting that Annette A. Bennett—hired at a starting salary of $120,000—was underpaid considering her qualifications, given that her predecessor had earned $140,106.[21]

    In 2019, Waddell was re-elected chairman of the School Board for 2019 and won his next full term in November 2019 with 98.77 percent of the vote (running essentially unopposed).[22]

    Transition and Return (2020–2021)

    After 15 years of service and 8 years as chairman, Bobby Waddell resigned from the Appomattox County School Board on November 20, 2020, due to moving out of the Falling River District he represented.[23] His departure marked the end of a transformative tenure spanning two decades of educational leadership.

    However, less than six months later, Waddell returned to public service. In May 2021, he was selected as interim representative for the Piney Mountain District on the Appomattox County School Board, having relocated to that district. He served in that interim capacity and indicated his intention to run for election in November 2021.[24]

    VSBA Regional Member of the Year

    In November 2024, Bobby Waddell received the Virginia School Boards Association (VSBA) Regional Member of the Year award, one of the organization's most prestigious honors. The award was presented during the opening session of the VSBA Annual Convention in Williamsburg, Virginia, and was generously sponsored by Southland Industries.[25]

    The award recognized Waddell's exceptional commitment over more than 18 years of service to the Appomattox County School Board, with particular emphasis on his 14 years as chairman. According to the award application, "His motives have always been clear: to provide the highest quality educational program in Appomattox by supporting its students and staff."[25] The recognition highlighted Waddell's mission-driven approach to board governance and his sustained dedication to advancing student achievement.

    Waddell was honored alongside two other distinguished school board members: Jacinta Greene of Alexandria City Public Schools (a six-year veteran) and Champ Hardie of Henry County Public Schools (a two-year veteran), underscoring the breadth of effective leadership across Virginia's educational system.

    Community Involvement

    Beyond his School Board service, Bobby Waddell maintained deep community roots through multiple civic roles. In November 2010, he was recognized alongside Wyatt Torrence as a local citizen of the year for his broad community contributions.[26]

    His community involvement included:

    • Appomattox Habitat for Humanity: Served as president of the Appomattox Habitat for Humanity board, and was recognized in 2004 for dedication to the organization's mission of affordable housing.[27]
    • Fire Service: Served as a volunteer firefighter with the Appomattox County Volunteer Fire Department and previously served as president of the organization.[28]
    • Agricultural Organizations: Maintained active involvement with the FFA (Future Farmers of America) Alumni Association, drawing on his deep agricultural background.[28]
    • Faith Community: Served in leadership roles at Memorial United Methodist Church.[28]
    • Retired Teachers: Remained a member of the Appomattox County Retired Teachers Association, supporting education policy advocacy for retirees.[28]

    Notably, Waddell had supervised agricultural education instruction, including mentoring future educators in their student teaching placements. In 2019, he recalled supervising Dr. B. Allen Talbert during his student teaching in 1983 when Waddell taught agriculture classes.[29]
