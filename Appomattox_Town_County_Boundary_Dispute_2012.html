    The Appomattox Town–County Boundary Line Dispute was a protracted inter-governmental conflict between the Town of Appomattox and Appomattox County, Virginia over whether the Town should be permitted to annex land along the U.S. 460 West corridor in exchange for extending public sewer service. The dispute first surfaced in 2006 when Town Council explored expanding its corporate limits to protect its water wells from a proposed subdivision, resurfaced during the contentious U.S. 460 waterline negotiations of 2008–2009, and reached its climax in the summer and fall of 2012 when the Town formally proposed extending its boundaries to Police Tower Road (State Route 613) and constructing a $2.3 million sewer line at no cost to the County. The Board of Supervisors rejected the proposal on three separate votes—all by 3–2 margins—before the Town withdrew its offer in late October 2012. A final attempt by Supervisor Sam Carter to reopen negotiations failed on December 3, 2012, effectively ending the dispute.[1][2]

    The boundary line question exposed a fundamental philosophical divide between the two governing bodies: the Town viewed annexation as essential for controlling the development corridor and generating the tax revenue needed to fund infrastructure, while a County majority viewed it as an unjust imposition of double taxation on unwilling residents. The dispute delayed sewer development along U.S. 460 for years and complicated the Town's plans to close its aging trickling filter wastewater treatment plant.[3]

    Background

    The 1987 precedent

    The idea of expanding the Town of Appomattox's corporate limits had historical precedent. In December 1987, the Appomattox Town Council unanimously voted to enter negotiations with Appomattox County to expand the town's boundary lines, though that effort did not result in a formal adjustment.[4]

    2006: First boundary proposal

    The modern boundary dispute began in April 2006, when Town Council explored a boundary line adjustment that could extend the town limits approximately one mile down State Route 727 (and potentially into other areas) to serve a proposed residential subdivision by developer Fred Jones. The subdivision, which could include up to 100 homes, raised concerns that numerous new private wells would threaten the town's existing water supply. A boundary line adjustment was seen as a mechanism to bring the subdivision under the town's water and sewer jurisdiction and protect the water table.[5]

    Town Attorney David Hawkins advised Council that if the County and Town agreed to a boundary line adjustment, courts would automatically approve it without a voter referendum, overriding any citizen protests.[5] County Administrator Aileen Ferguson noted that approximately 32 property owners would be affected and would face combined town and county taxes.[6]

    All five County supervisors expressed strong reservations. Courthouse District Supervisor Sammy Carter stated he could not support the measure without first hearing from affected residents. Supervisor Russell Moore said he saw no immediate benefits for county citizens. Supervisor Willie Craft deemed the proposal "unrealistic." Mayor Ronnie Spiggle (who was also serving as mayor at that time) doubted a six-month timeline was feasible. The proposal was shelved, with Council agreeing to continue the matter in committee.[7][8]

    Boundary demands during the waterline negotiations (2008–2009)

    The boundary question resurfaced in 2008 as a central sticking point in the protracted U.S. 460 waterline negotiations between the Town and County. In April 2008, Town Council included a "friendly boundary line adjustment" in its waterline counteroffer, proposing to extend town limits on State Route 24 to the Surrender Grounds and westward to encompass areas with future commercial water demand.[9] The County tabled the counteroffer and eventually proposed an alternative in which the County would own the waterline and sell water to the Town at a master meter on the town boundary.[10]

    Throughout 2009, the Town Council insisted that any waterline agreement must include a boundary line adjustment. In October 2009, Council unanimously passed a resolution stating that boundary line adjustments were "the sole remaining negotiation point" for the waterline project.[11] Mayor Paul Harvey convened a special meeting on October 21 to discuss the specifics, at which Town Manager Bart Van Nieuwenhuise explained that a "friendly" adjustment would cost roughly $15,000–$20,000 (surveys and legal fees), while an "unfriendly" one could cost $100,000.[12] The County, however, was unwilling to agree to a boundary adjustment along the U.S. 460 corridor, calling further discussion "pointless" if the Town insisted on annexation as a condition.[13]

    The waterline project ultimately proceeded without a boundary adjustment. The County financed and built the $5.6 million Concord-to-Appomattox waterline on its own, with the line becoming operational in early 2012.[14]

    2010–2011: Museum of the Confederacy adjustment

    Despite the failure of the broader 460-corridor annexation, the two sides cooperated on a smaller boundary adjustment in 2010–2011. The Board of Supervisors agreed to allow the Town to annex 23.2 acres along State Route 24, incorporating 13 parcels off Old Courthouse Road and Horseshoe Road where the Museum of the Confederacy (now the American Civil War Museum) was being built.[15] This was a voluntary annexation that received strong support from affected property owners. The Town Council approved the adjustment in January 2011 after a public hearing at which no one spoke in opposition.[15]

    The Museum adjustment would later become a rhetorical touchstone in the 2012 dispute: supporters of the Town's sewer proposal cited it as proof the two governments could cooperate, while opponents noted it had succeeded precisely because affected citizens supported it—unlike the 460-corridor plan.[16]

    The 2012 dispute

    The Town's proposal (June 2012)

    With the U.S. 460 waterline now operational but no sewer service available along the corridor, the Town of Appomattox returned to the boundary issue. In a letter to the Board of Supervisors, Mayor Paul Harvey detailed the Town's new proposal: in exchange for a boundary line adjustment extending the town limits through the industrial park and westward to Police Tower Road (State Route 613), the Town would construct a sewer line along U.S. 460 at an estimated cost of $2.3 million, funded entirely from the Town's budget. The Town would own and maintain the sewer line, handling billing, connections, field repairs, clogged lines, and customer complaints.[17]

    Harvey argued the proposal was a "win-win" that would replace the Town's outdated trickling filter wastewater treatment plant, stimulate growth, increase tax revenue for both localities, relieve tax burdens, and bring business to the U.S. 460 corridor.[18] Interim Town Manager Bill Gillespie stressed that while the waterline was in place, the corridor could not develop without public sewer service.[19]

    At the Board of Supervisors' June 18 meeting, the five supervisors split along lines that would persist throughout the dispute. Supervisors Sam Carter and Ronnie Spiggle expressed support; supervisors Willie Craft, Gary Tanner, and Jerry Small expressed reservations. Tanner reported that he had visited "two-to-three dozen" homeowners and businesses in the proposed area and that nearly all opposed annexation. Craft stated, "I have trouble with moving people around, and I do not want to ram it down [their throats]." The Board agreed only to seek public comment on the matter.[17]

    Public hearing and community reaction (August 2012)

    On August 15, Gillespie presented the Town's case at a regular Town Council meeting, calling it a "dry run" for the upcoming public forum. He illustrated with maps where the sewer line would run and explained that the town would assume all costs, including increasing the capacity of its sequential batch reactor (SBR) wastewater treatment plant.[19]

    The public hearing was held on August 23 at the Board of Supervisors meeting room at the CVCC Appomattox Center. Approximately 40 people attended, including realtors and local business owners. Gillespie presented a PowerPoint detailing the proposal and noting that Reed Engineering Company Inc. had conducted a study on the development impact of the proposed area.[20][21] Town officials argued that if the boundary adjustment were denied, the County could face a $2.75 million cost for expanding its SBR plant, or as much as $9.5 million for sewer expansion and replacement of the trickling filter plant combined.[22]

    First rejection and feasibility study (September 2012)

    On September 4, the Board of Supervisors voted 3–2 to table the Town's proposal. In lieu of a vote on the boundary adjustment, the three-member majority (Small, Craft, and Tanner) authorized a feasibility study to examine alternative options. Carter and Spiggle voted against the study, with Carter questioning whether the County could afford it given only $50,000 in operating reserves.[23]

    Supervisor Tanner offered what became one of the dispute's most-quoted statements: "I am not now, nor will I ever be, for throwing that group of people under the bus. We did a friendly boundary line adjustment for the Town at the Museum, but there is nothing friendly about the 460 boundary line adjustment."[23] He reported that 96 percent of the affected residents opposed the plan.[24]

    Supervisor Craft cited the Town of Pamplin as a counter-example, noting that Pamplin had recently extended its sewer lines outside its town limits and into the county without requesting a boundary line extension.[25]

    Town's October deadline

    The feasibility study apparently struck a wrong chord with Town Council. In a letter dated September 13, Mayor Harvey informed the Board of Supervisors that the Town was giving the County until October 19 to respond—accept or reject—or the Town would withdraw its proposal entirely. Harvey wrote that the Town had "lost potential funding opportunities for other projects in its utility master plan" while waiting for the County to respond, and that the delay had "in effect lost a year in those project completion timelines."[26]

    At the September 17 Board meeting, Supervisor Carter questioned the wisdom of spending money on a feasibility study: "It scares me that we only have $50,000 in operating reserve and now we might have to pay for a study." Craft responded that the Town's proposal "was supposed to be a friendly boundary line adjustment, but there's not anything friendly about it." Supervisor Small interjected when Carter called the proposal a "golden opportunity," saying, "I question the 'golden opportunity.' It's not a golden opportunity for the people who will be double-taxed."[27]

    Second rejection (October 1, 2012)

    At the October 1 meeting, three citizens spoke during the public comment period urging supervisors to accept the Town's proposal. Former Supervisor Dennis Torrence said, "We operate as two governments but one group of people. This is the last piece of the puzzle." Citizen Chad Millner emphasized the proposal would cost the County nothing and urged both sides to negotiate. Local attorney Tommy Lawson told the Board, "This is probably the most important decision for Appomattox County. Are we gonna grow? Are we gonna get a tax base? Or are we gonna stand still?"[28]

    Despite the citizen appeals, the Board voted 3–2 to reject the Town's proposal. Small, Craft, and Tanner voted against it; Carter and Spiggle voted in favor. The Board stated it would prepare alternative counteroffers for its next meeting.[25]

    County's four counteroffers (October 15, 2012)

    On October 15, Board Chairman Jerry Small sent a letter to Mayor Harvey outlining four alternative proposals, which were approved on a 4–1 vote (Carter dissenting):[29]

    1. Return to the original MOU. The Town and County would jointly pursue a sewer line extension under a Memorandum of Understanding, as had been discussed earlier in 2012.

    2. Revenue-sharing agreement. The two localities would establish a revenue-sharing arrangement similar to the one between Bedford County and the City of Bedford.

    3. Boundary extension with tax exemptions. The Town could extend its limits to Police Tower Road, but affected landowners would receive a permanent, 100-percent tax exemption from town taxes. The exemption would be "permanent for all residents and business owners unless their property is sold for profit or if they choose to connect to the sewer system." The industrial park and Community Park would be excluded. Landowners would not be forced to connect to sewer service.

    4. Public Service Authority. A PSA would be established to govern all utilities in Appomattox County.

    Supervisor Carter, the lone dissenter, believed a boundary line adjustment with standard taxation was the better approach.[29]

    Town rejects all counteroffers (October 23, 2012)

    At its October 23 meeting, Town Council rejected all four counteroffers. Interim Town Manager Gillespie estimated the Town would lose $800,000 to $1 million in tax revenue under the tax-exemption counteroffer, making the sewer extension financially unfeasible. In a letter dated October 24, Mayor Harvey outlined the Council's reasons for each rejection:[30]

    The MOU was rejected because the Council "does not believe it is worth doing since it does not include a boundary line adjustment." Revenue sharing was rejected because it would not maximize revenue potential compared to the Bedford example, where the city had greater taxing capabilities. The tax-exempt boundary extension was rejected because the lost revenue would leave the Town unable to finance the sewer line construction. The PSA was rejected as impractical given the existing distribution of utility responsibilities.[30]

    Council also formally withdrew its own proposal from the table.[30]

    Carter's last-ditch motion and final vote (December 3, 2012)

    On November 5, Supervisor Carter announced at the Board meeting that he would introduce a new motion at the December meeting to restart negotiations. "At our December meeting, I'm going to bring it back up to vote on it again," he said. Carter's proposed motion would authorize County Administrator Ferguson to send a letter to the Town agreeing to a boundary line extension with standard taxation (no exemptions), with the Town installing the sewer line and assuming all costs.[31]

    On December 3, Carter formally introduced his motion, which Spiggle seconded. With little to no discussion, the Board voted it down 3–2 on the same divide: Small, Craft, and Tanner against; Carter and Spiggle in favor. The Times-Virginian reported that the vote "probably effectively ends any talks between the County and Town concerning the extension of boundary lines and sewer lines."[1]

    Arguments and positions

    Town's position

    The Town of Appomattox advanced several arguments in support of the boundary adjustment. First, the recently completed U.S. 460 waterline could not spur development without accompanying sewer service: "It would not develop as you would want it without public sewer," Gillespie stated.[19] Second, the Town was willing to assume all costs of construction, maintenance, and operation of the sewer line—estimated at $2.3 million immediately and $12.2 million over the long term—with no cost to the County.[20] Third, town officials argued that the boundary adjustment would increase property values for owners in the affected area and attract commercial development to a corridor that had historically been underutilized.[22]

    Town officials also framed the decision in terms of the costs of inaction. If the boundary adjustment were denied, they warned, the County could face $9.5 million in sewer-related costs: $2.7 million for SBR plant expansion, $2.3 million for the sewer line itself, and $4.4 million in additional infrastructure costs.[22]

    County opposition

    The three-supervisor majority (Small, Craft, and Tanner) rested their opposition on a central principle: they would not force unwilling county residents and businesses into the Town's jurisdiction. Tanner's door-to-door survey indicated 96 percent of affected property owners opposed the plan.[24] Craft frequently invoked a quote he attributed to a Town Council member: "Sometimes you have to throw some people under the bus." He responded, "I am not ready to do this."[23]

    All five supervisors supported extending sewer lines westward in principle; the disagreement was purely over whether a boundary adjustment was the necessary mechanism. Craft pointed to the Town of Pamplin, which had extended its sewer lines into the county without requiring annexation.[25] The opposing supervisors also noted that residents brought into the Town would face double taxation—paying both county and town real estate and personal property taxes—creating a financial burden on citizens who had not consented to it.[17]

    Public and editorial opinion

    The dispute generated a significant amount of public commentary. Letters to the Times-Virginian appeared on both sides throughout September and October 2012. Supporters of the Town's proposal—including former Supervisor Dennis Torrence, attorney Tommy Lawson, and citizen Chad Millner—argued that sewer access was essential for economic development and that blocking the proposal was shortsighted.[28] One letter writer urged citizens to attend the October 1 meeting, stating that the Town had already spent $40,000 on an engineering study and that the supervisors' decision to commission yet another study was "a potential delaying tactic."[32]

    Critics of Supervisors Tanner, Craft, and Small accused them of being "detrimental to the county's economic future."[33] On the other side, Appomattox resident Bill Burke, while supporting the sewer line, argued forcefully against the boundary expansion: "We need the sewer line. But we don't need to enlarge the Town of Appomattox boundary lines to do it." Burke was "totally against any business or residence being exempt from paying taxes if they are in Town," dismissing the County's tax-exemption counteroffer as illogical.[34]

    Aftermath

    Impact on the wastewater treatment plant

    The collapse of negotiations had immediate consequences for the Town's wastewater treatment infrastructure. The Town had been delaying a decision on closing its aging trickling filter plant pending the outcome of the boundary dispute. In October 2012, the USDA sent a letter asking when the Town would submit a funding application for the closure project. The Council was forced to proceed with closing the trickling filter plant and upgrading its SBR plant without the benefit of the new sewer customers that a boundary extension would have brought.[3]

    2013–2014: Hardee's boundary adjustment

    Ironically, a much smaller boundary adjustment succeeded just months after the larger dispute collapsed. In May 2013, Clayton C. Bryant Land Company requested a boundary extension to connect properties along U.S. 460 to the Town's water and sewer lines for a Hardee's restaurant development. The Town Council approved the voluntary adjustment in November 2013, and the Board of Supervisors agreed in October 2013. In July 2014, the Board approved a 0.07-acre boundary line adjustment to ease access for the Hardee's site, which opened in September 2014.[35][36]

    Mayor Harvey expressed satisfaction that the small adjustment would "spur economic development along U.S. 460 and facilitate negotiations between the Town and County for future boundary line adjustments."[36] The Hardee's episode demonstrated that boundary adjustments remained possible when they were narrowly scoped and had the support of the affected property owners—the very dynamic that the broader 2012 proposal had lacked.
