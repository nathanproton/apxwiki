    John Lennie Wilson (September 20, 1931 – September 13, 2016) was an American military officer, airline captain, historic preservationist, and local politician who served as Mayor of the Town of Appomattox, Virginia, from 2006 to 2008. A polarizing figure in Appomattox County public life, Wilson was first elected to the Appomattox Town Council in 2004 and won the mayoral seat in 2006, becoming the town's first new mayor in 28 years. He ran for elected office five times between 2004 and 2010, winning only once—his 2006 mayoral victory—but left an outsized mark on local politics through his vigorous advocacy for citizen participation, opposition to a regional waterline, and founding of the Appomattox Citizens Coalition.[1][2][3]

    Early life and career

    John Lennie Wilson was born on September 20, 1931, in Randolph County, North Carolina, a son of Weldon Dock Wilson and Queen Victoria Hinshaw Wilson-Lambe.[4] He served as a commissioned officer in the United States Army during the Korean War.[4] After his military service, Wilson pursued a career in commercial aviation and retired as a captain with United Airlines.[4]

    The Nebraska House

    Wilson and his wife Patsy purchased a historic home at 107 North Church Street in downtown Appomattox in 1993. The house, known as the "Nebraska House," is named for the original name of the community—Nebraska—which the settlement bore from 1855 until it was renamed West Appomattox in 1895.[5] The house was originally a two-room structure expanded into a six-room, two-story home in 1854 by Colonel Samuel McDearmon, a Virginia Militia colonel who served as the first postmaster of Nebraska and owned the Clover Hill Tavern, now part of the Appomattox Court House National Historical Park. The house was later remodeled in the Queen Anne style by John Atwood between 1890 and 1892.[5]

    The Wilsons undertook an extensive restoration of the property over more than nine years. On April 10, 2004, they opened the home for a public tour to benefit the Appomattox Historical Society, drawing more than 220 visitors who paid ten dollars each. The event featured Civil War reenactors, including a descendant of George Armstrong Custer, and refreshments on the lawn.[5][6] The restoration was widely praised; W. Eston Harvey called the result "a jewel" and noted that the Wilsons' work had added to the charm of the town.[7] In 2004, one of the white dogwood trees bequeathed to the town by the late Mildred Tucker McDearmon was planted in the yard of the Nebraska House.[8]

    Early civic involvement

    Before running for office, Wilson was active in local civic affairs. He served as a member of the Appomattox County Planning Commission from at least 2002, appearing in joint public hearings with the Board of Supervisors and Town Council throughout 2002 and 2003.[9] In November 2002, Wilson appeared before the Board of Supervisors to raise concerns about the county's cable television franchise agreement, presenting research showing that cable rates had increased substantially while service had not improved. Board Chairman Samuel E. Carter acknowledged that Wilson had done "an excellent job of looking into this matter."[10]

    Wilson was also recognized for his role in preserving the old Appomattox County Courthouse. At a 2003 Veterans Day ceremony, he and Frank Carwile were honored for persuading the Board of Supervisors to preserve the main frame of the historic 1892 building rather than demolish it during the construction of the new courthouse.[11]

    Appomattox Town Council (2004–2006)

    In March 2004, Wilson filed his candidacy for the Appomattox Town Council, the sole non-incumbent in the race.[12] He received endorsements from citizens praising his intelligence, ethics, and commitment to seeking constituent input.[13] In the May 4, 2004, election, Wilson won a council seat with 98 votes, replacing William H. "Buddy" Carson, who had failed to file for re-election and mounted an unsuccessful write-in campaign that netted approximately 60 votes.[14] Incumbents Joyce Bennett (120), Steven Lawson (121), Lewis McDearmon (121), Marvin Mitchell (113), and Jimmy Mayberry also won seats.[14]

    Upon his election, Wilson stated his intention to be a voice for citizen interests. He quickly became one of the most active and outspoken members of the council, chairing the Ordinance Committee and pushing for initiatives including the recodification of the Town Code, televised council meetings, and improved citizen access to government information.[15][16]

    Opposition to the U.S. 460 waterline

    Wilson's defining issue on the council—and throughout his political career—was his opposition to proposals to pipe water from Lynchburg or Campbell County to the Town of Appomattox via U.S. Route 460. He argued that the town and county should instead develop local water sources, including drilling additional wells and building a reservoir, which he maintained would be less expensive, preserve local control, and create local jobs.[17] Wilson contended that the pipeline proposal had been advanced without adequate public input and that local officials and Region 2000 were pushing a regional solution the majority of citizens opposed.[18]

    His position placed him in frequent conflict with Board of Supervisors Chairman Sammy Carter, Mayor Ronnie Spiggle, and a majority of his fellow council members, who supported exploring the waterline as a long-term solution to the town's water supply challenges. Wilson wrote extensively on the subject in letters to the Times-Virginian, at times publishing weekly letters questioning the cost estimates, engineering assumptions, and decision-making process behind the waterline proposals.[19][20]

    Appomattox Citizens Coalition

    In February 2005, Wilson organized the first meeting of the Appomattox Citizens Coalition at the Appomattox Community Center. Approximately 80 to 100 people attended to discuss their concerns about the proposed waterline, recent restrictions on citizen comments at Board of Supervisors meetings, and the Board's lease offer for the Carver-Price Legacy Museum. Board Chairman Carter also attended and spoke.[21]

    The Coalition became an ongoing advocacy group, holding periodic public meetings and circulating petitions opposing the waterline and the proposed Public Service Authority. Wilson described its purpose as providing a forum for open discussion where citizens could express views without the restrictions imposed by official government meetings.[22] The group continued to meet during Wilson's tenure as mayor and beyond, holding meetings as late as July 2006 that drew more than 30 attendees.[23]

    Transparency and citizen access

    Wilson championed government transparency throughout his council tenure. In 2004, he proposed a resolution requiring both the Town Council and the Board of Supervisors to place copies of their agenda packets in the J. Robert Jamerson Memorial Library at least one week before meetings, so citizens could review upcoming business in advance. The Town Council adopted a version of the proposal, but the Board of Supervisors rejected it.[24][25] Wilson also played a role in getting town council meetings recorded and broadcast on Nesbe Cable Channel 33, beginning in July 2004.[16]

    Board of Supervisors race (2005)

    While still serving on Town Council, Wilson challenged incumbent Courthouse District Supervisor Samuel E. Carter in the November 2005 general election. Wilson ran on a platform of increased citizen input, opposition to the waterline, support for tourism, and criticism of what he characterized as the Board's unresponsive governance.[26] The race drew competing endorsement letters in the Times-Virginian, with supporters praising Wilson as a champion of the people and critics questioning his temperament and citing a property-line dispute with a neighbor.[27][28]

    Carter won decisively, receiving 510 votes to Wilson's 290, with three write-in votes—a margin of approximately 64 to 36 percent.[29] Wilson accepted the result gracefully, stating that he had given voters a choice and that it was time to move on.[29] The Virginia Department of Elections recorded the race as a general election for the Board of Supervisors, Courthouse District.[30]

    Mayor of Appomattox (2006–2008)

    2006 election

    Undeterred by his 2005 loss, Wilson entered the May 2006 mayoral race against Vice-Mayor Marvin Hugh Mitchell. The election drew unusually heavy voter turnout of 36 percent among registered town voters. Wilson won with 209 votes to Mitchell's 164, a margin of 56 to 44 percent, becoming the Town of Appomattox's first new mayor in 28 years—Mayor Ronnie Spiggle had held the office since 1978.[31][32]

    Wilson acknowledged the significance of his victory, noting that he felt he had run not only against Mitchell but also against the incumbent mayor and the broader political establishment. He thanked his supporters, including his wife Patsy, whom he called his campaign manager, and pledged to be "Mayor of all the people" with a non-partisan approach.[31][33]

    Tenure as mayor

    Wilson took office on July 1, 2006, and opened his tenure by extending an olive branch to the Town Council, acknowledging past disagreements and soliciting cooperation.[34] In January 2007, he delivered a "State of the Town Report" in which he reported the town's financial status as strong, with assets of approximately $12 million against debts of $2.6 million. He recommended financing capital improvements through low-interest loans rather than depleting general reserves and emphasized the need to adjust tax rates and fees to recover operating expenses.[35]

    Wilson identified three key priorities for his administration: maintaining the town's financial integrity, creating an overlay district to protect entrance corridors from unrestricted development, and promoting tourism.[35] He served on the 2007 Virginia Municipal League's General Laws Steering Committee, contributing to statewide policy decisions on issues including water, biosolids, and wastewater disposal.[36]

    On the waterline issue, Wilson continued to oppose the proposed Concord-to-Appomattox pipeline. In July 2006, he declined the county's request for the town to appoint representatives to a waterline negotiating committee, arguing that the council needed more time to study the issue.[37] This stance drew criticism from county officials, with Board Chairman Carter noting it created a "strategic void" in negotiations.[37]

    Charter change controversy

    In May 2007, Wilson clashed publicly with three council members—Jimmy Mayberry, Lewis McDearmon, and Joyce Bennett—over a proposed change to the Town Charter that would have transferred most of the mayor's authority to the Town Manager. Wilson accused the three of acting out of personal spite and obstructionism, and expressed strong opposition to what he characterized as an undermining of the office voters had elected him to fill. He urged citizens to contact council members to voice their opinions on the proposed changes.[38]

    Wal-Mart and economic development

    Wilson publicly welcomed the arrival of Wal-Mart to Appomattox in 2007, predicting the store would act as an anchor that would attract shoppers and boost local businesses and tourism.[39] However, he drew criticism from some citizens who accused the mayor and council of keeping negotiations with Wal-Mart from the public, which they viewed as contradicting Wilson's platform of transparency.[40]

    Later campaigns (2008–2010)

    2008 mayoral race

    Wilson sought re-election as mayor in the May 2008 town elections but was narrowly defeated by local dentist Paul D. Harvey, who won 202 votes to Wilson's 189—a margin of just 13 votes.[41] The same election saw two newcomers, Bryan Baine and Karl "Toots" Carter, oust incumbents Joyce Bennett and Steven Lawson from the council.[41] Wilson's loss was attributed in part to voter sentiment on the waterline issue, with the town divided between those favoring regional cooperation and those supporting Wilson's local-control approach.

    2010 mayoral race

    In February 2010, Wilson filed to challenge Harvey in a rematch. He distributed a newsletter and questionnaire to town residents outlining his opposition to the waterline, which drew criticism from opponents who called the survey questions biased.[42] The May 4, 2010, election was widely characterized as a referendum on whether the town should cooperate with the county on the proposed waterline. Harvey won decisively, 279 to 156—a nearly 2-to-1 margin.[43]

    The 2010 election also saw a clean sweep by waterline-supporting council candidates, with all six winning seats and unseating two incumbents who had opposed the project. Harvey described the result as proof that citizens supported not only the waterline but also cooperation between the town and county.[43] The loss effectively ended Wilson's electoral career.

    Wilson's record of one win in five contests—as recorded by the Virginia Department of Elections—belies the impact he had on local politics. His advocacy helped push the county to revise its waterline cost estimates multiple times, prompted the hiring of a professional planner, and forced greater public engagement on land-use issues.[44][30]

    Personal life and legacy

    Wilson was married to Patricia Jean Guill Wilson, known as Patsy, who was active alongside him in civic and historical preservation activities.[4] The couple had five children: Cynthia Wilson Shebuski, Terry Lynn Hatcher, Kelly M. Bevins, Henry Weldon Wilson, and Elaine W. Glose, who predeceased him. He was also survived by ten grandchildren and two great-grandchildren.[4]

    Wilson was a member of Liberty Baptist Church in Appomattox.[4] On June 30, 2014, Appomattox Town Manager David Garrett presented Wilson with a plaque recognizing his years of service as a councilman and mayor.[45]

    Wilson died on Tuesday, September 13, 2016, one week before his 85th birthday. A celebration of his life was held on October 2, 2016, at his residence on Church Street. In lieu of flowers, his family requested donations to the building fund at Liberty Baptist Church.[4]

    In the years since Wilson's death, the issues he championed have continued to resonate in Appomattox County politics. The proposed Concord-to-Appomattox waterline—which Wilson fought for six years—was ultimately constructed in a form shaped in part by the public scrutiny he and the Citizens Coalition brought to the project. His insistence on government transparency and citizen engagement left a lasting mark on local civic culture, even as his electoral record reflected the limits of his appeal at the ballot box.
