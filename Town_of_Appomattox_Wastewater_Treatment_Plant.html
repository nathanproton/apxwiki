    The Town of Appomattox Wastewater Treatment Plant refers collectively to two municipal sewage treatment facilities operated by the Town of Appomattox, Virginia, from the mid-twentieth century through the 2010s: an older trickling filter plant constructed around 1936 on Church Street, and a modern Sequential Batch Reactor (SBR) facility built in 2000 on Ethel Street. Between 2005 and 2020, both plants were at the center of a series of intersecting crises—litigation over construction defects at the SBR, chronic violations of state water-quality standards for copper and zinc, and a long-running effort to close the aging trickling filter plant and expand SBR capacity. The town's management of these overlapping challenges consumed years of Town Council deliberation, millions of dollars in repairs and compliance costs, and eventually earned national recognition when plant operators achieved compliance through a low-cost chemical treatment regimen in 2017.

    Background

    The trickling filter plant

    The Town of Appomattox has operated centralized sewage treatment since at least the 1930s. The older of its two facilities, commonly called the trickling filter plant, was constructed around 1936 and located on Church Street within the town limits. Trickling filter technology—which passes wastewater over a fixed bed of rocks or media on which bacteria break down organic material—was standard practice at the time, but the plant was not designed with the metal-removal standards that would later be required by state and federal regulators. When the copper issue was finally addressed in the 2000s, officials discovered that no standards had existed for heavy metal discharge at the time of the plant's construction.[11] By the early 2010s, the plant's technology was described by town officials as "outdated" with "unsafe operating conditions," and it remained in service primarily because consolidating all wastewater flows onto the SBR required capital investment the town had difficulty financing.[28]

    Construction of the SBR (2000–2002)

    By the late 1990s, the town's older lagoon-based sewage system had reached the limit of its expansion capacity, prompting the decision to replace it with a modern Sequential Batch Reactor facility. The Town of Appomattox contracted Lynchburg-based engineering firm Hurt & Proffitt, Inc. to oversee the project; Hurt & Proffitt in turn subcontracted Maryland-based Johnson, Mirmiran and Thompson (JMT) to handle the technical design, since JMT specialized in wastewater treatment plant engineering. The $3.7 million facility—jointly financed by the Town and Appomattox County, with a matching state grant offsetting part of the debt—was constructed in 2000 and completed in 2001.[7] The plant is a 130×42-foot structure containing two 45×45-foot concrete settling basins, located on Ethel Street. Its design capacity was approximately 192,000 gallons per day for treatment, with a discharge capacity of up to 300,000 gallons per day.[7]

    Early defect discovery

    Cracks in the SBR's concrete basins were discovered almost immediately after the plant opened.[8] The basins had been constructed with walls 12 inches thick—four inches thinner than the 16-inch standard for liquid-retaining concrete structures. Engineers later established that the design should have been governed by American Concrete Institute standard ACI 350, which specifies requirements for environmental engineering concrete structures, rather than the less stringent ACI 318 standard that was used.[3] At a special called Town Council meeting in September 2002, representatives of JMT and Hurt & Proffitt met with council members to discuss potential repairs. According to subsequent testimony from the mayor and town clerk, a JMT vice president acknowledged at that meeting that "We screwed up."[4b] At the same meeting, the council voted to engage structural engineer Ned Cleland of Blue Ridge Design, Inc., to independently study the SBR basins and make recommendations.[38] A repair plan—dubbed "box-in-a-box"—called for pouring new concrete inside the cracked basins to form reinforced interior walls.[3] Despite agreeing to make repairs, JMT did not execute them, setting the stage for litigation.

    Design Defects and Litigation (2005–2007)

    Lawsuit filed

    In July 2005, the Appomattox Town Council began formally exploring its legal options for recovering repair costs from the alleged negligent design of the SBR. Town Attorney David Hawkins estimated the cost of remediation at approximately $2 million.[1a] The town filed suit against both Hurt & Proffitt and JMT on grounds of negligence and breach of contract—the latter based on JMT's 2002 agreement to correct the facility's design flaws, which a court would later characterize as an enforceable contract. Town attorneys Robin Wood of Edmunds & Williams and David Hawkins of Overbey, Hawkins and Selz handled the case.[2] In March 2005, Appomattox Circuit Court Judge Richard S. Blanton had already ruled that JMT could be held as a legal defendant, rejecting the firm's demurrer argument that no binding contract existed between it and the town.[2]

    In December 2005, the Town Council held a special meeting to convene in closed executive session to receive a legal briefing on the lawsuit. Council members were informed that depositions were scheduled for the following week in Lynchburg, and that the court date had been set for February 16, 2006.[1b]

    Trial

    The civil trial began before Judge Blanton in Appomattox County Circuit Court in February 2006, with the town seeking $1.2 million in damages—a figure that reflected the estimated cost of the "box-in-a-box" remediation rather than the full original $2 million estimate. The town presented two days of testimony on February 23–24, and the trial was scheduled to resume March 23–24 for JMT's defense.[3]

    Expert witness testimony was central to the town's case. Independent structural engineer Gary Loomis of Masters Engineers testified that the original designs were "deficient" and failed to meet applicable American Concrete Institute standards. Town engineer Ned Cleland of Blue Ridge Design provided similar testimony.[3] A Virginia DEQ official, Fred Di Lella—Water Compliance and Assessment Manager—testified that in inspecting the Appomattox facility, "I have never seen cracks like I saw at the Appomattox facility," and stated that he planned to recommend groundwater testing near the plant due to the risk that wastewater might be leaking through cracked basin walls into the surrounding soil.[4] After the town and Hurt & Proffitt finished presenting their cases, JMT attorneys moved on multiple grounds to strike the town's evidence; Judge Blanton denied all motions.[4]

    In May 2006, following JMT's presentation of its defense, Judge Blanton ruled in favor of the Town of Appomattox, ordering JMT to pay $1.2 million for repairs to the plant. He found Hurt & Proffitt not liable, placing full responsibility on JMT for the defective design work.[8]

    Settlement and judgment

    JMT initiated an appeal of the $1.2 million judgment. During extended negotiations, JMT offered a settlement of $1.164 million—$36,000 below the court's award. The Town Council voted to accept the offer, with Town Attorney Frank Wright noting that an appeal process would have cost the town approximately $50,000 and that the council considered it unlikely the judgment would be overturned.[8] JMT submitted a check for $1.164 million in June 2007.[9] In total, the litigation had cost the Town more than $263,000 in attorney's fees, with an additional $163,000 in fees attributable to Hurt & Proffitt's involvement.[42] In May 2006, the town also approved a $17,722 proposal from Hurt & Proffitt to conduct DEQ-required groundwater testing at the plant site to check for leakage through the cracked basin walls.[6]

    Selecting a repair engineer (2007–2008)

    With settlement funds in hand, the council turned to the question of who would administer the repairs. In December 2007, the council voted to engage Ned Cleland of Blue Ridge Design to produce the remedial engineering drawings for the basin walls.[15] However, a contentious debate ensued over whether to return to Hurt & Proffitt—who had been exonerated by the court—for contract administration, or to hire a different firm. Council member Steve Lawson argued that it was imprudent to re-engage a firm associated with the original debacle; Council member Joyce Bennett countered that switching firms would waste taxpayer money and time, noting that a new engineer would need one to one and a half months to review existing documents before work could begin.[16]

    Over three weeks in March and April 2008, the council repeatedly tabled votes on the question. On April 1, 2008, the council voted 4–1 to appoint Hurt & Proffitt as lead engineering firm for contract administration of the repairs, with Lawson casting the lone dissenting vote.[17] Hurt & Proffitt subsequently apologized in writing to the town for the mistakes of its subcontractor JMT.[17] The "box-in-a-box" remediation—pouring new concrete inside the cracked basins—proceeded under their supervision using the settlement funds.

    Metal Discharge Compliance (2004–2017)

    Concurrent with the litigation, the town faced a separate and in some respects more protracted challenge: chronic violations of state water-quality limits for heavy metals at both treatment facilities. The root cause was the corrosive nature of the town's groundwater. Appomattox's well water was naturally acidic, and as it traveled through copper pipes in homes and businesses, it caused the metal to leach out. That copper—and later zinc from galvanized pipes—passed through the sewer system and discharged into Caldwell's Creek, a tributary of Falling River. The creek's aquatic life, not human drinking water, bore the environmental impact, but both the trickling filter plant and the SBR operated under Virginia DEQ discharge permits that set strict limits on heavy metals.[11]

    Copper violations at the trickling filter plant

    By May 2004, the town had already received a warning letter from DEQ regarding copper and zinc issues at the Church Street trickling filter plant. Town Manager David Garrett Jr. met with DEQ, and at its June 2004 regular meeting, the council entered a consent order with the state to resolve the copper violations, allowing the town time to bring levels into compliance while halting further enforcement action.[40] The permit limit for copper was 21 micrograms per liter. Because the plant dated to 1936, it had never been designed to filter metals; its sole original purpose was to clean suspended solids from wastewater.[11]

    For years the town employed a variety of chemical strategies—soda ash to change pH levels, magnesium hydroxide to precipitate metals out of solution, and sequestering agents to bind metal ions before discharge. In May 2007, with copper levels still a concern, the council adopted an ordinance amending Chapter 62 of the Town Code to prohibit copper piping in all new construction within the town limits, with a narrow exception allowing up to five linear feet of copper for appliance connections.[12] Town Attorney Frank Wright acknowledged that the ordinance would not solve the existing copper problem but would prevent further additions to the town's copper load. Council member Steve Lawson summed up the prevailing view: "We do not see it as a hindrance."[12] In July 2007, DEQ amended the existing consent order to recognize the town's progress in reducing copper levels.[13] In August 2007, the town began a pilot program using a chemical called MIDFLOC 2603L from Rochester Midland Corp. to further reduce copper and zinc in both plants.[14] A second pilot program using magnesium hydroxide at the trickling filter plant was authorized in August 2008.[18]

    Zinc violations at the SBR (2008–2011)

    Even as copper levels at the trickling filter plant began to improve, the SBR developed its own metal compliance problem. Between October 2008 and January 2009, the SBR discharged zinc at levels ranging from 90.5 to 199 micrograms per liter—far above its permit limit of 49 micrograms per liter. DEQ Regional Enforcement Representative Marvin Booth described the Town as "pro-active" in addressing the problem and noted that DEQ attempts to work cooperatively with localities, offering suggestions without dictating specific remedies.[19]

    In July 2009, DEQ issued a formal consent order requiring the town to hire a licensed professional engineer, conduct a Site-Specific Water Effect Ratio (SSWER) study on the plant's effluent and the receiving stream to determine the impact on aquatic life, and submit an interim progress report within a year.[20] Mayor Paul Harvey publicly attributed the metal problems to the corrosive nature of the town's groundwater, which damaged pipes throughout the system and caused metals to leach out before reaching the treatment plants.[21]

    In March 2010, the council approved a $15,000 engineering fee to execute the plan of action required by the zinc consent order, and DEQ approved the plan shortly thereafter. A revised zinc permit limit of 53 micrograms per liter was established, effective October 26, 2011.[22][23] By November 2010, the metal compliance situation had become severe enough that four of the town's eight wells were taken offline because their contribution of corrosive water was driving zinc levels too high at the SBR.[24] By October 2011, Town Manager Bill Gillespie reported that zinc levels at the SBR had dropped to approximately 20 micrograms per liter—well below the new permit limit of 53.[26]

    The waterline connection

    From at least 2009 onward, town officials identified the U.S. 460 Concord-to-Appomattox waterline project as a potential long-term solution to the metal compliance problem. By switching from corrosive well water to treated surface water supplied by Campbell County Utility Service Authority, the town hoped to eliminate the source of the metal loading in its sewer system entirely. In July 2010, the newly elected Town Council voted 5–0 to join Appomattox County on the waterline project, ending years of internal debate.[25]

    However, once the waterline became operational in 2012, zinc levels at the SBR did not drop as quickly as anticipated. Officials suspected that zinc attached to existing pipes required flushing over time, with levels still registering 30 to 40 parts per million despite the permit limit of 249 ppm (a different metric).[32] The town continued chemical treatment programs for several more years.

    National recognition (2017)

    The zinc consent order was finally resolved in 2017 after plant staff identified an effective treatment protocol. On the recommendation of consultant John Hricko, operators began adding a daily combination of lime and soda ash to raise the pH of the water, which caused zinc to precipitate out of solution rather than discharge with the effluent. The protocol cost approximately $50 per day to operate. Within two months, zinc levels dropped from 253 micrograms per liter to approximately 30 micrograms per liter—well below the permit limit of 53.[36]

    The achievement was significant enough to earn the town coverage in Treatment Plant Operator, a national trade publication focused on wastewater treatment practice and professionals. Plant operators Jerome Houston, Patrick Witcher, and Kyle Lerner, along with Plant Supervisor Mitch Stone, were featured in the magazine's October 2017 issue. Stone expressed pride in the outcome: "We were really excited about the article," he said, adding that he was proud of all the operators.[36] The lime-and-soda-ash approach saved the town from having to invest in electrocoagulation, a more expensive alternative technology estimated to cost $800,000 to install and $40,000 to $50,000 per year to operate.[36] With the exit from the consent order, the SBR's metal discharge issues—which had consumed years of regulatory attention—were effectively resolved.

    Facility Modernization and Consolidation (2011–2020)

    Closure of the trickling filter plant

    As early as April 2011, town officials began discussing the possibility of eliminating the aging trickling filter plant entirely, consolidating all wastewater flow onto the SBR. Interim Town Manager Bill Gillespie proposed conducting an analysis to determine whether the SBR alone could handle the town's full wastewater load.[27] By April 2012, Gillespie presented the council with a formal case for upgrading the SBR rather than replacing the trickling filter plant at a cost of approximately $4 million, arguing that the trickling filter's outdated technology and unsafe operating conditions made renovation impractical.[28]

    An April 2012 smoke test of nine miles of town sewer piping revealed a significant problem contributing to the trickling filter plant's difficulties: at least three locations where cracks and holes in the lines allowed stormwater to infiltrate the system after rainfall. Under normal conditions, the trickling filter plant processed about 75,000 gallons per day; during rain events, infiltration was spiking flows to approximately 345,000 gallons per day.[29] Inspections the following year confirmed approximately a dozen cracks and holes in roughly 90,000 feet of sewer lines throughout the town.[33]

    SBR expansion and the boundary line dispute

    Closing the trickling filter plant required not only fixing infiltration problems in the collector lines, but also expanding the SBR's capacity to handle flows from the entire town—a project that intersected with a separate boundary line adjustment dispute between the town and Appomattox County. In August 2012, Gillespie warned the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors that if the boundary adjustment was denied, the county could face $2.75 million in costs associated with building its own sewer infrastructure along the U.S. 460 corridor.[30] The town's proposal called for it to bear the full cost of constructing a sewer line and expanding the SBR to serve the Route 460 corridor, with no cost to county taxpayers, in exchange for the boundary extension.[31]

    The SBR—formally renamed the Appomattox Water Reclamation Facility by the mid-2010s—would serve as the sole treatment facility once the trickling filter plant was decommissioned. Closing the older plant was estimated to save the town money annually by eliminating the costs of maintaining separate testing protocols, environmental permits, and chemical treatment programs for the Church Street facility.

    USDA grant application

    In May 2014, Gillespie informed the council that the town intended to apply for a federal matching grant through the U.S. Department of Agriculture to help finance the closure project. The total estimated project cost was $3.7 million, encompassing the construction of a pump station (estimated at $2.3 million) to transfer wastewater from the trickling filter location to the SBR, and the repair of infiltration-prone collector lines (estimated at $1.2 to $1.4 million).[33] In July 2014, the town submitted a preliminary engineering report to the USDA; the agency indicated it would respond with a funding amount and loan terms within approximately 60 days.[34] By October 2014, the USDA indicated that an environmental review was required before a formal funding offer could be extended.[35]

    The USDA grant process moved slowly. By February 2018, engineering teams presented a new design to the council addressing the remaining issues at the wastewater facilities, and Hurt & Proffitt was again selected to manage the work.[44] Full decommissioning of the trickling filter plant and completion of the consolidation project extended beyond the 2020 window covered by this article, but significant progress had been made in planning and securing funding by that point.

    Public Reception and Controversy

    The SBR's defects and the subsequent litigation generated sustained community attention throughout the mid-2000s. Readers submitted letters to the Times-Virginian debating whether local government had adequately explained the situation to citizens, and some raised broader concerns about proposals to transfer ownership of the town's water and sewer infrastructure to a regional Public Service Authority. Council member John L. Wilson published a letter to the editor in March 2005 arguing that local self-government required local control of public services—"without local ownership and control of public services there seems to be little need for local government"—expressing opposition to any transfer of the wastewater plants to a PSA or to Lynchburg's system.[1c]

    The April 2008 vote to re-engage Hurt & Proffitt for the repair contract also exposed divisions within the council. Council member Lawson's months-long effort to switch firms—despite Hurt & Proffitt's legal exoneration—reflected a segment of public and official opinion that remained skeptical of the firm. The council's final 4–1 vote in favor of Hurt & Proffitt concluded what the Times-Virginian described as "several weeks of back-and-forth debate on the company's merits."[17]

    The metal discharge saga generated less overt public controversy but was a persistent topic at council workshops and regular meetings across more than a decade. Mayor Paul Harvey's characterization of the town's well water as "corrosive" was used to explain the metal problems without assigning blame to the treatment plants themselves, and the consent orders were consistently presented to the public as manageable regulatory requirements rather than environmental emergencies. Harvey acknowledged in public remarks that DEQ had been "fair and patient" in allowing the town time to correct high copper levels.[21b]
