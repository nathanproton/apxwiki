Paul Douglas Harvey, DDS (c. 1952 – October 19, 2020) was an American dentist and politician who served as Mayor of the Town of Appomattox, Virginia, for twelve consecutive years from 2008 to 2020. He was the longest-serving Appomattox mayor of the modern era. A champion of the controversial U.S. 460 waterline project and the creator of Courtland Festival Park, Harvey reshaped the town’s infrastructure and civic life during a period of significant change. He was elected to the Town Council in May 2020 after choosing not to seek a fifth term as mayor, but died five months later at the age of 68.[1][2]

Harvey’s death prompted an outpouring of community tributes, including resolutions from the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors and the Virginia House of Delegates, a celebration of life attended by approximately two hundred people at Courtland Festival Park, a park bench dedication, and the naming of an annual memorial golf tournament in his honor.[3][4][5]

Early life and dental career

Paul Douglas Harvey earned his Doctor of Dental Surgery (DDS) degree and established a dental practice, Paul Harvey DDS, initially in Midlothian, Virginia. He relocated the practice to the Town of Appomattox in 2000, where it operated as Appomattox Family Dentistry. Harvey became a registered voter in Appomattox the same year and resided at 168 First Street.[6][7]

In addition to his private practice, Harvey volunteered annually at the Bland Ministry Center Dental Clinic through the Appomattox Baptist Association, providing free dental services to underserved patients in Bland County. The association publicly thanked him on multiple occasions for his donated expertise.[8][9]

Following Harvey’s death in October 2020, Dr. Donald Murry Jr., a semi-retired dentist from Midlothian, arrived the next day to help maintain the practice and care for Harvey’s patients. Murry’s sons, Dr. Donald Murry III and Dr. Ken Murry, along with associate Dr. Todd Kuhn, subsequently joined the practice.[10]

Mayoral tenure (2008–2020)

2008 election

Harvey entered the 2008 mayoral race against incumbent mayor John Wilson. The campaign focused heavily on whether the Town of Appomattox should cooperate with Appomattox County on a proposed waterline from Concord, with Harvey supporting the project and Wilson opposing it. During the campaign, Harvey’s residency in Appomattox became a point of contention; opponents alleged he only spent limited time in the town. Supporters, including the county voter registrar, publicly affirmed that Harvey resided and worked full-time in Appomattox.[7][11]

Harvey won on May 6, 2008, defeating Wilson by a margin of thirteen votes, 202 to 189. The Clerk of the Appomattox Circuit Court, Barbara Williams, swore him in as mayor.[12][13] The narrow outcome was widely interpreted as a mandate on the waterline question.

The waterline campaign

The waterline issue dominated Harvey’s first two terms. The Town of Appomattox relied on wells that produced corrosive water with elevated copper and zinc levels, leading to consent orders from the Virginia Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ). Harvey consistently argued that connecting to a county waterline sourced from the Otter River would solve these quality problems, reduce monthly water bills, attract businesses, and support long-term growth.[14][15]

The issue divided the Town Council. Council members John “Plicky” Williams, Bryan Baine, Karl “Toots” Carter, and Jennifer Jamerson-Scruggs were skeptical of the project, while Norman H. “Jimmy” Mayberry and Lewis McDearmon aligned with Harvey in support.[16] Harvey publicly criticized the Council’s tone in letters to the County Board of Supervisors, stating that the town should pursue a partnership or, failing that, become a customer of the county water system.[17]

Harvey signed a consent order with the DEQ on behalf of the Town to address elevated zinc levels at the SBR wastewater treatment plant and repeatedly submitted proposals to the County detailing the Town’s desired terms for participation in the waterline project.[18][19] He also advocated for extending the waterline down Police Tower Road, citing cheaper bidding prices and future development potential, though the County repeatedly rejected the proposal.[20]

2010 re-election

Harvey faced John Wilson again in the May 2010 mayoral race. The campaign was once more a referendum on the waterline, with a coordinated slate of pro-waterline council candidates endorsed alongside Harvey by Virginia Delegate Watkins M. Abbitt. Numerous letters to the editor urged support for Harvey, praised his leadership style, and defended his residency and work ethic against continued attacks.[21][22]

Harvey won decisively, defeating Wilson by a margin of nearly two to one, 279 to 156.[23] All six pro-waterline council candidates also won their seats. Shortly after the new council was seated, the body voted to cooperate with Appomattox County on a $5.6 million waterline agreement, and Harvey was authorized to execute a water purchase contract with the County Board of Supervisors.[24][25]

Later terms (2012–2020)

Harvey ran unopposed in both 2012 and 2016, receiving 132 and 55 votes respectively.[26][27] With the waterline question resolved, his later terms focused on sewer infrastructure, boundary line adjustments with the county, economic development, and community programming. He pushed for a sewer system upgrade estimated at $2.3 million, funded in part through proposed boundary adjustments that would expand the town’s revenue base along the U.S. 460 corridor.[28]

Harvey also presided over the town’s first vision process meeting, where approximately twenty-five citizens participated in a facilitated discussion about the town’s direction through 2025.[29] He reported reducing town spending by twenty-three percent through cuts to legal and engineering fees and greater reliance on town staff for projects.[30]

In January 2020, Harvey announced that he would not seek a fifth term as mayor but planned to run for a Town Council seat instead, stating he wished to continue serving the town in a different capacity after twelve years in the mayor’s office.[31]

Courtland Festival Park

Courtland Festival Park became Harvey’s signature accomplishment and most visible legacy. He described the park as a personal “pet project” and oversaw its development during his tenure as mayor. The park, located near Court Street and Highland Avenue, hosted community events, concerts, and festivals, including the annual Historic Appomattox Railroad Festival. Harvey served on the Courtland Park Foundation board alongside County Administrator Susan Adams and organized annual events featuring vendors, music, and stage performances.[32][33]

Harvey also served as president of the Railroad Festival Board of Directors after the resignation of Tony Clifton, guiding the annual festival through organizational challenges.[34] After his death, the park became the site of his celebration of life service, a dedicated memorial bench, and annual stew fundraisers and golf tournaments held in his memory.[3][5]

2016 tornado response

On February 24, 2016, an EF-3 tornado struck Appomattox County, causing significant damage to homes and property across the area. Harvey was singled out by the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors for providing calm and strong leadership during the aftermath, spending days without rest while representing the town and its citizens. The Board presented a resolution honoring both Harvey and Gene Stewart, the Regional Coordinator of the Virginia Department of Emergency Management, for their leadership during the disaster.[35]

Other initiatives

Throughout his twelve years as mayor, Harvey was involved in a wide range of civic issues. He supported the arrival of Walmart, viewing it as an asset that would increase the local tax base and provide convenient access for elderly and retired citizens.[36] He opposed a proposed 380-foot Sweet Briar College radio tower on Tower Hill Road, describing it as an eyesore near the Appomattox Station Battlefield and the Museum of the Confederacy.[37]

Harvey endorsed a landscaping ordinance for businesses, advocated for cooperation with the county on an animal control officer, proclaimed February 22 as “Invest in Kids Day” in support of early childhood programs, led the town’s first Arbor Day celebration as a Tree City, and explored the creation of a joint Economic Development Authority with Appomattox County.[38][39][40] He also championed the train depot’s revitalization, describing it as a potential focal point for downtown that could enhance its historic value, a cause later taken up by council member Nathan A. Simpson.[41]

In June 2020, Harvey commended a Black Lives Matter event held at the Appomattox Courthouse, describing it as well-conducted and peaceful, and praised both the organizers and the sheriff’s department for ensuring the event’s orderly nature.[42]

Town Council (2020)

Harvey was elected to the Appomattox Town Council on May 19, 2020, alongside five other candidates. Mayor Richard Conner appointed him to the Finance Committee and the Planning Commission. Council member Timothy Garrett presented Harvey with a plaque in appreciation for his twelve years of service as mayor.[43][44][45]

During his brief time on Council, Harvey suggested inviting B2X Online to present a town-wide internet solution, building on the company’s recent pitch to the County Board of Supervisors. He also participated in discussions about the skateboard ordinance alongside fellow new council member Nathan Simpson.[46]

Harvey died on October 19, 2020, approximately five months after taking his council seat. James J. Boyce Sr. was subsequently appointed to fill the vacancy.[47]

Community and charitable service

Harvey was active in community organizations throughout his time in Appomattox. He served on the Courtland Park Foundation board, led the Historic Appomattox Railroad Festival Board of Directors as president, and participated in multiple charitable events. He organized an annual Brunswick stew fundraiser at his dental office to benefit Brothers In Arms, a nonprofit assisting first responders, with family members helping at each event.[48]

Harvey volunteered annually with the Appomattox Baptist Association on dental mission trips to Bland County, providing free dental care to underserved communities.[8] He also donated to the American Legion Auxiliary’s “Boxes for the Military” program and participated in the annual Tour of Homes event, where his office displayed a Dickens Village collection during the Christmas season.[49][50]

Death and legacy

Harvey died on October 19, 2020, at the age of sixty-eight. His passing came during the COVID-19 pandemic, which shaped the memorial events that followed.[1]

A celebration of life service was held on October 22, 2020, at Courtland Festival Park—the park Harvey had championed as his signature mayoral project. Approximately two hundred people attended, with tributes from family and friends, music, and prayer. Attendees were asked to bring lawn chairs, wear masks, and practice social distancing.[3]

At the October 10, 2020 meeting of the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors, Supervisor Watkins Abbitt suggested a moment of honor for Harvey. The Board subsequently passed a formal resolution honoring his service, and Delegate Matt Fariss presented a resolution from the Virginia House of Delegates on behalf of the General Assembly.[4][51] Mayor Richard Conner formally recognized Harvey’s contributions at a Town Council meeting, describing him as a gentleman whose goals the council would strive to continue achieving.[52]

Harvey’s legacy continued through multiple memorial events in the following years. The 2021 Historic Appomattox Railroad Festival was dedicated to his memory, with a ceremony honoring his contributions to the town and a park bench dedicated at the corner of Court Street and Highland Avenue.[5][53] The inaugural Paul Harvey Open golf tournament was held at Falling River Country Club on October 23, 2021, raising $10,500 for the Courtland Festival Park Foundation. The tournament became an annual event, with proceeds supporting both park maintenance and a scholarship at Appomattox County High School.[54][55] The Harvey family also continued his tradition of stew fundraisers at Courtland Park, selling out over two hundred quarts at a single event.[56]
