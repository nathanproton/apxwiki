    The Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department (AVFD) is the primary fire suppression and emergency response organization serving the Town of Appomattox and surrounding areas of Appomattox County, Virginia. Established as a volunteer organization in the mid-twentieth century, the AVFD has been a cornerstone of community safety and civic engagement for decades. The department operates from its modern firehouse, completed in October 2005, and maintains a staff of dedicated volunteer firefighters supported by an auxiliary organization. Since its founding, the AVFD has served the community through emergency response, fire prevention education, and public engagement initiatives.

    History

    Early history (1950s–2000)

    Records of the Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department's founding are limited, but the organization was well-established by the 1950s, operating from a firehouse located beneath the Appomattox Town Office Building on Linden Street.[1] The department's early operations reflected the volunteer tradition characteristic of rural Virginia fire services, with community members donating their time to provide fire suppression and rescue services.

    Throughout the latter half of the twentieth century, the AVFD maintained its commitment to serving both the Town of Appomattox and surrounding county areas. By the early 2000s, the organization had become one of the most recognizable civic institutions in the region, participating regularly in community events including the Christmas parade and engaging in public education initiatives at local schools.[2] The department operated a fleet of fire apparatus and rescue vehicles, and by 2003–2004, was led by Fire Chief Timothy P. Garrett, a figure who would become synonymous with the department's expansion and modernization.

    New firehouse construction (2003–2005)

    In 2003, the Board of Supervisors and Town Council recognized that the aging firehouse—a cramped facility beneath the town office building—could not meet the department's growing operational needs. Ground was broken on September 3, 2003, for a new, purpose-built firehouse that would provide adequate space for apparatus storage, crew facilities, training, and administration.[3]

    Fire Chief Timmy Garrett spearheaded the construction effort, working with a fundraising committee comprised of prominent community members including John Caldwell, Ronnie Spiggle, Watkins Abbitt, Del Chambers, James Cheatham, Greg Heath, Phillip Jamerson, Anne Miller, and Bill Slagle. The total project cost was approximately $490,000.[4] The County Board of Supervisors contributed $150,000, and the Town of Appomattox allocated $90,000, covering roughly half of the construction budget. The remaining funds were raised through community donations, golf tournaments, yard sales, bake sales, grants from the Walter J. Payne Foundation ($10,000), and other fundraising activities.[5]

    By February 2005, the new firehouse was approaching 75 percent completion of its first phase. Fire Chief Garrett reported that the department was short approximately $140,000 to fully complete the project but noted that firefighters had significantly reduced costs through volunteer labor on construction tasks including partition walls, sheetrock installation, and portions of the plumbing and electrical work.[6] The 8,400-square-foot facility was sited on Business Route 460 East in the Town of Appomattox, adjacent to the Appomattox Volunteer Rescue Squad building, whose leadership graciously donated the land for the new firehouse.[7]

    An open house and ribbon-cutting ceremony were scheduled for October 2, 2005, featuring remarks from U.S. Congressman Virgil Goode (R-5th), Appomattox Mayor Ronnie Spiggle, and Board of Supervisors Chairman Samuel Carter.[8] More than 200 people attended the grand opening on October 5, 2005, celebrating the completion of the new facility, which was approximately twice the size of the old firehouse.[9] Fire Chief Garrett expressed his gratitude for community support and acknowledged the sacrifices of firefighters who had worked nights and weekends to assist in construction efforts.[10]

    Modern era (2005–present)

    Following the opening of the new firehouse in 2005, the Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department entered a period of enhanced capability and expanded community engagement. The organization formed its first-ever auxiliary in 2005, comprised of spouses, family members, and friends of firefighters, to support fundraising and community awareness initiatives.[11] Under Auxiliary President Jane Vaughan's early leadership, the group undertook significant fundraising efforts to acquire new equipment and enhance fire department capabilities.

    In 2007, the AVFD Auxiliary successfully raised funds to purchase a thermal imaging camera—a piece of equipment that would enable firefighters to locate victims in smoke-filled structures by detecting body heat.[12] This acquisition represented a meaningful upgrade to the department's rescue capabilities and demonstrated the community's commitment to firefighter safety and effectiveness. The department continued to host annual golf tournaments, yard sales, and other fundraisers to maintain equipment and fund operations.

    In early 2018, Fire Chief Timmy Garrett retired after sixteen years of leadership, and the department elected his son, Jonathan Garrett, as the new chief. Jonathan had been with the department since 2007 and had served as Lieutenant and Assistant Fire Chief. The transition maintained continuity in the Garrett family's decades-long tradition of fire service leadership.[22]

    Under Jonathan Garrett's leadership, the AVFD continued to respond to hundreds of calls annually—approximately 300 calls in 2017 and 368 in 2018.[23] The department was honored at the 2018 "Salute to Appomattox" fundraiser at Liberty Baptist Church, which recognized the department's reliance on approximately 25 volunteers and highlighted the cost of firefighter gear at $12,000 per person.[24]

    In the 2020s, the AVFD has remained actively engaged in emergency response across its service area. The department secured funding for a new Pierce Enforcer Pumper fire engine in 2020–2021, made possible by $240,000 from the Town Council and additional fundraising, with the truck dedicated in January 2022.[25] In 2023, the Appomattox Town Council approved funding for a new breathing air compressor to support the department's respiratory protection systems.[13] In December 2024, the county and town jointly funded the purchase of a 2014 Pierce Arrow XT ladder truck for approximately $650,000, with the Board of Supervisors approving a $450,000 interest-free loan and the Town Council contributing $300,000.[14]

    Organization and governance

    The Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department operates as a volunteer organization providing fire suppression, rescue, and emergency medical services to the Town of Appomattox and surrounding unincorporated areas of Appomattox County. The department is accountable to the Board of Supervisors and the Town Council, which provide annual appropriations and approve policy matters affecting fire service operations. The organization operates in close coordination with the Appomattox Volunteer Rescue Squad, sharing facilities and coordinating emergency response.

    Like most volunteer fire departments in rural Virginia, the AVFD relies on a cadre of dedicated community members who commit their time and training to public safety. Volunteers participate in regular training, maintain equipment certification, and respond to emergency calls during both day and night hours. The department has historically maintained adequate staffing to provide rapid response times; in one notable 2004 incident, the AVFD responded to a structure fire within 12 minutes of receiving the call, deploying 14 firefighters, two engines, and a ladder truck.[15]

    Leadership

    Timothy W. "Timmy" Garrett served as Fire Chief of the Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department from approximately 2002 until his retirement in early 2018. Garrett joined the department in 1986 and led it through the construction of the new firehouse (2003–2005), the 2008 Williams Transco pipeline explosion response, and numerous emergency incidents. He was a visible and respected leader in the community, regularly representing the fire department at public events and working collaboratively with county and town officials to advance fire service capabilities. Garrett also served on the Appomattox Town Council beginning in 2010 and as Vice-Mayor from 2015.

    Following Garrett's retirement, his son Jonathan Garrett was voted in as Fire Chief by the department membership in 2018. Jonathan had joined the AVFD in 2007 after starting as a driver for the Rescue Squad. He rose through the ranks as Lieutenant and then Assistant Fire Chief over five years before succeeding his father. Jonathan also served briefly on the Appomattox Town Council (2018–2020) and teaches Automotive Technology at Appomattox County High School.[22]

    Facilities and equipment

    Firehouse

    The AVFD operates from a modern 8,400-square-foot firehouse located on Business Route 460 East in the Town of Appomattox, completed in October 2005. The facility replaced the department's original cramped quarters beneath the Appomattox Town Office Building on Linden Street, which had become inadequate for growing operational needs. The new firehouse features apparatus bays, administrative offices, meeting areas, gear lockers, and crew facilities. The structure was funded through a combination of county and town appropriations, community donations, and fundraising efforts totaling approximately $490,000.[16]

    Equipment and apparatus

    The AVFD maintains a fleet of fire suppression and rescue equipment. By 2003–2004, the department operated with at least two fire engines and a ladder truck.[17] The department has historically maintained modern fire apparatus through a combination of capital purchases and equipment donations from the community. Equipment acquisitions have included vehicles, hoses, nozzles, and specialized rescue tools. Notable equipment additions include the thermal imaging camera purchased by the auxiliary in 2007.[18]

    Operations and services

    The Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department provides comprehensive emergency services including structure fire suppression, vehicle extrication, rescue operations, and coordination with emergency medical services. The department responds to calls throughout the Town of Appomattox and surrounding areas, maintaining rapid response times that have historically averaged approximately 12 minutes from dispatch to arrival.[19]

    In addition to emergency response, the AVFD conducts public fire prevention education, including fire drills at local schools and information sessions at community facilities. The department participates in public events including the annual Appomattox Christmas Parade and maintains community relationships through outreach and education initiatives.[20]

    Auxiliary and community support

    The Appomattox Volunteer Fire Department Auxiliary was established in 2005, representing the first formal organization of spouses, family members, and friends to support fire department operations and fundraising. Under President Jane Vaughan's leadership, the auxiliary held its first meeting on April 19, 2005, and immediately undertook fundraising initiatives to support the department's capital needs. The auxiliary has organized benefit suppers, yard sales, bake sales, and other community events to raise funds for equipment and operational support.

    Notable auxiliary accomplishments include raising funds for a thermal imaging camera in 2007, purchasing equipment that would enhance firefighter safety and victim rescue capabilities.[21] The auxiliary has also participated in community awareness campaigns, including annual September 11 memorials honoring first responders and national service members. Through consistent fundraising efforts, the auxiliary has demonstrated strong community support for fire department operations and has helped bridge funding gaps between volunteer service provision and operational costs.
