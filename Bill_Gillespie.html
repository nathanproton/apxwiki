    Background

    William "Bill" Gillespie is an experienced municipal administrator and consultant who served the Town of Appomattox in various capacities throughout the 2000s and 2010s. Operating through his consulting firm MRG Consulting, LLC, Gillespie brought professional expertise in town management, infrastructure development, and regional planning to Central Virginia communities.

    Prior to his extended tenure as Interim Town Manager, Gillespie gained significant experience in regional governance and planning through his work with the Region 2000 Planning District Commission, which serves Central Virginia municipalities and counties. His experience in both regional and local governance positioned him to address the infrastructure and fiscal challenges facing smaller communities undergoing economic transitions.

    Region 2000 Service

    As Executive Director of Region 2000 (the Central Virginia Planning District Commission) from approximately 2004 to 2007, Gillespie conducted research and analysis on regional infrastructure needs. His work during this period focused on infrastructure planning and financial feasibility studies for member communities.

    Water Infrastructure Planning

    In 2004, while serving as Executive Director, Gillespie researched water line options for the Town of Appomattox and provided analysis to the Town Council on the feasibility and costs of different approaches to expanding water service to regional areas. This early work laid groundwork for later initiatives during his tenure as Town Manager.

    Regional Jail Study

    During 2006 and 2007, Gillespie participated in a comprehensive study examining regional jail consolidation and design standards. He presented findings to the region's jurisdictions showing that antiquated jail design in Appomattox County represented significant operational challenges and cost inefficiencies. The study analyzed cost-sharing scenarios and demonstrated potential savings for Appomattox through regional consolidation approaches.

    Interim Town Manager of Appomattox

    Appointment and Contract Extensions

    In October 2010, following the resignation of Town Manager Bart Van Nieuwenhuise in July 2010, the Appomattox Town Council hired Gillespie as interim town manager through MRG Consulting for an initial 60-day contract. However, Gillespie's tenure proved far more extensive than initially anticipated, lasting approximately 5.5 years as the Town Council extended his contract multiple times.

    Gillespie's first major contract extension came in January 2011, just three months into his interim tenure, reflecting Town Council confidence in his direction. Throughout his tenure, he maintained the "interim" designation despite years of continuous service, as the Town Council periodically renewed his contract rather than appointing him to a permanent position.

    In December 2014, Town Council hired Johnnie Roark to replace Gillespie as town manager effective January 20, 2015. However, Roark resigned within weeks, and Gillespie returned as interim manager for the remainder of the fiscal year and beyond.

    In February 2016, after five years of continuous service, Gillespie informed the Town Council of his decision to transition out of the interim position. The Town Council began the search for a permanent replacement, and Phillip Gabathuler was named new Town Manager in April 2016, succeeding Gillespie.

    Five-Year Strategic Plan

    One of Gillespie's early priorities upon assuming interim management was developing a comprehensive strategic direction for the town. In 2011, he developed a five-year strategic plan for the Town Council, establishing organizational priorities and long-term objectives for addressing the community's infrastructure and economic development needs. This planning framework guided many of the substantive initiatives undertaken during his tenure.

    Waterline Activation and Infrastructure Development

    One of Gillespie's most significant accomplishments was overseeing the successful activation of the U.S. Route 460 waterline in July 2012. This critical infrastructure project, which had been under development for several years, represented a major expansion of water service availability and was essential to attracting new commercial and industrial development to the town.

    The waterline activation resolved long-standing service capacity constraints that had limited growth potential. Gillespie championed this project through the final phases of development and coordinated the logistics of bringing the system online, positioning Appomattox to compete for economic development opportunities requiring reliable water infrastructure.

    Beyond the waterline activation, Gillespie oversaw additional infrastructure improvements including the Meadowlark Area upgrades completed in 2015, which represented approximately $1.5 million in community facility and park enhancements.

    Boundary Line Adjustment and Growth Strategy

    In 2012, Gillespie led negotiations and analysis on a boundary line adjustment between the Town of Appomattox and surrounding county land. In Town Council presentations, Gillespie advocated that the infrastructure investments the town was making—particularly the activated waterline—made contiguous development more feasible and attractive to potential developers.

    His argument to the Council emphasized that infrastructure improvements and expanded service territory attracted businesses and residents, creating a multiplier effect for the town's long-term revenue base and tax capacity. This strategic perspective informed several subsequent development initiatives during his tenure.

    Economic Development Initiatives

    Recognizing the challenges posed by industrial closures in the region, Gillespie implemented several programs designed to attract new business investment to Appomattox. His economic development strategy addressed the impacts of the Thomasville Furniture closure and the associated job losses and revenue decline.

    Business Tax Incentive Program

    Gillespie developed a business tax incentive program to encourage new companies to locate in Appomattox. This program offered targeted incentives to businesses meeting specified criteria regarding job creation, investment size, and wage levels. The incentive program was part of a broader effort to diversify the town's tax base and economic foundation beyond traditional industrial employment.

    Tourism and Hospitality Development

    In 2015, recognizing the tourism potential of the town's historic resources and strategic location, Gillespie traveled to Minneapolis to study successful small-community hotel and hospitality development models. The research visit informed a feasibility study examining the viability of hospitality-oriented investments that could serve visitors while generating tax revenue and employment.

    This forward-looking initiative reflected Gillespie's understanding that communities like Appomattox needed to develop diverse revenue streams and economic foundations to sustain municipal services and attract new residents and businesses.

    Directional Signage and Visitor Infrastructure

    Gillespie pursued grants and funding to improve visitor orientation and wayfinding in Appomattox. Using a Virginia Indemnification Commission grant, the town implemented directional signage to improve tourist accessibility to downtown businesses and historic sites. These infrastructure investments, while modest in cost, supported the broader tourism strategy.

    Train Depot Renovation

    In 2014, Gillespie led a needs assessment study for renovation of the historic train depot in downtown Appomattox. This facility, which had fallen into disrepair, represented an asset for heritage tourism and downtown revitalization. The study examined restoration costs, potential uses, and funding mechanisms for bringing the structure back into productive use.

    Business District Revitalization

    By 2016, as Gillespie was preparing to transition out of his interim role, he was actively advocating for a business district revitalization project. This initiative represented the culmination of years of groundwork in attracting investment and creating conditions for downtown renewal.

    Industrial Property Monitoring

    Throughout 2015, Gillespie reported to the Town Council on renovations underway at Lindenburg Industries, a major industrial property in the area. As the project progressed, Gillespie later raised concerns about the company's financial condition and viability, demonstrating the kind of ongoing monitoring and analysis he provided to keep the Council informed of significant economic developments.

    Budget and Fiscal Management

    During his tenure, Gillespie managed the town's finances through challenging economic conditions following the national financial crisis of 2008–2009 and the decline of traditional manufacturing employment in the region.

    Tourism Budget Optimization

    In 2011, while maintaining community engagement in tourism promotion, Gillespie restructured the town's tourism budget, reducing expenditures from $230,000 to $97,000—a reduction of approximately 58 percent. This fiscal adjustment was accomplished without raising taxes, reflecting his focus on operational efficiency and careful resource prioritization in tight budget environments.

    Revenue Enhancement Proposals

    Recognizing the fiscal pressures facing the small municipality, Gillespie developed alternative revenue proposals to fund community priorities without broad-based tax increases. In 2014, he proposed a cigarette tax expected to generate approximately $15,000 in annual revenue. Such targeted revenue measures were designed to fund specific priorities while distributing fiscal burden fairly across different revenue sources.

    Wastewater Treatment Plant Upgrades

    A major infrastructure and fiscal priority throughout Gillespie's tenure involved comprehensive upgrades to the town's wastewater treatment system. As the town grew and wastewater quality regulations became more stringent, facility improvements became essential.

    Sequencing Batch Reactor Project

    Gillespie advocated for sewer rate increases to fund a Sequencing Batch Reactor (SBR) wastewater treatment plant upgrade project estimated at approximately $5 million. This state-of-the-art treatment technology promised to improve treatment quality, reduce operational costs, and position the town to meet anticipated future regulatory requirements. Gillespie presented the business case for the rate increases to the Town Council and community, emphasizing the long-term benefits and necessity of the investment.

    USDA Grant Pursuit

    In 2014, Gillespie pursued a USDA grant for wastewater plant closure funding estimated at approximately $3.7 million. This federal funding opportunity represented significant financial assistance for necessary infrastructure investment and reduced the burden on local ratepayers.

    Water Quality Monitoring

    Gillespie regularly monitored and reported to the Town Council on wastewater quality parameters including zinc and copper levels, demonstrating attention to water safety and environmental compliance. His technical reporting kept decision-makers informed of system performance and any emerging operational challenges.

    Departure and Transition

    In January 2016, recognizing that his tenure as interim manager had extended well beyond what either party had initially anticipated, the Town Council began searching for a permanent town manager to replace Gillespie. In February 2016, Gillespie personally informed the Council of his decision to move on after five years of continuous service.

    The transition was marked by widespread community appreciation. In December 2014 (well before his departure), citizens submitted a letter to the Town Council expressing "sincere and unanimous appreciation" for Gillespie's "courtesy, brilliance, and problem-solving abilities" in managing town affairs. This testimonial reflected the respect he had earned during his tenure.

    In April 2016, Phillip Gabathuler was appointed to the permanent position of Town Manager, succeeding Gillespie. To ensure continuity during the transition, Gillespie agreed to remain available as a consultant to the town. His consulting engagement was initially extended through March 2017, providing the new administration with access to his institutional knowledge and expertise on ongoing projects and priorities.

    The contract extension through September 2016 and then through March 2017 allowed Gillespie to provide guidance on major infrastructure projects, economic development initiatives, and governance matters as Gabathuler assumed permanent leadership of the municipality.

    Subsequent Consulting Work

    After departing his interim position in April 2016, Gillespie continued to serve the town in a consulting capacity through March 2017. By November 2020, Gillespie was referenced in town records as "former Town Manager" in reports on the ongoing Wastewater Improvement Project, which had an anticipated timeline of 18 months. The continuity of reference to the wastewater initiative he championed illustrates the lasting impact of his tenure.

    Gillespie's consulting work during this transition period focused on knowledge transfer and guidance on major capital projects, particularly the wastewater treatment facility upgrades that represented the most significant financial commitment undertaken during his tenure as interim manager.
