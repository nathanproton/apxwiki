    James Addison "Jim" Nolen (June 15, 1936 – July 31, 2011) was an American real estate broker, appraiser, agricultural extension agent, banker, and civic leader who lived and worked in Appomattox County, Virginia for more than four decades. A Virginia Tech graduate and U.S. Army veteran, Nolen built a varied career spanning cooperative extension work, commercial banking, and real estate before establishing his own firm, Jim Nolen Real Estate, which he operated from approximately 1980 until his retirement in 2008. He was an active participant in local government affairs—regularly appearing before the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors and the Town of Appomattox Town Council on zoning, taxation, and tourism matters—and served as acting president of the Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce in the early 1980s.[1][2][3]

    Early life and education

    James Addison Nolen was born on June 15, 1936, in Wolf Trap, a community in Halifax County, Virginia.[1] He attended Amelia County High School, graduating in 1953, and then enrolled at Virginia Polytechnic Institute (Virginia Tech) in Blacksburg. At Virginia Tech, Nolen was a member of the Corps of Cadets and played in the Highty-Tighties, the university's regimental marching band. He graduated in 1957.[1][4]

    Nolen later pursued graduate education and in 1969 earned a Master of Education degree from North Carolina State University.[1][5]

    Military service

    Nolen served in the United States Army as a Military Policeman (MP). He also served with Company B of the Virginia National Guard.[1][6]

    Career

    Extension service and Farm Bureau

    Drawing on his Virginia Tech education, Nolen worked as a cooperative extension agent in Buckingham County and Appomattox County, Virginia. He subsequently worked with the Farm Bureau in Cordele, Georgia.[1][7] These early positions gave him firsthand knowledge of agricultural land economics and rural community needs that would inform his later involvement in local policy debates.

    Banking

    Nolen served as a vice president with Central Fidelity Bank, one of the larger regional banking institutions in Virginia at the time.[1][8]

    Real estate and appraisal

    In 1977, Nolen began a career in real estate and appraisal. By approximately 1980, he had opened his own sales firm, Jim Nolen Real Estate, based in Appomattox.[1][9] The business operated under the United Country Real Estate franchise and was variously known as "United Country Jim Nolen Real Estate" and, in earlier years, as "ERA Jim Nolen Association Inc."[10][11]

    In 1999, the Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce recognized United Country Jim Nolen Real Estate as its Business of the Month.[12] In 2003, the firm earned the United Country Diamond Award for outstanding sales production.[13]

    Beyond sales, Nolen was a licensed real estate appraiser whose services were used by Appomattox County government. In 1994, the Board of Supervisors directed that Nolen conduct an appraisal of a property under contract with a citizen named Henry Wright.[14] In 1999, the Board initially tasked Nolen with appraising two potential school sites, though Supervisor Dennis Torrence subsequently noted that Nolen was not qualified for commercial appraisals and the assignment was reassigned.[15][16] Nolen also provided a pro bono property appraisal for the Appomattox Community Park project around 2004.[17]

    Nolen retired from the real estate business in 2008.[1] His son Thomas continued in the field, operating Nolen Real Estate in Appomattox.[18]

    Civic engagement

    Throughout his decades in Appomattox County, Nolen was a frequent and outspoken participant in local government. He appeared before both the Board of Supervisors and the Town Council on numerous occasions, addressing issues ranging from zoning regulations to tax policy to tourism funding. His style was direct, data-driven, and often contrarian—he was willing to take positions opposed by the majority of speakers at public hearings when he believed the evidence supported a different view.

    Chamber of Commerce

    Nolen held the position of acting president of the Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce in 1982. In that capacity, he appeared before the Town Council on multiple occasions to request funding for the printing of community brochures. In April 1982, he asked Council for a $400 donation toward brochure printing costs, which was approved unanimously.[3][19] He returned in August 1982 with a similar request, again receiving unanimous approval.[20]

    Zoning and land use taxation

    Nolen was a regular presence at zoning hearings. In January 1999, he appeared before the Board of Supervisors to speak against proposed changes to the county's zoning ordinance that would have increased minimum lot sizes. Presenting a handout showing small-parcel sales data from 1997 and 1998, Nolen argued that the proposed changes would double the price of residential lots and urged the Board to leave the existing standards in place.[21]

    In 2003, Nolen, along with his son Thomas and daughter Ann Coleman, petitioned to rezone several family-owned parcels on Route 645 from R-1 Residential to A-1 Agricultural. Nolen explained that the rezoning would make the properties more consistent with adjacent land and allow for the placement of manufactured homes. The petition was approved unanimously by the Planning Commission and then by the Board of Supervisors.[22][23][24]

    Nolen's most prominent policy engagement came during the 2007–2008 debate over land use taxation in Appomattox County. In March 2007, he wrote to the Times-Virginian criticizing the state's definition of a farm as inadequate and expressing concern about the uncertainty of the new tax base.[25] At the packed public hearing on June 2, 2008—attended by approximately 200 citizens, nearly all of whom supported land use taxation—Nolen was one of only two speakers to voice opposition. He argued that the five-acre minimum and $1,000 annual revenue threshold to qualify as a farm was far too low, saying it was akin to helping truck drivers by starting with anyone who owns a wheelbarrow. He also contended that homeowners deserved a say in a policy that would shift their tax burden upward.[26][27]

    In a follow-up letter published June 11, 2008, Nolen expanded on his position. While acknowledging that land use taxation probably should have been enacted decades earlier, he argued the Board was proceeding prematurely without adequate study of the fiscal impact on homeowners. Drawing on his appraisal experience, he presented a case study of the Cannon Oaks subdivision near his home, calculating that 54 homes on 95 acres generated approximately $36,600 in annual real estate taxes compared to perhaps $1,540 the same acreage would produce if taxed as woodland. He noted that homeowners were not well represented at the hearing and that the great majority of beneficiaries would be part-time hobby farmers, not those depending on agriculture for their primary income.[28]

    Tourism advocacy

    Despite his skepticism toward certain government spending, Nolen was a consistent advocate for tourism investment in Appomattox. In May 2011, he spoke at a Town Council public hearing on the proposed FY 2012 budget, opposing a $100,000 reduction in the tourism budget. Nolen told Council that Appomattox had a prime product for attracting business and suggested the town and county jointly fund an aggressive tourism promotion effort rather than working separately. He urged cooperation, noting it made no sense for tourism efforts to stop at the corporate limits of the town.[29][30]

    In a subsequent letter to the editor published May 18, 2011, Nolen challenged Councilman Steve Conner's assessment of tourism's economic impact, arguing that Conner had selectively attributed tourism revenue to only a few businesses. Nolen contended that many local businesses benefited from tourism traffic and cited the multiplier effect of tourism wages on the broader local economy. He also raised the concern that, as a property owner living in the county but paying town taxes, decisions about the town budget affected non-resident property owners who had no vote in town elections—a situation he likened to taxation without representation.[30][31]

    Waterline debate

    Nolen weighed in on the contentious town–county waterline dispute in April 2008, writing a sharply worded letter to the Times-Virginian in response to the Town's counteroffer regarding waterline ownership. He criticized the Town for taking five months to submit a proposal he described as simple arrogance, questioning a plan under which the County would pay for a waterline the Town would own while simultaneously extending the town boundaries into the most commercially promising areas. Nolen advocated that if county taxpayers paid for the line, the county should own it and sell water to the Town. He called the situation an example of the need to consolidate the town and county governments into a single entity.[32]

    Community and church life

    Nolen was a charter member of Grace Hills Baptist Church in Appomattox, where he served as a Deacon.[1][33] He was active in numerous civic organizations throughout his life, including the Oakville Ruritan Club, the Appomattox Lions Club, the Appomattox Moose Lodge, the Virginia Tech Alumni Association, the Lynchburg Civil War Roundtable, and the Lynchburg Association of Realtors.[1][34]

    His business, United Country Jim Nolen Real Estate, was a regular financial sponsor of community events including the Appomattox Christmas Parade, the Ann Wilcox Mended Hearts Golf Tournament, the Appomattox Cheerz festival season, the Ember Fest, and the Battle of Lynchburg Civil War Reenactment.[35][36][37][38][39]

    In August 1982, Nolen appeared before the Town Council as a representative of the Chamber of Commerce. In November 1974, he had attended a Council meeting alongside his wife, Paige, to raise concerns about odor from a sewage lagoon, presenting a petition signed by 48 area residents. Council responded by voting to set up a meeting with the State Water Control Board to discuss upgrading the Town's construction grant priority.[40]

    Nolen was also a member of the informal "McDonald's Coalition for a Better Government," a group of regulars at the local McDonald's who published a humorous joint letter to the Times-Virginian in August 2008 declaring that if nominated for vice president of either party, they would not run, and if elected, they would not serve.[41]

    Family

    Nolen married Paige Clark, and the couple were together for fifty years at the time of his death. They had six children: Ann, Martha, Thomas, Misty, Jonas, and Jeff, as well as six grandchildren.[1] Several of his children remained in Appomattox County. His son Thomas continued the family real estate business under the name Nolen Real Estate and was active in community events, sponsoring a Veterans Day cookout for veterans and their families beginning in at least 2019.[18][42] Thomas's wife Misty coached the Appomattox Elementary School "Girls on the Run" team and completed the New York City Marathon in 2021.[43]

    Nolen's sister, Addie Mae Nolen Jordan, predeceased him, passing away on August 20, 2010, at the age of 84. She had been the wife of the late Dr. Wm. R. Jordan of Richmond and was a member of Grace Hills Baptist Church in Appomattox.[44]

    In 1992, the Board of Supervisors appointed Nolen to a citizen committee formed to review the county's Subdivision Ordinance, where he was listed in the capacity of "Developer."[45] In 1995, his name was submitted as a potential member of a Board of Assessors panel from the Southside District.[46] In 1996, he and his daughter Ann Paige Nolen successfully acquired a 0.016-acre parcel of county land adjoining their home in the Town of Appomattox, after Nolen appeared at a public hearing to explain that the tiny parcel was of no use to the County and he wished to incorporate it into his lot. The Board approved the transfer unanimously.[47][48]

    Death and legacy

    James Addison Nolen died on July 31, 2011, at the age of 75. A memorial service was held on August 4, 2011, at Grace Hills Baptist Church, officiated by Rev. Adam Tyler, with a reception following. A private burial took place at Liberty Cemetery in Appomattox.[1][49]

    Nolen's decades of involvement in Appomattox County civic life left a record of sustained engagement with the issues that shaped the community. From his early work as an extension agent through his years in real estate and his persistent participation in public hearings and letters to the editor, he was a figure who bridged the agricultural and business communities of the county. His willingness to take minority positions at public forums—opposing land use taxation at a hearing where he was outnumbered roughly twelve to one, or criticizing both town and county leadership on the waterline dispute—reflected an independent streak that characterized his public involvement.
