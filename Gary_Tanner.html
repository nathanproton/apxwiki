    Biography

    Gary Wayne Tanner is a longtime Appomattox County government official and volunteer fire service leader. He served on the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors for two terms from 2007 to 2015, representing the Wreck Island District. During his tenure, Tanner served as Vice-Chairman (2009, 2012–2013) and Chairman (2010–2011) of the Board. He was instrumental in major county projects including the U.S. 460 waterline construction and primary school renovation. Tanner also served as Fire Chief of the Concord Volunteer Fire Department and later was appointed to the Virginia State Emergency Medical Services Advisory Board.

    Early career and fire service

    Gary Wayne Tanner, born circa 1961, became Fire Chief of the Concord Volunteer Fire Department by the mid-1980s.[1] In his role as fire chief, Tanner was responsible for administrative oversight of the department and supervising new members. The Concord Volunteer Fire Department served an important role in the community, and Tanner would maintain this position throughout his career in county government and beyond.

    Election to Board of Supervisors

    In June 2007, Tanner, then 46 years old, announced his candidacy for the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors in the Wreck Island District.[2] He challenged incumbent Shawn Armbrust, who had held the seat previously. In his announcement, Tanner cited constituent requests and a desire to address the county's tax burden as his primary motivations for running.[3]

    Tanner's platform centered on fiscal conservatism and economic development. He expressed a desire to cap or reduce the county's real estate tax rate, believing that increases negatively impacted residents' budgets, especially elderly citizens on fixed incomes and young couples starting out.[4] He also advocated for conservative, managed growth and attracting new businesses to diversify the tax base and reduce the burden on individual property owners.[5]

    In the November 6, 2007, election, Tanner decisively defeated the incumbent Armbrust with 770 votes to 222, representing a convincing mandate from Wreck Island District voters.[6] Tanner was then welcomed to the Board of Supervisors by county officials in January 2008.[7]

    First term (2007–2011)

    Tanner began his first term on the Board in 2008 and quickly became active in county affairs. In his early months, he was appointed to the Local Government Council.[8] One of his first major initiatives was pursuing adoption of a Land Use ordinance, which would provide tax breaks to farmers and landowners. In April 2008, Tanner pushed hard for the county to adopt a Land Use ordinance by July 2008, hoping to mitigate the impact of recent significant land value reassessments on agricultural property owners.[9]

    A defining issue of Tanner's early tenure was the proposed U.S. 460 waterline project from Concord to Appomattox. The county sought to partner with the Town of Appomattox on the project, which would provide improved fire protection and support economic development. In July 2008, Tanner and fellow Supervisor Sam Carter convinced the Board to wait until September to extend an "olive branch" to the Town Council regarding the waterline, allowing time for negotiation.[10] Later that year, Tanner was appointed to the joint waterline committee that would meet with town representatives to discuss the project.[11]

    In September 2008, Tanner participated in discussions about hiring a Director of Economic Development, a position the county was considering to stimulate business growth.[12] He also advocated for the waterline project and proposed that the county should proceed with the design phase of the waterline even if the town declined to participate, emphasizing the project's importance to the county's future.[13] Tanner and Carter emphasized that the U.S. 460 waterline was necessary for fire protection and future economic growth.[14]

    Leadership positions

    In January 2009, Tanner was unanimously approved as Vice-Chairman of the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors, succeeding Thomas H. Conrad.[15] In this role, Tanner continued to advocate for key priorities, including waterline design initiation and the creation of an Economic Development Director position.[16]

    After serving as Vice-Chairman for one year, Tanner was unanimously elected Chairman of the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors in January 2010, succeeding Thomas Conrad.[17] Tanner expressed gratitude for the position and welcomed the newly elected supervisor to the Board. As Chairman, Tanner oversaw a period of significant fiscal management challenges and major county projects.

    In 2010, under Tanner's leadership as Chairman, the Board achieved a major accomplishment in fiscal responsibility. The Appomattox County Board of Supervisors unanimously agreed to leave the real estate tax rate unchanged at 48 cents per $100 of assessed value and did not increase other local taxes, in response to the challenging economic conditions of the time.[18] Tanner expressed pride in the board's fiscal restraint. Additionally, the county refinanced $14.6 million in debt through a lower interest rate, resulting in savings of approximately $875,000 for taxpayers—an effort led by County Administrator Aileen Ferguson, Assistant County Administrator John Spencer, and Chairman Tanner.[19]

    During his chairmanship, Tanner also advocated for careful conservation of the county's financial reserves. In May 2010, he explained to the Board that a $200,000 allocation to schools had depleted the county's "rainy day fund" by 52 percent, emphasizing the necessity of retaining sufficient funds for county operations.[20]

    As Chairman, Tanner expressed optimism about the waterline project's future prospects. Following the May 2010 Town of Appomattox council and mayoral elections, in which candidates supporting the waterline won, Tanner saw the results as a mandate for cooperation on the proposed waterline project.[21]

    Major initiatives

    Waterline project

    The most significant initiative during Tanner's tenure was the U.S. 460 waterline project. Tanner was instrumental in promoting this infrastructure project, which would provide improved water service and fire protection while supporting economic development. In 2008, Tanner proposed a joint waterline committee with supervisors Sam Carter and County Administrator Aileen Ferguson working alongside the Town of Appomattox representatives.[22]

    As Chairman, Tanner argued that the town's continued rejection of the county's waterline compromise would ultimately cost town taxpayers more. He stated that partnering with the county would provide savings, while moving forward without the town would double costs.[23] In 2010, a committee comprising supervisors Gary Tanner and Sam Carter, along with councilmen Jimmy Mayberry and Timmy Garrett, was tasked with ironing out the final details of the waterline agreement, including provisions for the town's emergency-only use of its well system.[24]

    In December 2010, Chairman Tanner suggested that the financial firm Davenport and Company analyze the waterline project's numbers to determine its financial viability.[25] Waterline bids were advertised and the project moved forward toward implementation.

    Economic development and tax policy

    Tanner was consistently focused on fiscal responsibility and tax policy. He was particularly critical of unfunded mandates from state and federal governments, which he believed forced tax increases on local taxpayers. In March 2010, he acknowledged the difficulty and cost of enforcing a proposed noise ordinance without established decibel levels, demonstrating his pragmatic approach to policy implementation.[26]

    In May 2009, the Board voted 3-2 to recommend a $40.3 million budget and a 10-cent real estate tax increase. Supervisors Sam Carter, Gary Tanner, and Russell Moore voted in favor, while Tom Conrad and Willie Craft voted against.[27] As the county faced financial pressures, Tanner navigated difficult budget discussions, always balancing the need for county services with his commitment to fiscal restraint.

    Tanner continued to advocate for economic development throughout his tenure. In 2014, amid planning for the 150th anniversary of Civil War events scheduled for April 2015, Supervisor Tanner recommended against cutting the economic development budget, recognizing the opportunity such commemorative events presented for business and tourism development.[28]

    Second term (2011–2015)

    In March 2011, Tanner announced his intention to seek a second term on the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors in the Wreck Island District.[29] He cited unfinished business, including completing the waterline project and renovating primary school buildings, as reasons for running again.[30] Tanner also expressed a desire for the county to increase investment in its local education system and highlighted the proactive approach being taken to the primary school renovation project.[31]

    Tanner was re-elected to his second term in 2011. In January 2012, he was unanimously approved as Vice-Chairman of the Board of Supervisors, following the election of Jerry Small as Chairman.[32] During his second Vice-Chairmanship, Tanner continued to serve as a strong voice on county issues.

    In his second term, Tanner continued to advocate for fiscal responsibility. In 2012, Supervisors Willie Craft, Gary Tanner, and Sam Carter voted in favor of a tax increase, citing the need to fund county departments, including the school system, and to address unfunded state mandates.[33] Later that year, Tanner criticized federal and state unfunded mandates, citing the Line of Duty Act, Virginia Retirement System, and the Virginia Secondary Roads proposal as burdens on local governments.[34]

    In February 2012, the debate over dissolving the Social Services Board began, initiated by Supervisor Gary Tanner, who proposed researching methods to dissolve the board due to concerns about its personnel practices and adherence to county policy.[35]

    In 2012, Tanner proposed a joint sewer line extension between the Town and County as an alternative to a boundary line adjustment, suggesting it could improve grant opportunities and benefit all parties without forcing annexation.[36] Later that year, Supervisors Willie Craft, Gary Tanner, and Jerry Small formed a majority to defeat a motion related to the Town's proposal, citing concerns about creating a double-taxation burden on approximately 60 affected citizens.[37]

    In his final years on the Board, Tanner remained a fiscal watchdog. In July 2014, Supervisors Gary Tanner, Sara Carter, Willie Craft, Sam Carter, and Ronnie Spiggle expressed gratitude to county employees for their efforts in saving money, acknowledging that a $700,000 budget surplus was a result of many small cost-saving decisions.[38] Tanner continued his monthly criticisms of state and federal mandates imposed on local governments and taxpayers.[39]

    Education and professional development

    In 2011, Tanner completed the Sorensen Institute for Political Leadership, a prestigious program at the University of Virginia designed to train political leaders. Tanner was accepted into the Institute in Fall 2009 following an application process that included essays and interviews with prominent figures like Shannon Valentine and Elliott Schewel.[40] The Institute accepts approximately 35 applicants annually from across Virginia, and Tanner was the first from Appomattox County to be accepted.[41]

    The 10-part seminar provided hands-on knowledge and focused on topics including ethics, public policy, campaign skills, and bipartisanship.[42] At the Sorensen Institute graduation ceremony in February 2011, Tanner was awarded a "best sense of humor" superlative and delivered a five-minute speech. His wife, daughters, granddaughter, and a neonatal nurse attended to support him.[43]

    Retirement and later service

    In April 2015, Tanner announced that he would not seek re-election to a third term on the Board of Supervisors. The decision marked the end of an eight-year tenure representing the Wreck Island District. Tanner cited a new career opportunity with B&W (Babcock & Wilcox) that required significant travel and time commitment as the reason for his retirement from elected office.[44] At BWXT Nuclear Operations Group in Lynchburg, Tanner worked as a Nuclear Safety Trainer.

    Despite retiring from the Board of Supervisors, Tanner stated plans to remain active in politics by attending meetings and advocating for citizen concerns, particularly regarding the tax rate and unfunded mandates.[45]

    In his statement upon retirement, Tanner highlighted key achievements during his tenure as supervisor, including the waterline project, primary school renovation, convenience centers, recruitment of Lindenburg Industries and the Appomattox Inn and Suites, the county's Civil War Sesquicentennial event, road clean-up crews, resource officers at schools, and improved school funding approaches.[46]

    While serving on the Board of Supervisors, Tanner also held positions on the Virginia Association of Counties (VaCo) Board of Directors and served on Region 2000's Local Government Council, helping coordinate regional initiatives affecting Appomattox County and neighboring jurisdictions.[47]

    In his final months on the Board in 2015, Tanner urged the Board of Supervisors to explore debt refinancing options to prevent a potential three-cent real estate tax increase. Davenport & Company presented four scenarios to the board, some of which would not require a tax hike.[48]

    Following his retirement from the Board, Tanner continued his public service. In 2018, Governor Ralph Northam appointed former Appomattox County Board of Supervisors member Gary Tanner to the State Emergency Medical Services Advisory Board.[49] Tanner, who was surprised by the nomination, accepted the position and was sworn in.[50] The appointment reflected his long dedication to emergency services and public safety through his volunteer fire chief role. He retained this position as of 2021.[51]
