Charles Matthew Fariss (born c. 1968) is a Virginia politician and businessman who served in the Virginia House of Delegates representing the 59th District from 2012 to approximately 2024. A Republican and co-owner of Lynchburg Livestock Market, Fariss represented rural areas of Campbell County and advocated for agricultural interests, gun rights, and traditional conservative values during his tenure. His legislative record included committee service on agriculture, natural resources, public safety, and appropriations matters, with significant contributions to health and human resources subcommittee work. However, his political career was marked by personal legal challenges spanning several decades, culminating in serious criminal charges in 2023 and 2024 that effectively ended his tenure in elected office.

Early life and career

As co-owner, Fariss gained considerable influence in the regional agricultural industry and developed extensive professional relationships with farmers, ranchers, and agricultural service providers throughout south-central Virginia. The livestock market serves as a crucial nexus in the regional agricultural economy, allowing producers to sell commodity animals at market prices and providing buyers access to livestock inventory. These business ventures positioned him as a prominent member of the local agricultural community and gave him considerable influence among the region's farming population.[2]

Fariss's early professional life, however, was complicated by a series of personal legal difficulties that would later become public during his political career. Despite these challenges, he maintained his business interests and developed a reputation within rural Virginia circles as someone deeply invested in agricultural policy and rural economic development. Charges and arrest (2023–2024)

In March 2023, Fariss became involved in a serious incident that would ultimately result in major criminal charges and effectively end his legislative career. On March 2, 2023, Fariss was operating a 2015 Chevrolet Tahoe and was traveling south on Route 501 in Campbell County when the vehicle crossed the centerline of the roadway. Rather than remaining at the scene to render assistance and cooperate with authorities, Fariss fled the accident site, a decision that substantially increased the severity of the criminal liability he would face.[27]

However, Fariss turned himself in to law enforcement authorities the following day, on March 3, 2023. Upon his surrender, he was charged with malicious wounding, felony hit-and-run, and reckless driving—all serious felony and misdemeanor charges reflecting the severity of the accident and his departure from the scene. The malicious wounding charge is a felony serious enough to carry potential incarceration. The hit-and-run charge is also a felony offense. The combination of these charges indicated that Fariss faced substantial potential criminal penalties for his involvement in the incident.[28]

The hit-and-run incident put significant pressure on Fariss's continuation in legislative office. As a sitting delegate with serious felony charges pending, his political standing became untenable, and the incident marked the beginning of the end of his tenure in the House. Colleagues and constituents called for his resignation, though he initially resisted stepping down despite the seriousness of the charges.

Less than a year later, on March 24-25, 2024, Fariss was arrested again and booked into the Blue Ridge Regional Jail at 12:03 a.m. on March 25, 2024. In this arrest, he faced charges including firearm possession while in possession of Schedule I or II controlled drugs, narcotics possession, and violation of a protective order. The combination of the 2023 hit-and-run charges and the 2024 drug and firearm charges effectively ended Fariss's legislative career.
