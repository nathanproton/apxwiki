    Appomattox Youth Sports (AYS), formerly the Appomattox Youth Association, is a volunteer-run nonprofit organization that administers recreational and competitive youth athletics in Appomattox County, Virginia. The organization offers programs in baseball, softball, football, cheerleading, basketball, soccer, and wrestling for children and teenagers generally between the ages of five and eighteen. AYS is affiliated with Dixie Youth Baseball and has sent all-star teams to the Dixie Youth World Series on multiple occasions. As the county's primary youth recreational league, AYS has served hundreds of participants annually—registering 784 children in 2004 and 759 in 2005—and has been a significant force in the development of athletic facilities at the Appomattox County Community Park.[1][2]

    History

    Origins and early years

    Organized youth athletics in Appomattox County trace their roots to the mid-twentieth century. The late Coach Bragg, a locally prominent figure in scholastic athletics, is credited with establishing the Appomattox Youth Association to provide summer recreational activities for the county's young people.[3] In parallel, Ralph Reynolds envisioned and facilitated the creation of a little league baseball field on property belonging to the Woodmen of the World (W.O.W.) fraternal organization in Appomattox, which became a central venue for youth baseball for decades.[4] Girls' softball gained dedicated facilities when the Spout Spring Ruritan Club developed a sports complex with fields specifically for girls' softball, contributing substantial volunteer labor and funding.[5]

    The W.O.W. Ball Park and the Town Ball Park on Court Street served as the primary playing venues through the 1990s. The Dixie Youth Baseball Association and W.O.W. honored the late Fred Weakley in 2002 for his pioneering role in youth baseball participation in Appomattox, underscoring the deep community roots of the program.[6]

    Reorganization and the Youth Association era (1990s–2002)

    By the late 1990s the organization was operating under the name Appomattox Youth Sports, administering football, cheerleading, and baseball programs. A 1997 newspaper account recorded David Creasy presenting a $300 check from Primestar of Lynchburg to AYS Football Commissioner Luke Paulette, one of the earliest documented references to the AYS name and structure.[7] During this period the county's Board of Supervisors contributed $5,000 annually to the youth association and provided access to county facilities including the old elementary school field for football and cheerleading, as well as the Appomattox County Ballpark for the entire baseball season.[8]

    In late 2000, the Sports Complex Committee—a county working group exploring the creation of a recreational park—began organizing volunteers for preparatory work on what would become the Community Park, while the Youth Sports Committee secured office space at the Appomattox Community Center for file storage and registration activities.[9]

    The organization faced a significant challenge in early 2002 when the Virginia State Police Bureau of Criminal Investigation launched a review of the Appomattox Youth Association's financial records. Parents had alleged mismanagement, with claims that the organization had overspent its funds by more than $30,000 over the preceding two years.[10] No public record of criminal charges resulting from the investigation has been identified in the available databases.

    Community Park era (2003–2010)

    The opening years of the 2000s marked a transformative period for AYS, driven largely by the construction of the Appomattox County Community Park on Police Tower Road (State Route 613). In November 2003, grading began on a cloverleaf of four youth baseball fields at the park, with D.S. Nash Construction performing the work. AYS contributed $50,000 toward the grading costs, a substantial investment that demonstrated the organization's commitment to the project.[11] By mid-2004, AYS had donated an additional $8,000 for seeding the cloverleaf fields and provided $50,000 in matching funds to help secure a State of Virginia grant for the park.[12] The Walter J. Payne Foundation awarded AYS a $10,000 grant in 2004 to support the Community Park effort.[13]

    Registration and participation grew during this era. In 2004, AYS registered 784 children at a fee of $35 per child, spending an average of $109.08 per participant. In 2005, 759 children participated, with spending rising to $154.00 per child. Outgoing AYS President Allen B. Smith detailed these figures in a December 2005 letter to the Times-Virginian, noting that the organization was run by a small core of dedicated volunteers serving hundreds of families.[1]

    AYS expanded its programming during this period, offering tee ball, Dixie Youth Baseball (multiple age divisions), Dixie softball, football (ages 5–13), cheerleading, basketball, and fall baseball. The fall baseball league, organized by Commissioner Mike Lawson, operated as an instructional program with age groups from seven through eighteen playing at the W.O.W. field and the Town Ball Park.[14]

    The development of the Community Park continued through the late 2000s. In February 2009, the Board of Supervisors supported AYS's proposal for a two-story concession stand at the center of the cloverleaf, a structure that would include bathrooms and press boxes. Ray Perkins of AYS initially suggested upgrading to a three-story design for better visibility of all four fields, though Supervisor Gary Tanner proposed elevating the grade of the land instead to save the $2,000 cost of redrawing plans.[15] The Spout Spring Ruritan Club, led by project manager James Cheatham, assumed a central fundraising role. A ceremonial groundbreaking in August 2009 drew nearly fifty people, and the concession stand was completed by early 2011.[16][17]

    Facilities expansion (2011–2018)

    The 2010s saw continued investment in Community Park infrastructure, often driven by AYS advocacy. In March 2011, the Board of Supervisors granted AYS exclusive use of the new concession stand for one season under a lease agreement.[18] In September 2012, AYS President Ray Perkins appeared before the Board of Supervisors urging an annual budget allocation for continued facility development, and in October addressed the Town Council requesting a one-time donation for ballfield lighting, estimated at $137,000.[19][20]

    The lighting project gained momentum through community fundraising led by the Spout Spring Ruritan Club and individual donors. By June 2012, more than $12,000 had been collected, with the Ruritan Club donating a substantial portion of the work. In December 2012, the Board of Supervisors voted 5–0 to advance $137,000 for the installation of baseball stadium lights at Community Park, with the understanding that the funds would be repaid through donations. The lights were intended to enable AYS to host a July tournament that would bring additional revenue to the county.[21]

    In 2017, four baseball scoreboards were installed at Community Park. Local businesses Cardinal Chevrolet, Farmer's Bank, and Walmart each sponsored one scoreboard, while a fourth remained unsponsored. Volunteers assisted with the installation.[22]

    Recent years (2019–present)

    AYS continued to evolve through the 2020s, adding new programs and recognition initiatives. In May 2022, the organization launched an "Athlete of the Month" program honoring outstanding current or former AYS athletes. The award was named in honor of Curtis Mayberry, described as a lifetime supporter of AYS.[23]

    The organization suffered a significant loss in August 2023 with the sudden death of Lee Scruggs, who had served as both AYS President and Baseball Commissioner. Scruggs had been instrumental in securing Appomattox's selection to host the 2024 Virginia State DYB Baseball Tournament, having formally requested the event at the annual meeting in February 2023. In November 2023, AYS established the "BOOYAH" award in his honor, presented to children who demonstrate sportsmanship, coachability, and enjoyment of their sport. The first recipients were Colton Jones (baseball), Hunter Curtis (football), and Kenzlee Elliott (cheer).[24][25]

    In the summer of 2024, Appomattox hosted the Virginia State DYB Baseball Tournament at the Community Park fields from July 5 through 10, featuring Minors Division I (ages 9–10) and O-Zone Division I and II (ages 11–12) brackets. The event marked the second time Appomattox had hosted a state tournament, following a successful 2016 tournament organized under then-President Ray Perkins. AYS Baseball Commissioner Bryan Ferguson coordinated volunteers for gate collection, concessions, public address, scorekeeping, and field maintenance.[26]

    As of late 2024, AYS maintained commissioner positions for seven sports—baseball, basketball, cheerleading, football, softball, and wrestling—with Bryan Simpson serving as a contact for election nominations.[27]

    Programs and sports

    Baseball and softball

    Baseball has been AYS's flagship program since the organization's inception. The league operates under the Dixie Youth Baseball (DYB) umbrella and fields teams across multiple age divisions: tee ball (ages 5–6), coach-pitch/machine-pitch (ages 6–8), minors (ages 9–10), ozone (ages 11–12), and pre-majors/majors (ages 13–15). A Dixie Boys division has also been offered for older teenagers. Softball divisions have included the Ponytails, Darlings, Belles, and Angels age groups, also affiliated with Dixie Youth Softball.[14][28]

    Spring baseball and softball registration has typically been held at the Appomattox Community Center in February and March, with a fall instructional baseball league offered from approximately September through November. Registration fees were $35 per child in the mid-2000s and had risen to $70 by 2022.[1][29]

    AYS baseball teams have compiled a distinguished record in postseason tournament play. Appomattox all-star teams have won multiple Virginia state championships and represented the state in the Dixie Youth World Series on at least seven occasions between 2007 and 2021, competing against teams from across the southeastern United States.[30]

    Football and cheerleading

    AYS has operated a youth football program for children ages five through thirteen, with teams divided into varsity and junior varsity divisions. Football sign-ups have traditionally been held in July at the Community Center, with a registration fee of $35 in the mid-2000s and $70 by 2022. Luke Paulette served as AYS Football Commissioner during the late 1990s and continued his involvement for many years, later managing all-star teams in Dixie Youth Baseball as well.[7][29]

    Cheerleading has been part of AYS since at least the mid-2000s, with squads supporting the youth football teams. The program has been administered by a dedicated cheer director or commissioner; Stacy Purcell served in this role by 2012. Registration has typically coincided with football sign-ups.[31] In 2022, the AYS Junior Varsity Football team won its championship, defeating Rustburg 6–0 in overtime.[32]

    Basketball

    The AYS basketball league has operated during the winter months, using school gymnasiums at Appomattox County High School and Appomattox Middle School. The league has organized teams by age group, with a format modeled on college conference names—an "ACC" division for younger players (ages 7–8 or 7–9) and additional divisions for older age groups. Sign-ups have typically been held in late October and November at the Community Center, with registration fees of $40 in 2006.[33] Lee Scruggs coordinated the younger age groups for several seasons and was praised for his dedication to AYS basketball.[34]

    Soccer

    Youth soccer has been offered by AYS, with games played at Courtland Field in downtown Appomattox and on fields at the Community Park. The program has fielded teams in various age divisions, and several AYS soccer players have gone on to represent Appomattox at higher levels. In 2009, eleven-year-old Hannah Foster, an Appomattox U-12 soccer player, was selected to participate in the People to People Sports Ambassadors Program in Vienna, Austria.[35]

    Wrestling

    Wrestling was added to the AYS roster in the summer of 2015, making it one of the organization's newest programs. Coach Lew Longnecker led the program's inaugural season, introducing at least twenty-seven children to the sport. Several wrestlers earned medals at their first competitive event, the Linkhorne Middle School Wrestling Tournament in December 2015.[36] By 2022, the AYS wrestling program was fielding teams in K–2 and 3–5 grade divisions, with multiple wrestlers placing at district tournaments. The team placed fourth overall as a team at the 2022 District Tournament.[37]

    Organization and governance

    Appomattox Youth Sports is governed by an elected executive board and sport-specific commissioners. The executive board includes a president, first and second vice presidents, a secretary, a treasurer, and several members-at-large. Each sport has a dedicated commissioner who oversees league operations, scheduling, and coach selection for that discipline. As of 2024, commissioner positions existed for baseball, basketball, cheerleading, football, softball, and wrestling.[27]

    Elections are held annually each December at the Appomattox Community Center. Voting is restricted to Appomattox County residents aged eighteen and older. Board members and commissioners serve two-year terms. Candidates must submit their names to the organization by a published deadline, typically about one week before the election. Quarterly general membership meetings, open to any parent or guardian of an AYS participant, provide a forum for organizational business between elections.[38][27]

    All board members, commissioners, coaches, and volunteers are required to complete background checks before participating in AYS activities.[29]

    Facilities

    AYS programs have used a network of county-owned and school-owned facilities over the decades. The primary venue has shifted over time from the W.O.W. Ball Park and Town Ball Park to the Appomattox County Community Park, which opened in phases beginning around 2003.

    The Community Park, located on Police Tower Road (State Route 613), features a cloverleaf of four baseball fields that were graded in 2003–2004, seeded in mid-2004, and progressively improved thereafter. A two-story concession stand with bathrooms and press boxes was completed at the center of the cloverleaf in 2010–2011. Stadium lights for two upper fields were installed in 2012–2013 at a cost of approximately $137,000, funded through a combination of county advances, Ruritan Club contributions, and community donations. Four sponsored scoreboards were added in 2017.[11][16][21][22]

    In addition to the Community Park, AYS has used Courtland Field in downtown Appomattox for soccer, the old elementary school field for football and cheerleading practice, and school gymnasiums at Appomattox County High School and Appomattox Middle School for basketball. The Appomattox Community Center has served as the venue for registration, membership meetings, banquets, and elections. The Board of Supervisors has also covered utility costs at the county ballpark, including lights, concession stand operations, and maintenance.[8]

    Awards and recognition programs

    In 2022, AYS launched the Athlete of the Month program, honoring outstanding current or former AYS athletes. The award was named for Curtis Mayberry, described as a lifetime supporter of the organization. Kelsey Hackett, a member of the Appomattox County High School state champion varsity softball team and the AYS Raiders Gold 16U softball team, was named the inaugural Player of the Month for May 2022.[23][49]

    In November 2023, AYS established the BOOYAH award in honor of the late Lee Scruggs. The award recognizes one child in each sport who demonstrates sportsmanship, coachability, and genuine enjoyment of the game. The first recipients were Colton Jones (baseball), Hunter Curtis (football), and Kenzlee Elliott (cheer), with a softball award to follow in December.[24]

    The Lee Scruggs Memorial Golf Tournament, held at Falling River Country Club, was established in 2024 as an annual fundraiser benefiting AYS.[50]

    Fundraising

    AYS has relied on a combination of registration fees, sponsorships, raffles, concession sales, and grant funding to support its programs and facility development. Major fundraising activities over the years have included an annual $10,000 raffle held at the Black Rock Hunt Club, which generated a profit of $12,400 in 2007; Sunday afternoon exhibition softball games with coaches and umpires; benefit events at the Appomattox Friday Cheers concert series; and corporate sponsorships.[51][52]

    AYS has received significant external support, including $10,000 from the Walter J. Payne Foundation in 2004 and sponsorships from local businesses such as Pizza Hut, Huddle House, McDonald's, J.L. Crawford & Sons, and Nationwide Insurance. The Friday Cheers benefit event on May 14, 2004, directed proceeds to AYS.[13][53]

    Controversies and disputes

    2002 financial investigation

    In March 2002, the Virginia State Police Bureau of Criminal Investigation initiated a review of the Appomattox Youth Association's financial records following allegations from parents of gross mismanagement. The complainants alleged that the organization had overspent its funds by more than $30,000 over the preceding two years. The investigation was reported in the Times-Virginian and later referenced in the newspaper's "Looking Back" retrospective column.[10] No public record of criminal charges resulting from the investigation has been identified in the available databases.

    2009 football field dispute

    In August 2009, the Appomattox County School Board voted 4–1 not to allow the AYS youth football league to play on the newly renovated football field at Bragg Stadium at Appomattox County High School. The board cited the need to protect the new grass from excessive use. AYS President Ray Perkins argued for continued access to Bragg Stadium, citing safety concerns and inadequate parking at the alternative venue. School Board member Stacy Garrett cast the lone dissenting vote. The board offered Appomattox Middle School's field as a temporary home and agreed to make the high school field available the following year once the grass had matured. The decision was made in front of approximately twenty children and parents.[54]

    2018 gym usage billing dispute

    In early 2018, tensions arose between AYS and the Appomattox County School Board when the school system sent an invoice of approximately $12,000–$13,000 to AYS for five years of accumulated gymnasium usage fees for its basketball program. AYS President Ray Perkins publicly criticized the school board at its February 2018 meeting, characterizing the bill as unexpected and burdensome to a volunteer organization. The dispute highlighted the sometimes complex relationship between AYS and county institutions over shared facility access.[55][56]

    The county's relationship with AYS regarding funding and facility access also generated periodic debate among supervisors. In 2005, Supervisor Tom Conrad questioned whether AYS should receive the annual $5,000 county donation given that the organization reportedly held substantial funds in its bank account. Supervisor Sammy Carter countered that AYS had contributed approximately $50,000 to the Community Park project, noting the organization's significant investment in county-owned facilities.[57]
