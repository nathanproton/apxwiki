    Aileen T. Ferguson (born c. 1953) is an American local government administrator who served as Appomattox County Administrator across two separate tenures totaling approximately twenty-one years. Ferguson first joined the Appomattox County government in 1974 as a secretarial employee when the county had only three full-time staff members, was promoted to Interim County Administrator around 1990, and was permanently appointed to the position in 1993. After resigning under political pressure in June 2000, she returned to the post by unanimous vote in March 2004 and served until her retirement on June 30, 2015. During her combined decades of service, Ferguson oversaw a period of significant infrastructure growth for the county, including a new courthouse, a new library, a community park, a 911 emergency call center, and the extension of water service along U.S. 460.[1][2][3]

    Early life and education

    Aileen T. Ferguson was born in Appomattox County, Virginia, approximately 1953, the daughter of Charles Torrence and Minerva Torrence.[1] She graduated from Appomattox County High School in the Class of 1971.[2][3]

    Early career in county government (1974–1990)

    Ferguson began working for Appomattox County in 1974, when the county government was far smaller than it would later become. At that time, the county had only three full-time employees, and many major government functions were handled by constitutional officers. Ferguson's first assignment was performing secretarial work for the building inspector, a position created after the county implemented its first building code ordinance.[1]

    As the county government expanded over the following sixteen years, Ferguson took on increasing responsibilities. By the time she was promoted to Interim County Administrator around 1990, the office had grown considerably from its earlier minimal staffing.[1][3]

    First tenure as County Administrator (1990–2000)

    Interim appointment and permanent hiring

    Ferguson was promoted to Interim County Administrator in 1990.[3] In her interim capacity, she served as the administrative liaison between the Board of Supervisors and county departments, presenting agenda items, managing personnel, and coordinating with constitutional officers.

    On October 25, 1993, Supervisor Dennis W. Torrence made a motion, seconded by Supervisor Nina B. Beasley, to hire Ferguson as the permanent County Administrator at an annual salary of $30,000. The vote was not without dissent: Supervisor Frank H. Carwile objected, arguing that the position should be publicly advertised in fairness to all qualified candidates. Carwile stated that while Ferguson had stepped in when needed, the Board owed it to the citizens to conduct an open search. Despite his objection, the motion passed 4–1, with Carwile casting the lone dissenting vote.[4]

    Infrastructure projects of the 1990s

    Ferguson's first tenure coincided with a period of significant county development. Working under Board of Supervisors chairmen including W. E. "Bill" Jamerson, she helped oversee several major projects: the transformation of the county's open dump landfill into a lined cell facility before its eventual closure in favor of a regional solid waste program; the construction and subsequent renovation of the county jail (which later closed when the county joined a regional jail authority); and early planning for the new courthouse complex that would be completed in 2004.[1]

    During this period, Ferguson also served as clerk to the Board of Supervisors, a role she held concurrently with her administrator duties. Board minutes from 1993 through 2000 record her presenting administrative items, coordinating with VDOT on road matters, managing grant applications, and liaising with regional bodies such as the Central Virginia Piedmont District Commission.[4][5][6]

    Resignation in 2000

    In June 2000, Ferguson abruptly resigned as County Administrator. In her resignation letter, she cited strife and conflict in county government. The departure was widely understood as the result of a local political battle in which Ferguson was caught in the middle. The resignation was a significant professional and personal blow; Ferguson later reflected that the experience was one of the most stressful periods of her life and that she felt she was letting her family down by giving in to the pressures of the time.[2][1]

    Andy Carroll was subsequently hired to replace Ferguson as County Administrator.[2]

    Prince Edward County (2000–2004)

    After leaving Appomattox, Ferguson accepted a position as a deputy clerk in the Prince Edward County court system, where she worked for approximately three and a half years. She credited friends Watkins Abbitt and Sam Kerr with helping her find the position. Ferguson later recalled that despite the difficult circumstances of her departure, her time in Prince Edward County allowed her to regain her footing and build new professional friendships.[1][2]

    Second tenure as County Administrator (2004–2015)

    Return to Appomattox

    When Andy Carroll resigned as County Administrator in December 2003, Ferguson was encouraged by former board members and county citizens to apply for her old position. After consulting with her husband Donnie, she submitted an application. On March 4, 2004, the Board of Supervisors unanimously voted to offer Ferguson the job at an annual salary of $55,000. She began work on March 26, 2004.[2]

    Board Chairman Samuel "Sammy" Carter praised the decision, stating that Ferguson's "winning attitude and charisma" were decisive factors and that the board's vote was unanimous. Ferguson described her return in emotional terms, saying she felt like she was "coming home."[2]

    Ferguson was also approved as clerk to the Board of Supervisors in January 2005, a role she continued to hold through her second tenure.[7]

    Budget and fiscal management

    Ferguson played a central role in the county's annual budget process, which she consistently described as the most challenging part of the job. In her first year back, she developed a $27.4 million budget for fiscal year 2004–05 that was more than $1 million less than the previous year, reflecting the county's conservative fiscal approach.[8]

    In 2006, facing a significant budget shortfall, Ferguson proposed raising the personal property tax rate from $3.50 to $4.50 per $100 of assessed value, a measure she estimated would recover over $699,000 in lost revenue. She also highlighted rising fuel costs as a major contributing factor to the county's budget pressures.[9]

    In 2014, during her final full budget cycle, the Board directed Ferguson to identify an additional $539,312 in spending cuts. Despite these challenges, she reported that preliminary figures for 2014 showed county expenses running approximately $700,000 under budget, and she presented four parcels of unused county-owned land valued at $2.5 million for potential sale to return them to the tax rolls.[10]

    Water system development

    Ferguson was deeply involved in the long-running effort to extend public water service in Appomattox County. In 2006, she reported that Draper Aden Associates had been hired in October 2005 to model water rates for a potential purchase from Concord via Campbell County. She served on a joint waterline committee with Supervisors Sam Carter and Gary Tanner, coordinating negotiations between the county and the Town of Appomattox.[11][12]

    The county water system effort culminated in a water purchase contract between the Town and the County Board of Supervisors, which was signed in September 2011. Ferguson confirmed that the contract had been reviewed by the County Attorney before execution.[13]

    Community Park

    Ferguson championed the development of Appomattox Community Park on Police Tower Road. In 2009, she persuaded the Board of Supervisors to allow the Spout Spring Ruritan Club to serve as the lead fundraising entity for the park project. By March 2010, she reported that $170,572 had been spent on the park's development. A two-story concession stand and press box at the center of the park's ballfield complex was targeted for completion by the end of July 2010.[14][15]

    Other projects and initiatives

    During her second tenure, Ferguson oversaw or participated in numerous additional initiatives. These included: the renovation of the Carver-Price Educational Complex, for which the Board authorized her to investigate financing options in March 2006[16]; the conversion of the old elementary school into a CVCC Satellite campus and Board of Supervisors meeting room[1]; the construction of a new animal pound facility[17]; the development of the county's first website, with Assistant County Administrator John Spencer[18]; and the joint hiring of an Erosion and Sediment Control Program inspector shared with Amelia and Nottoway counties.[19]

    She also facilitated county response during emergencies, including the 2008 pipeline explosion, for which she and her staff received praise from the Board of Supervisors for their professional and timely coordination of resources.[20]

    Debt refinancing

    In early 2010, Ferguson led an effort to refinance approximately $14.6 million in county debt. A planned trip to New York City on February 26, 2010, to consult with financial advisors about establishing a county bond rating was canceled due to a snowstorm. Despite this setback, the refinancing was completed at a lower interest rate, saving county taxpayers approximately $875,000.[21]

    Retirement

    Ferguson announced her retirement in September 2014, stating that her final day as County Administrator would be June 30, 2015—the last day of the fiscal year. At the time of her announcement, she was 60 years old and had served more than 36 years in county government, including two separate stints as administrator totaling approximately 21 years.[3]

    In February 2015, the Board of Supervisors unanimously hired Susan M. Adams, formerly deputy county administrator of Charlotte County, as Ferguson's successor at a salary of $100,000 per year. Ferguson expressed confidence in Adams, noting that the salary increase from her own $83,000 brought the position closer to where it should be.[22][1]

    Ferguson's final weeks included two farewell events organized by colleagues and supporters. On July 1, 2015, the Times-Virginian published a retrospective on her career, noting that the county government had grown from three full-time employees when she started to nearly forty by the time she left.[1]

    Community and civic service

    Outside her county government role, Ferguson was active in community organizations. She served as treasurer for the Appomattox County Volunteer Rescue Squad; in October 1997, in that capacity, she accepted a $25,000 donation check from the Spout Spring Ruritan Club.[23] She also accepted a $5,000 donation from Wachovia Bank on behalf of the Rescue Squad.[24]

    Ferguson was a member of the Spout Spring Ruritan Club, where she worked closely with the organization on Community Park fundraising and other community projects.[1] She served as an officer of the Region 2000 Regional Commission alongside Supervisor Samuel Carter and Mayor Ronald Spiggle.[25]

    Upon retirement, Ferguson committed to continuing her service on the VASAP Board of Directors, the Spout Spring Ruritan Club, and the 1865 Historic Appomattox Foundation Board of Directors.[1]

    Personal life

    Ferguson married Donnie Ferguson around 1981. The couple have two daughters: Brandi Ferguson and Alison Ferguson Gobble. As of her 2015 retirement, Ferguson had three grandchildren: Christian Morgan, Jace Morgan, and Jackson Gobble. Both daughters remained residents of Appomattox County. Ferguson and her husband were active in both the Rescue Squad and the Ruritan Club together.[1][2]

    Ferguson has spoken publicly about the importance of her Christian faith, particularly during the difficult period surrounding her 2000 resignation and 2004 return. She described prayer as central to her decision about whether to accept the Board's offer to come back to Appomattox County government.[1]

    Legacy

    Ferguson's career spanned a transformative era in Appomattox County governance. When she entered county service in 1974, the government operated with a minimal staff and relied heavily on constitutional officers for administrative functions. By the time she retired in 2015, the county employed nearly forty people and had constructed or renovated a courthouse, jail, library, community center, elementary school, community park, 911 call center, and animal pound, while also extending public water service along the U.S. 460 corridor.[1]

    Her salary trajectory illustrated the evolving expectations of the position: from $30,000 when first permanently appointed in 1993, to $55,000 upon her return in 2004, to $83,000 at retirement—a figure she acknowledged was among the lowest for county administrators in Virginia. Her successor was hired at $100,000.[1][4][2][22]

    Reflecting on her career, Ferguson credited the various Boards of Supervisors she served under for having the vision to pursue improvements, noting that much of what was accomplished was mandated by the state. She emphasized the importance of regionalism for small localities facing unfunded mandates and expressed pride that Appomattox County had managed to grow while maintaining its small-town character.[1]
