Carver-Price Legacy Museum is a community-operated history museum in Appomattox, Virginia, dedicated to preserving and interpreting the history of African American education and community life in Appomattox County. Located in the restored former Carver-Price Elementary School building on Confederate Boulevard, the museum chronicles the experiences of Black students and educators from the segregation era through integration, with particular emphasis on the school's foundational role in providing quality education to African Americans during an era of legal racial discrimination.[1] The museum also celebrates the pioneering work of Mozella Jordan Price, the African American educator who directed the establishment and growth of Black schools in Appomattox County beginning in 1918.[2] Since its grand opening in 2008, the museum has served as a vital community resource and educational venue, hosting student groups, scholars, and visitors seeking to understand local and regional African American history.

History of the School Building

The building that now houses the Carver-Price Legacy Museum was constructed around 1930 with support from the Julius Rosenwald Fund, a philanthropic initiative that financed the construction of thousands of schools for African American students across the American South during the Jim Crow era.[3] The Rosenwald Fund provided matching grants to communities that contributed their own labor and resources, fostering a collaborative approach to addressing educational inequality. The Appomattox County community successfully mobilized funds and volunteer effort to construct a substantial brick school building with multiple classrooms, supporting facilities, and eventually an auditorium—a significant achievement in a county facing widespread segregation and limited public resources for Black education.[4]

The original facility opened as a school serving African American students in elementary and secondary grades. Over its decades of operation, the building became a centerpiece of Black educational and cultural life in Appomattox County, hosting not only academic instruction but also community gatherings, cultural events, and social activities that reinforced bonds within the African American community during a period of enforced segregation.[5]

Founding and Early Development

The effort to establish high-quality Black schools in Appomattox County originated with the leadership of Mozella Jordan Price, an African American educator appointed in 1918 by Superintendent N. Ragland Featherston as Supervisor of Colored Schools in Appomattox County.[6] In this groundbreaking position—uncommon for an African American woman in the early 20th century—Price oversaw significant improvements to the county's segregated school system. She expanded the school term from seven to nine months, increased the number of schools serving African American students, and secured substantial raises in teachers' salaries, despite the constrained budgets and discriminatory allocation of public funds typical of the era.[7]

Recognizing that public funding was insufficient to meet community needs, Price and local residents raised additional funds through private subscriptions to construct the Appomattox Training School in 1928 and subsequently the larger Carver-Price school building.[8] The school was initially named the George Washington Carver School in honor of the pioneering African American scientist and educator. In 1951, following a significant expansion that added new classrooms, a gymnasium, and a cafeteria, the school was renamed Carver-Price High School in recognition of Mozella Price's extraordinary contributions to African American education in the county.[9]

Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, Carver-Price High School served as the sole secondary institution for African American students in Appomattox County and attracted students from neighboring Prince Edward County.[10] The school employed dedicated educators, many with advanced degrees, and maintained rigorous academic standards despite resource constraints. Notable faculty members included Joseph Lewis, a teacher and coach from 1957 to 1969 who became legendary for his coaching achievements and contributions to health and biology education, eventually earning induction into the Appomattox Scholastic Sports Hall of Fame.[11]

Segregation and Integration

Carver-Price High School's significance in regional African American history extends beyond Appomattox County due to its role in the aftermath of Prince Edward County's school closures. In 1959, rather than comply with court-ordered desegregation, Prince Edward County officials closed the entire public school system for five years (1959–1964).[12] Hundreds of African American students from Prince Edward County—many separated from their families—were able to continue their education at Carver-Price High School in Appomattox, which admitted them despite the additional strain on facilities and resources. Some Prince Edward County students lived with local families; notably, educator Mozella Price opened her home to students without relatives in the area, exemplifying the community solidarity that sustained educational opportunity during this crisis.[13]

The integration of Appomattox County's schools occurred peacefully in February 1970, following Virginia's state-level adoption of a public-private hybrid funding system for education.[14] Carver-Price High School consolidated with the predominantly white Appomattox County High School, ending the de jure segregated education system in the county. The last graduating class of Carver-Price as a separate high school completed their studies in 1969, and by 1970, the building transitioned to serve other educational purposes.[15]

Museum Establishment

The concept for a museum dedicated to preserving Carver-Price School's legacy emerged organically from the school's alumni community. In 2004, during a high school reunion, alumni expressed a strong desire to document and preserve the school's history before institutional memory faded.[16] This grassroots enthusiasm led to the formation of the Carver-Price Alumni Association, which began organizing preservation efforts and collecting artifacts relating to the school's history and impact.

In August 2004, the Carver-Price Alumni Association formally proposed to the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors a lease agreement to establish the Carver-Price Legacy Museum in the front portion of the former elementary school building.[17] County leaders, recognizing the building's historical significance, renamed it the "Carver-Price Educational Complex" to acknowledge both its educational heritage and its emerging role as a multi-purpose community facility.[18] The Board of Supervisors approved the lease proposal, granting the Alumni Association space to develop the museum while supporting a parallel project to renovate other areas of the building for use by Central Virginia Community College.

After several years of fundraising, restoration, and exhibit development, the museum achieved a major milestone. The Carver-Price Legacy Museum officially opened on June 1, 2008, welcoming approximately 80 visitors on opening day.[19] The opening was preceded by a successful pre-opening event in May 2008, generating community awareness and excitement.[20] The official Grand Opening celebration, held later that year, drew approximately 300 attendees and was sponsored by the Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce.[21] The museum received funding support, including grants from the Julius Rosenwald Fund—honoring the organization whose grants had originally helped construct the school building itself—and pursued Community Development Block Grants to support ongoing operations and exhibit expansion.[22]

Collections and Exhibits

The museum's collections focus on documenting the lived experiences of African Americans in Appomattox County from the slavery era through the present day, with particular emphasis on the Carver-Price school years and the integration period. The museum operates several permanent exhibit spaces designed to engage visitors with local history:

Mozella J. Price Office Exhibit: A dedicated exhibit space honors the pioneering educator whose leadership established the foundation for quality Black education in Appomattox County. The exhibit displays Mozella Price's desk, piano, chair, and personal artifacts, along with documentary materials about her career and influence.[23] This exhibit was formally established in December 2023 as part of the museum's expanded programming.

Timeline Room: This permanent exhibit presents a chronological narrative of African American history in Appomattox County from the slavery period through the 21st century. Visual displays, photographs of former students, documents, and contextual text guide visitors through pivotal moments in the county's social and educational history, including the Jim Crow era, the struggle for equal educational resources, school integration, and contemporary community efforts toward reconciliation and historical preservation.[24]

Historical Memorabilia and Artifacts: The museum houses photographs of Carver-Price students and faculty spanning decades; yearbooks and academic records; personal narratives from alumni; materials documenting the Prince Edward County students' experience; athletic trophies and memorabilia from the school's sports programs; and items illustrating daily school life, curriculum, and extracurricular activities.[25] These artifacts provide tactile connection to the past and illuminate the achievements and challenges of multiple generations.

Video Presentation and Community Room: The Dominion Energy Community Room serves both as a programming space and exhibit area, featuring professional video presentations about the school's history and the broader context of African American education during segregation and integration.[26] This multipurpose space accommodates group tours, educational programs, and community events.

Programs and Educational Activities

Since 2008, the Carver-Price Legacy Museum has evolved into a significant educational venue serving students, researchers, and community members. The museum offers formal and informal educational programming designed to connect contemporary generations with local African American history:

School Group Tours: The museum has hosted tours for K-12 student groups, including classes from Appomattox County High School and Cornerstone Christian Academy.[27] Educational tours are structured to meet curriculum objectives, particularly for history, social studies, and civil rights education. Docent-led presentations in the Timeline Room and Mozella J. Price Office help students understand segregation, education equity, integration challenges, and community resilience.

Integration History Program: In October 2014, the museum hosted a significant community program in which Appomattox County High School students heard firsthand accounts of local school integration from five community panelists with direct experience of the desegregation process.[28] This intergenerational dialogue program connected youth with elder community members' lived experiences, enriching understanding of the county's racial history.

Oral History Initiatives: Beginning in 2015, the museum partnered with the National Park Service and folklore producers Michael and Carrie Kline to record oral histories from Carver-Price school alumni.[29] These recorded interviews preserve first-person narratives of school experiences, the segregation and integration era, and the school's cultural and social significance. This archival effort recognizes oral history as a vital means of documenting African American experience that might otherwise remain unrecorded.

Community Celebrations and Fundraising: The museum has hosted numerous community events and cultural celebrations that celebrate African American history and generate operating revenue. Annual Martin Luther King Jr. commemorations feature keynote speakers, community dinners, and programs addressing civil rights topics.[30] Other events have included fish fries, yard sales, and cultural performances, all serving to deepen community engagement and financial support for the museum's mission.

Literacy and Tutoring Services: The museum space has served community members for educational support beyond formal programming. In 2014, retired teacher Lynn Stahling conducted twice-weekly reading tutoring sessions with a local student at the museum, demonstrating the space's flexible use for literacy education aligned with the site's historical educational mission.[31]

The Carver-Price Educational Complex

The building housing the Carver-Price Legacy Museum comprises part of the larger Carver-Price Educational Complex, a multi-tenant facility shared by the museum and other educational institutions. Recognizing the building's potential to serve multiple community needs, Appomattox County worked to develop complementary educational uses that would sustain the facility and advance educational access in the region.

Central Virginia Community College Appomattox Center: In 2005, Central Virginia Community College (CVCC) announced plans to establish a satellite campus within the Carver-Price Educational Complex.[32] The college completed renovations of approximately 4,000 square feet for instructional and administrative spaces and opened its Appomattox Center in January 2007, targeting initial enrollment from both local residents and regional students.[33] The CVCC Appomattox Center offers associate-degree programs, certificates, and continuing education courses in general education, business, and technical fields, extending educational opportunity consistent with the site's historical mission.

By 2012, the CVCC Appomattox Center was offering courses including General Biology, Computer Business Applications, College Composition, World Literature, and supplementary academic services including admissions advising, financial aid counseling, and placement testing.[34] The Center's presence has helped sustain the building and reinforced its role as an educational hub serving the broader Appomattox region.

Auditorium Restoration Project: The Carver-Price Educational Complex contains a historic auditorium built as part of the school's 1951 expansion. Beginning in 2021, a citizens' group undertook a restoration project to convert the auditorium into a dedicated arts center, addressing structural and safety requirements while preserving the building's architectural integrity.[35] Local architect Brian Goldman was selected to oversee initial planning and permitting, signaling the community's commitment to revitalizing the entire complex as a cultural and educational resource.

Community Recognition

The Carver-Price Legacy Museum has received growing recognition at local, state, and regional levels for its contributions to historical preservation and community education. In 2007, the Appomattox County School Board formally donated an original framed photograph of Mozella Price to the museum, acknowledging the institution's role in honoring the pioneering educator's legacy while retaining a copy for the school board's administrative offices.[36]

In 2014, the museum hired Charlene Scruggs as its first paid director.[37] Scruggs, appointed by the Carver-Price Alumni Board and the Legacy Museum Board, has worked to increase museum visibility, expand programming, pursue grant funding for operations and exhibits, and strengthen the museum's capacity to fulfill its educational mission.[38] The museum underwent significant renovations and exhibit updates in 2023, reopening with expanded collections and improved facilities.

Virginia Landmarks Register Listing: In December 2023, the Carver-Price Legacy Museum received state-level historical recognition when the Commonwealth of Virginia's Board of Historic Resources officially added the building to the Virginia Landmarks Register.[39] The Landmarks Register is the commonwealth's official catalog of places possessing historic, architectural, archaeological, and cultural significance. The inclusion acknowledges the building's importance as a Rosenwald School and as a symbol of African American educational achievement during segregation, and recognizes the museum's contemporary contribution to preserving and interpreting that history.

Community Coalition and Regional Cooperation: The museum has increasingly positioned itself as part of a broader coalition of historical and cultural institutions in the Appomattox region. In 2024, the Carver-Price Legacy Museum participated alongside the American Civil War Museum, Patrick Henry's Red Hill, and Thomas Jefferson's Poplar Forest in the American Civil War Museum's Fall Festival, collaborating on joint promotional efforts and community programming.[40]
