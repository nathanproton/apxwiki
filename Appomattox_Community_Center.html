    The Appomattox Community Center is a county-owned multi-use public facility located at 220 Community Lane in the Town of Appomattox, Virginia, off Route 460 across from Town Hall Park. Opened c. 1990, the $550,000 building covers 6,000 square feet on a 7.7-acre parcel and serves as a central hub for county government meetings, youth athletics registration, senior services, emergency sheltering, and a wide variety of civic and community events.[1] Since its opening, the center has hosted hundreds of public gatherings each year, from Appomattox Youth Sports sign-ups and Board of Supervisors meetings to disaster-relief operations during the 2016 tornado and political candidate forums.

    History

    Construction and opening

    The Appomattox Community Center was constructed in the late 1980s and opened for full operation c. 1990. The facility was built on a 7.7-acre parcel off Route 460, across from Town Hall Park, at a cost of approximately $550,000.[1] The building was one of several major county infrastructure projects completed during the tenure of County Administrator Aileen Ferguson, who was promoted to that position in 1990 and later cited the community center among the county's most significant capital accomplishments alongside the new courthouse, library, and 911 center.[2]

    The first community activity held at the center was a Halloween party attended by an estimated 400 to 500 people, signaling the facility's immediate popularity as a gathering place.[1]

    Early governance and use policies

    The center was governed in its early years by a Board of Directors that established policies for facility use. Ora McCoy served as chairperson of the board in the early 1990s.[3] In July 1992, the Board of Supervisors addressed one of the first policy questions involving the center when Supervisor Frank Carwile reported that the Sentinels were considering conducting bingo games there for fundraising purposes. The supervisors voted unanimously to disallow bingo at the Community Center, and later voted to prohibit all gambling on county property.[4]

    The Board of Directors articulated a guiding principle that all non-governmental groups should have equal opportunity for Community Center use and that no special consideration should be given or exceptions made based on a group's purpose. In a letter to the Board of Supervisors, McCoy noted that the Board of Directors supported the goals of the local Youth Association but could not exclude them when establishing guidelines for facility access.[3]

    In 1991, the county entered into a lease agreement with the Central Virginia Area Agency on Aging (later renamed the Central Virginia Alliance for Community Living) at a rate of $275 per month, granting the agency use of the center's kitchen, office, and meeting space for senior services programming. At some point thereafter, the Board of Supervisors voted to waive the monthly fee.[5]

    The Community Center was assigned budget line item 8108 under the county's Community Development category, appearing in the county's general fund appropriations from at least the 1992–93 fiscal year onward.[6]

    Facility and operations

    Building and grounds

    The building encompasses 6,000 square feet and includes a main hall, kitchen facilities, office space, restrooms, and meeting areas. Fire regulations limit occupancy to 300 persons.[7] The 7.7-acre grounds include parking areas. A county staff person is on duty during all rental events to open and close the building and assist with needs.[7]

    Normal operating hours are 8:00 a.m. to 4:30 p.m., Monday through Friday. The facility may be rented for functions on weekdays and weekends up until 11:00 p.m.[7]

    Rental policies

    As of 2008, the rental charge was $20.00 per hour, inclusive of set-up time, rental time, and clean-up time. Payment was due seven days prior to the event, with a $25 cancellation fee for rentals cancelled within seven days. A $50 clean-up fee was assessed if the building was not left clean after use. The renter assumed full liability for any damages to the facility and county property.[7]

    Facility rules prohibited smoking, alcoholic beverages, gambling, amplified sound audible beyond 50 feet, pets, wall or door decorations, and running inside the building. Tables and chairs were not to be dragged across the floors, and all trash was required to be placed in designated receptacles and taken to the dumpster outside the kitchen.[7]

    In March 2018, the Board of Supervisors approved an updated fee policy for the Community Center following extensive discussion.[8]

    Use as a government meeting venue

    The Community Center has served as one of the primary meeting venues for the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors since at least the mid-2000s. In 2007, the supervisors met on the first and third Mondays of each month at 6:30 p.m. at the Community Center.[9] Beginning in 2008, under newly elected Chairman Willie Craft, the Board adopted an alternating schedule, meeting at the County Administrator's office on first Mondays and the Community Center on third Mondays.[10]

    The arrangement prompted debate. Supervisor Samuel Carter argued in January 2009 that all Board meetings should be held at the Community Center to eliminate confusion among citizens and to ensure adequate seating capacity for large crowds. Carter noted that the County Administrator's building could seat only about 25 people, while the Community Center could accommodate far more. Supervisors Craft, Conrad, Moore, and Tanner disagreed, noting that transporting materials and preparing the Community Center required up to two days of staff time. The Board voted 4–1 to continue the alternating arrangement.[11]

    The center has also hosted numerous public hearings on major county issues, including a June 2008 hearing on a proposed Land Use ordinance,[12] a May 2009 hearing on the proposed $40.3 million county budget and a 10-cent real estate tax increase,[13] and budget hearings in subsequent years. Contentious public meetings held at the center include a February 2005 gathering of the Appomattox Citizens Coalition, where approximately 80 to 100 residents turned out to voice opposition to the proposed U.S. 460 waterline project, the supervisors' restrictions on citizen comments, and the Carver-Price Legacy Museum lease.[14]

    Congressional representatives have also used the center for constituent events. In August 2009, Congressman Tom Perriello held a constituent meeting at the center as part of his "Tom In Your Town" series.[15] In October 2008, Williams pipeline representatives held a public meeting at the center to update citizens on repairs following a natural gas pipeline rupture.[16]

    Youth sports and recreation

    The Community Center has served as the primary registration site for Appomattox Youth Sports (AYS) since the early 2000s and likely from the facility's opening. Youth sports sign-ups—for baseball, softball, tee-ball, coach-pitch, football, cheerleading, basketball, and soccer—are held at the center multiple times per year, typically on designated evenings and Saturday mornings. Registration fees increased from $35 per player in the mid-2000s to $40 by 2007 and $50 by 2019.[17][18][19]

    The AYS Board of Directors has held its general meetings and annual elections at the Community Center. Commissioner and board member elections were conducted there in December of each year, open to the public.[20][21] The county's Recreation Advisory Committee, established by the Board of Supervisors in the early 1990s, also held its quarterly meetings at the center on the first Thursday of January, April, July, and October at 7:00 p.m.[22]

    Senior services

    Since 1991, the Community Center has served as a site for senior services programming operated by the Central Virginia Area Agency on Aging, later renamed the Central Virginia Alliance for Community Living (CVACL). The agency utilized the center's kitchen, office space, and meeting room to provide hot meals, classes, and social activities for area seniors.[5]

    In April 2015, the Board of Supervisors voted unanimously to eliminate the county's $5,768 annual contribution to the CVACL and to impose a monthly rental fee of $250 for the agency's continued use of the Community Center. Supervisor Sara Carter made the motion, seconded by Gary Tanner.[23] CVACL Executive Director Debra Silverman appeared before the Board in August 2015, warning that if the rental charge was not reversed, the agency might have to relocate and potentially transport seniors to Lynchburg for services.[24]

    At the September 2015 meeting, Silverman returned and offered to contribute $50 per month or $600 annually toward utility expenses, presenting updated information on the services CVACL provided to county residents. The Board and staff had met with Silverman to discuss several rental options, and the Board ultimately expressed continued support for the agency's presence at the center while working out a revised arrangement.[25]

    As of late 2024, senior activities continued at the Community Center. Site manager Shirley Wheeler voiced concerns at the December 2024 Board of Supervisors meeting about maintaining adequate space for senior programming if the Department of Social Services were to remain permanently at the facility.[26]

    Emergency and disaster use

    2009 snowstorm

    In early March 2009, a 10-inch snowfall caused widespread power outages across Appomattox County. The Board of Supervisors opened the Community Center as an emergency shelter. Chairman Tom Conrad and Supervisor Willie Craft determined on the morning of March 2 to open the facility, and county staff shoveled snow around the sidewalks for pedestrian access. A social services worker staffed the building from 8:00 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. the first day, followed by Director of Parks, Recreation and Tourism Anne Dixon the next morning.[27]

    The shelter drew public criticism when Joe and Linda Coflin of Appomattox wrote a letter to the Times-Virginian reporting that an elderly couple had gone to the Community Center on Monday evening only to find it empty and locked. Conrad responded that staff had posted a notice on the door directing anyone seeking shelter to call the 911 Center, where arrangements would be made.[27]

    2016 tornado

    Following the EF-3 tornado that struck Appomattox County on February 24, 2016, the Community Center served as one of the primary relief sites. The American Red Cross set up operations at the center on the night of the tornado, providing support to displaced residents. The center and the Pamplin Fire Department served as shelters for those whose homes were destroyed. Non-perishable food donations were collected at the center for distribution to affected families.[28]

    In June 2016, the U.S. Small Business Administration established a Disaster Loan Outreach Center at the Community Center, where representatives assisted residents and business owners in applying for low-interest disaster loans. The center at 220 Community Lane operated from June 14 through at least June 18.[29] Later that month, the National Weather Service hosted a public meeting at the center where Warning Coordination Meteorologist Phil Hysell presented details about the tornado and offered severe weather preparedness advice.[30]

    Civic and community events

    The Community Center has hosted a diverse range of civic, cultural, and political events over its three-plus decades of operation. Notable examples include:

    • In November 1997, Mable and Alex Cyrus of Concord celebrated their 25th wedding anniversary with a reception at the center.[31]
    • In October 2008, the Appomattox Railroad Festival invited the public to a planning meeting at the center.[32]
    • In March 2015, the Company of Strangers Theater presented the drama Twelve Angry Men at the center over four performances, with a suggested donation of $5.[33]
    • In April 2016, the Appomattox 1865 Foundation hosted a Civil War Ball at the center, featuring period music and refreshments.[34]
    • In May 2024, the Appomattox County Republican Committee hosted a U.S. Senate candidate forum at the center, where candidates Jonathan Emord and Scott Parkinson addressed topics for approximately two and a half hours before a local audience.[35]

    Church groups and nonprofits have also used the facility regularly. Mount Comfort United Methodist Church held its annual Spaghetti Dinner and Live Auction at the center, with proceeds benefiting a Guatemala mission trip.[36] The Marines' Toys for Tots program has used the center for logistics support during holiday distribution campaigns.[37] Wine festival advance tickets have been sold at the center, and blood drives organized by the American Red Cross have taken place there as well.[38]

    Recent developments

    In late 2024, the Appomattox County Department of Social Services (DSS) temporarily relocated its offices to the Community Center on Community Lane after the agency's longtime Court Street offices sustained water damage requiring renovation. Interim DSS Director Shannon Reed, who began duties during Thanksgiving week 2024, described the center as the department's current base of operations while the county evaluated options for a permanent DSS location.[39]

    At the December 16, 2024 Board of Supervisors meeting, County Maintenance Supervisor Jeff Shepherd reported that the Court Street offices lacked adequate space for confidential caseworker meetings and filing cabinets, raising questions about whether DSS should remain at the Community Center permanently. CVACL site manager Shirley Wheeler expressed concerns about the impact on senior programming if DSS continued to occupy space at the center. The topic was scheduled for further discussion at the Board's organizational meeting on January 2, 2025.[26]
