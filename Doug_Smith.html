    Doug Smith is an American high school football coach who served as head coach of the Appomattox County High School Raiders from the 2012 season through the 2023 season, transforming the program into one of the most successful small-school football dynasties in Virginia. During his twelve-year tenure, the Raiders won five Virginia High School League (VHSL) Class 2A state championships—in 2015, 2016, 2017, 2019, and 2020/21—amassing 118 victories at Appomattox to become the second-winningest head coach in the school's history. He was named VHSL Group 2A Coach of the Year three consecutive times (2015–2017) and in 2016 was recognized as one of two national winners of the U.S. Cellular Most Valuable Coach award. Smith stepped down from full-time coaching in February 2024 due to a recurring battle with multiple myeloma, a blood cancer with which he had first been diagnosed in late 2020.[1][2][3]

    Background and local ties

    Smith has deep personal connections to Appomattox that predate his coaching career there. When he was announced as the Raiders' new head coach in April 2012, the Times-Virginian described him as "a man with strong local ties to the community."[4] Smith himself spoke publicly about what drew him to the area: "I love Appomattox. It's where I found my sweetheart. My church is here. I feel like I am at home."[5]

    Smith is the father-in-law of Sam Hunt, the NASCAR driver and team owner who founded Sam Hunt Racing. In September 2022, Sam Hunt Racing honored Smith at Texas Motor Speedway as part of a national cancer awareness initiative, featuring his name on driver Jeffrey Earnhardt's race car.[6]

    Early coaching career

    Smith began his high school head coaching career in 2009 at Heritage High School in Lynchburg, Virginia, where he compiled 13 victories over three seasons through 2011.[7] When ACHS head coach Ben Martin departed for E.C. Glass High School in early 2012—after a four-year tenure at Appomattox that produced a 32–13 record and a pair of Dogwood District championships—Smith applied for the opening.[8]

    Arrival at Appomattox (2012)

    Smith was hired as Appomattox's varsity head football coach ahead of the 2012 season, with his appointment announced in the April 11, 2012 edition of the Times-Virginian.[4] He immediately set about establishing a program culture. In the summer of 2012 he launched a free youth football camp for ages 6–13, held at Bragg Stadium, and organized the first annual Raider Football Picnic at Fairview Christian Youth Camp during the opening week of fall practices.[9][10] That August, Smith was invited to speak at Mt. Airy Baptist Church's anti-bullying program—an early signal of the community engagement role he would play throughout his tenure.[11]

    Building the program (2012–2014)

    Smith's first Appomattox team opened the 2012 season 0–2 before rallying to give him his first win, a 32–8 defeat of Cumberland, followed immediately by a 50–6 rout of Randolph-Henry. Running back Kendall Blankenship rushed for 238 yards on just 11 carries in that second win, but Smith remained measured: "We're still making mistakes, and we haven't taken care of the ball at all this year. When we do, it will show. The kids are young and have a lot more growing and learning to do. But our coaches are working extremely hard and loving what they're seeing with these kids."[12]

    By 2014, in his third season, the Raiders broke through to the playoffs for the first time under Smith. The team went 7–5, won a first-round home playoff game at Bragg Stadium, and fell in the second round to Giles, that year's eventual state champion. It was the foundation for what came next.[13]

    The championship dynasty (2015–2019)

    2015: The first title and "Power of One"

    The 2015 Raiders went a perfect 15–0, the first undefeated state championship season in the program's modern history. Smith introduced the "Power of One" team mantra that year—a philosophy of collective unity that became the rallying identity not just of the football team but of the broader Appomattox community for years afterward.[14]

    The playoff run included one of the most memorable games in ACHS history: a comeback from a 21–7 deficit to defeat Richlands in the regional playoffs, a result local observers described as among the greatest high school football contests they had seen. The Raiders then routed Buffalo Gap 51–6 in the state semifinal before defeating Clarke County 42–6 in the Group 2A State Championship Game on December 12, 2015, at Salem Stadium in Salem, Virginia.[15][16] Smith was lifted onto his players' shoulders after the final whistle. "I'm so happy for our kids and our community," he said.[17]

    On December 14, 2015, the Appomattox Town Council passed a proclamation declaring December 14 as "2015 Appomattox Raider Football Team Day — Power of One" in honor of the championship squad.[18] Smith was named VHSL Group 2A Coach of the Year for 2015.[19]

    2016: Back-to-back

    Smith rebuilt the 2016 squad after losing 21 seniors from the championship roster and led the Raiders to a second consecutive 15–0 season. The year opened with a 57–6 Battle for the Axe victory over rival Buckingham County. On December 10, 2016, Appomattox defeated Richlands 42–7 in the Group 2A State Championship Game at Salem Stadium, extending the team's consecutive-victory run to 30 games across two perfect seasons.[20][21]

    The 2016 season produced national recognition for Smith. He was named one of two national winners of the U.S. Cellular Most Valuable Coach award, sharing the honor with Allan Trimble of Jenks High School in Oklahoma. The award came with a $5,000 donation to ACHS and a trip to the Under Armour All-America High School Football Game in Orlando, Florida, broadcast on ESPN.[22][23] He also received his second consecutive VHSL Group 2A Coach of the Year award.[24] In April 2016, Smith and the championship Raiders served as Grand Marshals of the Historic Appomattox Railroad Festival parade.[25]

    2017: Three in a row

    A third consecutive state title in 2017 cemented Appomattox's status as a dynasty. The Raiders opened with a 63–14 Battle for the Axe win over Buckingham and finished 9–1 in the regular season. Their playoff run included a 45–4 opening-round win over Giles, a 26–14 Regional 2C Championship victory over Glenvar, and a 23–20 Final Four win over Union before facing Robert E. Lee in the December 10, 2017 state championship at Salem. The Raiders won 38–34 in a close game that pushed their overall winning streak to 32 consecutive games and their championship-era record to 44 wins in 45 games.[26][27][28]

    Smith earned a third consecutive VHSL Group 2A Coach of the Year award—a rare distinction at any level of coaching.[19] The Appomattox County Board of Supervisors passed formal resolutions honoring both the 2016 and 2017 championships, each citing the "Power of One" by name and commending "the players, coaches and others involved with upholding the character of student athletics while showcasing to the world the ultimate Power of One."[30][31]

    Star defensive back Da'Von Graves, the 2016 Group 2A Defensive Player of the Year, departed after his senior season in 2017 and enrolled at North Carolina State University in spring 2018, one of several college-level players Smith developed during the dynasty years.[29]

    2018: A pause

    The 2018 season saw the Raiders remain a strong playoff team but fall short of Salem. Both losses that year came against Heritage High School—Smith's former school—at City Stadium in Lynchburg.[32]

    2019: A fourth championship

    After an uncertain start—opening 1–2—the 2019 Raiders rallied to finish 12–2 and returned to Salem for their fourth championship game in five years. They defeated Stuarts Draft to claim the Group 2A title, Smith's fourth state championship as head coach at ACHS.[33][34]

    Cancer diagnosis and fifth title (2020–2021)

    In late 2020, Smith was diagnosed with multiple myeloma, a cancer of plasma cells in the bone marrow. News of his diagnosis prompted an immediate and sustained community response. Supporters launched a "Smith Strong" t-shirt campaign in November 2020, selling shirts for $5 each; a beef stew fundraiser followed in December; and additional benefit events—including a Moose Lodge fundraiser and the Smith Strong golf tournament—were held into 2021.[35][36][37][38] Smith received radiation treatment and underwent a stem-cell transplant at Duke University Medical Center.[39]

    The fall 2020 football season had been postponed to spring 2021 because of the COVID-19 pandemic. Because of his ongoing treatment, Smith was unable to coach during that delayed season. Defensive coordinator Stephen Castello served as interim head coach. Under Castello, the Raiders completed a perfect season and defeated Stuarts Draft 48–41 in the Group 2A State Championship Game, delivering the program's fifth state title and one that, by convention, is counted among Smith's tenure at the school.[40][41]

    Return and final seasons (2021–2023)

    Smith returned to the ACHS sideline for the first home game of the 2021 season on August 20, 2021, when the Raiders hosted Rustburg in the "Battle for the Lantern" and won 37–20. It was his first appearance on the Bragg Stadium sideline since 2019, and drew considerable local attention. On the same evening, Smith was inducted into the Appomattox Scholastic Sports Hall of Fame alongside seven other honorees. Later that October, he received encouraging news when a biopsy returned negative results.[42][43][44] During that same 2021 season, Smith reached his 100th career win at ACHS, a 56–18 victory over Gretna on Hall of Fame Night.[45]

    On August 3, 2021, the Monroe Masonic Lodge No. 301 presented Smith, his coaching staff, the Raiders football team, and the team's parents with the Community Builder Award from the Grand Lodge of Virginia, A.F.& A.M., recognizing their outstanding service to the community. District Deputy Grand Master Ken Fredrick of the 25th Virginia Masonic District and Worshipful Master Wayne Cyrus presented the award.[56]

    The 2022 season produced another milestone: on October 26, at Chatham, Smith recorded his 125th career coaching victory and the Raiders extended their Dogwood District win streak to 50 consecutive games.[46]

    The 2023 season proved to be Smith's most difficult at ACHS. The Raiders fell to 2–5 at midseason before rebounding to win their final four regular-season games, including a 57–18 Senior and Military Appreciation Night victory over Dan River. They entered the playoffs before their season ended. It was Smith's final year as full-time head coach.[47][48]

    Resignation (2024)

    On February 20, 2024, Smith gathered the Raiders football team in the weight room to announce his resignation as head coach, with family members present. He cited his "ongoing bout with cancer"—including a new development involving lymph nodes in his chest—as the reason for stepping down after twelve seasons. He expressed his intention to remain involved with the program in a limited capacity, including the weight room and fundraising. At the time of his departure, his record at ACHS stood at 118 wins, second all-time at the school behind Gordon Bragg (144 wins, 1948–1969); his combined career record at Heritage and Appomattox totaled 131 wins.[1][49][50]

    On April 26, 2024, the school named Stephen Castello, who had served as Smith's defensive coordinator for all twelve of his ACHS seasons, as the new permanent head coach.[51]

    Coaching philosophy

    Smith built the Raiders program around a faith-based, family-oriented framework that placed character development alongside athletic achievement. The "Power of One" philosophy he introduced in 2015 described a culture in which individual accomplishment was subordinated to collective purpose, and in which the football program was understood as an extension of Appomattox County community life. Community chants of "We are One!" became a fixture at Raiders games during the dynasty years, and an estimated 5,000 fans traveled to each of the Salem championship games.[14][52]

    Smith placed strong emphasis on academics and community service. His "Raider of the Year" award criteria, established in 2014, evaluated players on weight training accomplishments, fundraising participation, service projects, academic performance, and character. His teams participated in community service efforts including cleanup work following the 2016 Appomattox tornado.[53][54] He ran a free annual youth football camp for children as young as five or six from his very first year at ACHS, and organized team camps at university facilities including Appalachian State University.[9]

    In August 2018, Mayor Paul D. Harvey and the Appomattox Town Council honored Smith with a special presentation at a regular council meeting, with Harvey praising his success in building a "football juggernaut" in the community. Smith, characteristically, departed the ceremony promptly to return to football practice.[55]

    Notable players

    Smith's program produced several players who went on to play college football. The most heralded was Da'Von Graves, a 6’2”, 185-pound defensive back who was named the 2016 Group 2A Defensive Player of the Year. Carrying a 3.8 GPA and capable of bench-pressing 275 pounds with a 4.53 40-yard dash, Graves received a football scholarship to North Carolina State University as a sophomore in 2016 and enrolled at NC State in spring 2018 following an early graduation.[29]

    Other All-State honorees during the dynasty years included linebacker Buster Henderson and defensive back Deven Dews (both First Team All-State, 2015); and offensive linemen Drake Cocks and Xzavier Morgan, defensive back Delonta Kelso, punter Carter Jamieson, and punt returner Tre Walker (all First Team All-State, 2016). Quarterback Tre Lawing was a key contributor across multiple championship seasons, and running back Cristian Ferguson was prominent in the 2018–2019 period.[57][58][59]

    Legacy

    Smith's twelve seasons at Appomattox represent the most successful stretch of football in the school's history. His five state championships, including three consecutive (2015–2017), placed the Raiders among the elite programs in Virginia Class 2A competition and brought the school national attention. The 32-game winning streak from 2015 to 2017, the 44 wins in 45 games over that three-year span, and the 50 consecutive Dogwood District victories accumulated by 2022 stand as statistical markers of a program operating at an extraordinary level of consistency for over a decade.

    The "Power of One" philosophy became woven into Appomattox County public life in a way rarely achieved by a high school athletic program: it appeared in Board of Supervisors resolutions, Town Council proclamations, t-shirts and merchandise, stadium chants, and community events. When Smith was unable to coach the 2020/21 championship team due to cancer treatment, the community response—fundraisers, benefit concerts, "Smith Strong" merchandise, a NASCAR tribute at a national race venue—illustrated the depth of the bonds his program had built over a decade.[60]

    His successor, Stephen Castello, spent all twelve of his ACHS seasons as Smith's defensive coordinator before being named permanent head coach in April 2024, carrying forward the program Smith had built.
