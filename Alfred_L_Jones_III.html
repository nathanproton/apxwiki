    Alfred L. "Al" Jones III (born c. 1957) is an American minister, educator, local historian, and public official who has represented the Piney Mountain District on the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors since January 2022. Before his election to the Board of Supervisors, Jones served seven years on the Appomattox County School Board (2014–2021) as the Piney Mountain District's elected representative. A lifelong resident of Appomattox County, Jones is also the founder and senior pastor of Jesus Center Ministries in Appomattox and spent his professional career teaching social studies at Appomattox County High School. He is widely known as a local historian of African American Civil War history, a body of research he has presented at the American Civil War Museum in Appomattox and shared through guided county tours.

    Early life and background

    Alfred L. Jones III was born c. 1957 in Appomattox County, Virginia, where he has lived throughout his life. Little has been reported in the public record about his early years or formal education, but by 2004 he was working simultaneously as a social studies teacher at Appomattox County High School and as the senior pastor and founder of Jesus Center Ministries in Appomattox.[1] In a 2019 interview, he described himself as "a minister and retired social studies teacher."[2]

    Ministry and teaching career

    Jesus Center Ministries

    Jones founded Jesus Center Ministries in Appomattox, an independent evangelical congregation of which he served as senior pastor for more than four decades. In 2022 he noted from the Board of Supervisors floor that he had "pastored for 40 years" in the community — a statement consistent with the ministry having been established in the early 1980s.[3] The church held a 62-acre site in Appomattox and operated buses for youth transportation, though Jones reported in 2004 that the vehicles "seem to stay in the shop more than on the road."[1]

    Jones officiated at funerals, delivered revival sermons at other congregations, and was a guest speaker at churches throughout the region. In July 2014 he served as evangelist for Beautiful Zion Baptist Church's annual revival week, and in subsequent years was a guest at New Beginnings Ministry and Mt. Lyle Baptist Church revivals.[4] By 2022, press accounts described him as a "retired minister," though he continued to be referred to by the title "Reverend" in official Board of Supervisors proceedings.[5]

    Mission Empowerment Group

    In 1990, Jones established the Mission Empowerment Group — also known as the First Youth Christian Church — as a community outreach arm of Jesus Center Ministries focused on at-risk youth and families in Appomattox and the surrounding region.[1] Jones described its mission as offering "Christian-based community service such as after-school programs, summer Christian day camps, and enrichment programs for youth," with the overarching goal of helping young people "come to know Jesus Christ" and "build strong families."[1]

    The Mission Empowerment Group ran a summer swimming program serving 60 to 80 children annually, offered Friday night basketball programs in the spring, and hosted youth classes on Christian values, education, alcohol and drug avoidance, and AIDS prevention.[1] Jones hoped to expand the program across the region, including into Lynchburg, Amherst, Buckingham, Nelson, Charlotte, and Campbell Counties, and aimed eventually to recruit "a thousand people in the region" as volunteers and supporters.[1]

    A 1996 account published in the Times-Virginian's "Looking Back" column described the Mission Empowerment Group as a "community service organization" and noted it had operated a House of Refuge, a halfway house for men in addiction recovery.[6]

    Teaching career

    Jones worked as a Social Studies teacher at Appomattox County High School. The 2004 Times-Virginian profile describes him as holding this position concurrently with his pastoral duties, noting that he devoted considerable personal time beyond school hours to the Mission Empowerment Group.[1] By the time Jones began his public career in local government, he was widely referred to as a "retired" teacher, suggesting his departure from the classroom occurred sometime in the late 2000s or early 2010s.[2]

    During his tenure on both the School Board and the Board of Supervisors, Jones frequently invoked his decades of educational experience. When a colleague questioned whether his prior School Board service would make him biased toward the school division, Jones responded from the supervisors' floor: "I have worked in this community for over 40 years helping the youth of this County. I have been heavily vested in helping the youth even before I was on the School Board."[7]

    Appomattox County School Board (2014–2021)

    Service and positions

    Jones was elected to represent the Piney Mountain District on the Appomattox County School Board, taking office on January 1, 2014.[8] He served with colleagues including Chairman Bobby Waddell, Vice-Chairman Greg Smith, and members Wyatt Torrence, Wes Williams, and Cindy Hall, under the superintendency of Dorinda Grasty and later Dr. Annette Bennett.[9]

    In January 2015, Jones was appointed as the School Board's representative to the Appomattox County Educational Foundation Scholarship Committee, a position to which he was re-appointed in 2016 and again in 2019 when he was listed as "Rev. Al Jones III, representing the Piney Mountain District."[10] He also served on the joint School Board–Board of Supervisors committees that periodically reviewed school funding requests and capital improvement priorities.[11]

    In February 2018, Appomattox County Public Schools publicly recognized Jones along with fellow board members for their "dedication and leadership" to the school division.[9]

    Budget and funding advocacy

    During his time on the School Board, Jones was an active voice on questions of school finance and teacher compensation. In November 2014 he spoke in favor of teacher pay increases at a board meeting, encouraging community members to attend Board of Supervisors meetings to rally support for school funding.[12] The following year, Jones and fellow board member Wyatt Torrence expressed concern that demonstrating financial efficiency might paradoxically lead the Board of Supervisors to reduce future funding allocations, emphasizing "the need for continued county support and better communication between the School Board and Board of Supervisors."[13]

    In July 2014, when the School Board considered matching the Board of Supervisors' 20-percent pay reduction, Jones voted against cutting School Board compensation — not out of self-interest, he explained, but because such a reduction "sends the wrong message" about the value of public service. "I think it would have been better if we were included in this decision," said fellow board member Wes Williams, a sentiment Jones shared.[14] The board ultimately voted unanimously to reduce compensation when full attendance allowed a clear majority, with Jones joining the unanimous vote despite his earlier reservations.[15]

    In April 2015, Jones argued at a joint board meeting that reduced county funding for the schools disproportionately harmed at-risk students, and he publicly supported an efficiency study funded by the Board of Supervisors as a path toward more equitable resource allocation.[16]

    Resignation

    On April 29, 2021, Jones submitted his resignation from the Appomattox County School Board at that evening's regular monthly business meeting, effective immediately. His current term had been set to expire on December 31, 2021. Jones cited two factors: the strain of the COVID-19 pandemic and the death of his wife, Judy Faye Davis Jones, on December 17, 2020, after her battle with cancer; they had been married for 40 years.[8]

    The School Board appointed Bobby Waddell — who had previously served 15 years as the Falling Creek District representative and resigned from that seat in November 2020 after relocating outside the district — as the interim Piney Mountain representative.[17] A special election was subsequently scheduled to fill the Piney Mountain School Board seat coinciding with the regular November 2021 elections.[18]

    Appomattox County Board of Supervisors (2022–present)

    Election and early tenure

    Jones ran for the Piney Mountain District seat on the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors in the November 2021 general election, running unopposed. The seat had been held for a single term by Watkins M. Abbitt Jr., who chose not to seek re-election following his own single term in office (Abbitt had himself served 26 years in the Virginia House of Delegates before his supervisor term).[19]

    Jones took office on January 1, 2022. At the January 3, 2022 organizational meeting of the Board of Supervisors, he was sworn in alongside his colleagues and the board voted to elect William Hogan as Vice-Chairman for the 2022 term.[20] At that same organizational meeting, Jones had previously been appointed — effective January 1, 2022 — to the Board's 2021 Redistricting Committee, a body tasked with reviewing and recommending district boundary adjustments following the 2020 Census.[21]

    He joined a board composed of Chairman Samuel E. Carter (Courthouse District), William H. Hogan (Appomattox River District), John F. Hinkle (Falling River District), and Trevor L. Hipps (Wreck Island District), with County Administrator Susan M. Adams and County Attorney Tom Lacheney serving the board.[20]

    Library board controversy (2023)

    In June 2023, Jones voted with a 3–2 majority of the Board of Supervisors to remove the three remaining members of the J. Robert Jamerson Memorial Library Board of Trustees. The motion followed public comment from citizens objecting to LGBTQ-themed children's books and graphic novels in the Young Adult section of the library. County Attorney Tom Lacheney advised that removal could proceed under state law on grounds of misconduct or neglect of duty.[22]

    At the July 2023 board meeting, Jones attempted to reverse part of that decision. He made a motion to reinstate Joetricia Humbles, the Piney Mountain District appointee who had been removed and not reinstated, a move that would have required displacing the newly appointed replacement. While no second was made on the motion, Jones used the occasion to explain his changed position. He acknowledged that his vote the previous month had been "poor governance," taken too hastily without adequate investigation. Upon further research, he said, he had found a Virginia state code exemption that permits public libraries to distribute materials that would otherwise be restricted under general obscenity statutes.[23]

    "I believe both he and the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors acted hastily in voting for the library board's removal in June without having further investigation and discussion before taking a final vote at a later date."[23]

    Jones was careful to note that his personal moral conviction remained unchanged — that explicit materials should not be accessible to minors in a public library — but that Virginia law as written did not support the board's removal action. The board subsequently directed County Attorney Lacheney to draft a resolution expanding the library board, creating room for Humbles to potentially be reinstated without removing an existing member. In September 2023, Jones nominated Madeline Abbitt to represent the Piney Mountain District on the library board after Nancy Jo Billings resigned due to work schedule conflicts; the nomination was approved 3–2.[24]

    Transparency and governance (2024)

    In February 2024, following the dismissal of Bobby Wingfield, Appomattox County's long-serving director of public safety, Jones spoke extensively at the regular board meeting criticizing the process by which the personnel decision had been made. Six citizens had already voiced opposition in public comment. Jones, serving as Vice-Chairman, called for greater procedural transparency, demanding that significant decisions be preceded by full board deliberation rather than being presented for an on-the-spot vote.

    "I felt like the decision had already been made, and as a board member no one contacted me, nobody had a conversation with me. I want to know how we're going to operate…I don't want to come to a board meeting, and out of nowhere I find out that a long-term employee is gonna be terminated, and that decision has to be made on the night of the meeting."[25]

    Jones also defended County Administrator Susan Adams, who had been publicly blamed for the Wingfield dismissal. He argued that it was "unfair for Miss Adams to take the fall" for a decision that rested with the board, and he called for roll call votes on personnel matters "so that the public ought to know what people's positions are."[25] Addressing Chairman John Hinkle, he invoked the "Appomattox way" of inclusive local governance: "I don't know much about politics, I pastored for 40 years but I believe in this community."[25]

    Earlier in 2024, Jones was one of two supervisors — along with Samuel Carter — to vote in favor of continued county funding for the American Civil War Museum at Appomattox, when three colleagues voted to cut the museum's $15,000 annual allocation from the fiscal year 2025 budget. Bill Jamerson, chairman of the ACWM fundraising committee, subsequently appeared before the board to ask for reconsideration.[26]

    Vice-chairmanship

    At the January 2, 2024 organizational meeting of the Board of Supervisors, Jones was elected Vice-Chairman for the 2024–2025 period by unanimous vote of the full board.[27] In this capacity he presided over meetings in the chairman's absence, conducted roll call votes, and was formally addressed as "Rev. Jones" in official minutes. He remained in the vice-chair role as of the board's regular scheduled meetings through mid-2024.[28]

    African American history research and public education

    Alongside his ministerial and governmental roles, Jones has pursued an extensive program of original historical research focused on African Americans in Appomattox County before, during, and after the Civil War. He described himself to the Times-Virginian in 2019 as a "minister and retired social studies teacher" who was actively researching and writing a book about "the stories of African American men, women, and children during the Civil War and emancipation period."[2]

    In December 2015 he presented a talk titled "The Civil War Amendments" — covering the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments — at the Museum of the Confederacy–Appomattox.[29] In February 2018 he spoke to students at Appomattox Middle School for Black History Month, discussing slavery, the Civil War, and stories from local African American history, including Pink McKinney, a freed slave who returned to Appomattox with his former owner, and Millie Craig, a woman who saved her owner's baby during the Battle of Appomattox Court House.[30]

    In early 2021, Jones designed and curated an exhibit of portraits and biographical accounts of African Americans who held roles "in both the background and foreground of American history from 1865 to the civil rights era," which was displayed at the American Civil War Museum in Appomattox. Press accounts described him as the founder of the Appomattox Black Civil War Legacy Museum.[31] In April 2021, he participated as a panelist in a virtual discussion titled "Legacies of Appomattox," co-hosted by the National Historical Park and the American Civil War Museum, alongside historian Patrick Schroeder and exhibits curator Chris Graham.[32]

    Jones also worked to secure placement of a portrait of Honorable James William D. Bland — a notable African American figure in local history — in the Appomattox County Courthouse, and planned to feature Bland's story in his forthcoming book.[33]

    By 2024, Jones had developed a guided tour program titled "Appomattox Tours in Black-White-Blue & Gray," through which he shared his research on African American Civil War history with visitors and community members. The tours covered "the history, genealogy, and impact of former slaves and their ancestors in Appomattox county just before, during and after the Civil War."[34] A fellow supervisor described Jones in a 2024 column as "a man who loves history" and noted that "our county is the better for it because Rev. Jones loves to share what he finds."[34]

    In April 2023, Jones presented a talk titled "Neither Slave, Nor Free: Voices of the Almost Emancipated of Appomattox" at the American Civil War Museum's annual Commemoration of the Surrender & Freedom Day events.[35]

    Other community service

    Jones officiated at memorial services and other events across the county, including community vigils and public ceremonies. In October 2022, he offered prayers at the annual Domestic Violence Awareness Month candlelight vigil at Courtland Park in Appomattox, co-officiated with Appomattox County Sheriff's Office Deputy Josh Davis.[5]

    Jones participated in the first Juneteenth celebration held in Appomattox, which took place at Abbitt Park on Main Street in June 2020. The event, organized by Appomattox for Equality, also featured Dr. Cameron Webb, a candidate for Virginia's 5th Congressional District seat, and author Ken Woodley.[36]

    Personal life

    Jones was married to Judy Faye Davis Jones for 40 years. She died on December 17, 2020, after a battle with cancer. Jones cited her death as one of two primary reasons for his resignation from the Appomattox County School Board in April 2021, the other being the strains of the COVID-19 pandemic.[8]

    Jones has lived in the Piney Mountain District of Appomattox County throughout his adult life. He has been an active community presence for decades, balancing pastoral leadership, classroom teaching, historical research, and elected public service simultaneously.
