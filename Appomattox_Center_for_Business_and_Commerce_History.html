Background and Origins

The effort to develop a dedicated industrial and commercial land park in Appomattox County, Virginia, did not begin in the late 1990s. According to reporting in the Appomattox Times-Virginian, an Industrial Development Authority (IDA) for Appomattox County was originally formed in the early 1970s, during a period of federal and state-level enthusiasm for rural industrial development incentives. The post-World War II era had seen repeated state efforts to attract manufacturing to Virginia's Southside and Piedmont regions—areas that lacked the interstate highway infrastructure of the I-81 and I-95 corridors but possessed relatively cheap land, willing labor, and proximity to mid-Atlantic markets.

Appomattox County's early IDA, however, was disbanded at some point in the 1980s or early 1990s, a period of significant transition for Virginia's rural manufacturing base. The rise of global competition, particularly from lower-cost Asian manufacturers in the furniture and textile industries that had long anchored Southside Virginia's economy, created widespread plant closures and consolidations throughout the region during the late 1980s and into the early 1990s recession. Appomattox itself remained anchored economically by the Thomasville Furniture plant (a division of the publicly traded Furniture Brands International), which had opened in October 1974 under the "Founders Furniture" brand name and had become the county's single largest private employer, at peak employing over 1,000 workers.

The broader Southside Virginia region during this period came to depend heavily on two industries—furniture manufacturing and textiles—even as national trends indicated long-term structural decline in both. County leaders and state officials recognized the vulnerability this created.

Reconstitution of the IDA and Land Acquisition (1997–2000)

In 1997, the Appomattox County Industrial Development Authority was resurrected, coinciding with the appointment of county administrator Darrell Carroll, who would go on to serve until 2003. Carroll's tenure was characterized by fiscal conservatism—he maintained the real estate tax rate and grew reserve funds—while simultaneously pursuing infrastructure investments that would position the county for economic diversification.

The macro-economic backdrop of the late 1990s was propitious for such ambitions. The national economy was in the midst of the longest peacetime expansion in American history. Low unemployment and strong consumer spending buoyed manufacturing orders, and states and localities across the South competed aggressively for industrial recruits. Virginia's Governor's Office and the Virginia Economic Development Partnership (VEDP) actively marketed the Commonwealth's rural localities as low-cost alternatives to increasingly congested Northern Virginia and the Hampton Roads corridor.

In April 1998, the reconstituted IDA took its pivotal first step: signing an option agreement with G.D. Gillman to purchase a large tract of land in central Appomattox County. The parcel was located along State Route 26, north of the town of Appomattox. This particular corridor had been identified as suitable for industrial use because it was located away from established residential communities, had reasonably flat topography, and sat adjacent to existing utility corridors.

The land acquisition was not without controversy even at its inception. A public hearing in 1998 reportedly drew at least six residents who opposed the proposed tax increase tied to the purchase, with some believing that the tax hike was imposed solely to fund the land buy. Citizens questioned the wisdom of the expenditure and the county's ability to attract industry to what many saw as an underserved rural location without adequate water supply, interstate access, or a deep labor pool.

Nonetheless, in February 2000, the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors broke ground on the industrial park, having finalized the purchase of approximately 580 acres for $1.4 million. The park was located on State Route 26 and would later be officially christened the Appomattox Center for Business and Commerce (ACBC). The initial phase of development included the installation of water and sewer lines, a gravity pump, and grading of approximately 36 acres.

Early Development and Macro-Context (2000–2004)

The years immediately following the groundbreaking proved difficult for the park's development. The national economy entered recession in 2001, triggered in part by the collapse of the dot-com bubble and deepened by the September 11, 2001 attacks. Corporate capital expenditure, plant expansion, and business relocation activity slowed sharply. This macro-level contraction coincided with accelerating structural decline in the manufacturing sector—particularly furniture—as Chinese imports flooded the American market following China's admission to the World Trade Organization in 2001.

Thomasville Furniture, the anchor of Appomattox's private-sector employment base, underwent internal reorganizations during this period. By December 2000, its Appomattox division, then operating as "Founders Furniture," had already been issuing layoffs. The chamber of commerce faced financial difficulties. These pressures underscored the urgency of economic diversification but also made the county's limited resources harder to allocate.

A major practical barrier quickly emerged: water supply. Appomattox County's water came primarily from the Town of Appomattox's well system, which relied on locally recharged fracture zones in bedrock—not from large aquifers—making the supply both limited in volume and vulnerable to drought. IDA chairman Bill Slagle would later explain that these wells could not reliably produce the 50 gallons per minute that state health and industrial standards typically required for an industrial water customer. When the 2002 drought struck Virginia—severe enough to prompt then-Governor Mark Warner to issue an executive order mandating that localities develop alternative water supply plans—the park's water inadequacy became an acute obstacle rather than a latent one.

In late 2003, community concerns surfaced in the local press about the water supply for the industrial park, with articles noting that the drought conditions highlighted the county's vulnerability and its effect on the park's ability to attract businesses. County Administrator Carroll's tenure was reviewed in December 2003 with mixed assessment—he had overseen the industrial park's creation alongside other projects, but the park remained without any confirmed tenant.

The IDA launched its website, www.appomattox.org, in September 2003 as a marketing tool to attract businesses through online searches—a forward-looking move for a rural Virginia locality at the time.

First Tenants, Public Controversy, and Defining the Park's Identity (2005)

By early 2005, the Appomattox Center for Business and Commerce had been in development for five years without a single confirmed industrial tenant. IDA Chairman Bill Slagle reported to the county supervisors in February 2005 that the authority was "still awaiting confirmation" from an industry that had chosen to locate there. On a more optimistic note, the same month brought an announcement that Martin Printwear, a screen-printing operation that had previously been located within the Town of Appomattox itself, had relocated to the ACBC, becoming the park's first business.

The arrival of Martin Printwear—a relocation rather than a recruitment—drew pointed criticism. A September 2005 opinion piece argued that the addition of "two existing businesses, a concrete plant and Martin Printwear," to the county industrial park represented no net gain for taxpayers, since neither represented new investment nor new jobs to the region. This criticism captured a genuine tension in rural economic development: the difficulty of distinguishing between business recruitment (bringing genuinely new activity) and business relocation (simply moving existing activity within the county).

The second tenant controversy arose with Lynchburg Ready-Mix, owned by Bobby O'Brian. O'Brian stated publicly that his company had previously attempted to locate a concrete plant at the industrial park on State Route 26 but had been denied, without being given specific reasons. IDA Chairman Slagle disputed this characterization, saying discussions had been preliminary. The dispute generated significant coverage and public debate, with critics accusing the IDA of operating in secrecy and being inhospitable to business. After an extended process—a joint public hearing, a 10-0 recommendation from the county Planning Commission, and final approval from the Board of Supervisors—Lynchburg Ready-Mix was granted a conditional use permit to operate a ready-mix concrete plant in the park in July 2005.

In a broader statement of purpose, the IDA published a formal articulation of criteria for the ACBC in April 2005, emphasizing compliance with zoning, protective covenants, and a projected return on investment. The authority also acknowledged that it had collaborated with organizers to stage three Civil War reenactments at the park, recognizing tourism as a component of the economic strategy—particularly fitting for a county whose most famous landmark was the site of Lee's surrender to Grant in April 1865. In April 2005, a Civil War reenactment ("The Final Campaign in Virginia: Pursuit To Appomattox") drew approximately 10,000 visitors and 3,000 reenactors to the park, confirming the venue's viability as a large-scale event space.

Perhaps most significantly, in April 2005, the IDA announced that it was investigating a name change to "Economic Development Authority", following legislation passed in Virginia's 2004 General Assembly session that authorized localities to reconstitute their industrial authorities under the broader EDA designation. The IDA also formalized its decision to dedicate 100 acres of the 585-acre total to a Community Recreational Park, leaving the remaining acreage available for commercial and industrial use.

Renaming, the Water Crisis, and the Broadband Hub (2005–2007)

In June 2006, Appomattox County formally amended its code to rename the Industrial Development Authority to the Economic Development Authority (EDA). The change, though largely semantic, reflected both the statutory opportunity offered by the 2004 General Assembly legislation and a philosophical evolution: the authority was no longer purely focused on attracting manufacturing, but on promoting broader economic development, including retail, commercial, and service-sector activity.

The name change drew at least one skeptical response in the local press, with resident Betty Siano calling it a "shell game" and suggesting the old industrial park could be more usefully converted into an outlet center—something that would create jobs without heavy water usage.

That reference to water consumption was prescient. Throughout 2005, 2006, and 2007, the water supply question became the central political controversy in Appomattox County's economic development debate. The EDA's preferred solution was a waterline from Campbell County, routed along U.S. 460, at an estimated cost of $4.7 million—far less expensive than a reservoir, which Slagle estimated would cost $15 million. By June 2006, EDA Chairman Slagle was stating publicly that over a dozen prospective businesses had disqualified Appomattox County specifically because it could not meet their water requirements. He noted that pharmaceutical companies and food processors—both significant targets for rural industrial recruitment—require reliable, large-volume water supplies that Appomattox's fractured-rock wells simply could not provide.

The Farm Bureau reported that 85-90% of its 1,100 members opposed the proposed waterline. The county Planning Commission chairman suggested drilling wells at the industrial park as an interim step. Citizens packed meetings throughout 2006 and 2007 to debate the project. The Town of Appomattox, which controlled the existing well system, initially declined to join the waterline project, concerned about ownership, cost-sharing, and the implications for its customer base. The controversy was further complicated by deteriorating relationships between Town and County leadership over revenue-sharing and governance of any future public service authority.

Against this backdrop of local controversy, the ACBC received an unexpected national distinction in September 2005: Governor Mark Warner attended a groundbreaking ceremony at the park for the Southside Regional Broadband Initiative, operated by the Mid-Atlantic Broadband Cooperative (MBC). Warner described the Appomattox site as "the major connection hub" for what he called one of the most ambitious broadband projects in the nation—a 700-mile fiber optic network funded by $21 million from the Virginia Tobacco Commission and $6 million from the U.S. Economic Development Administration. The project ultimately aimed to connect 700,000 Virginians and 19,000 businesses across five cities, 20 counties, and 56 industrial parks. For the first time, the ACBC had something that differentiated it from competing sites: redundant, high-speed fiber connectivity, a selling point particularly relevant for data-intensive industries.

By 2007 and 2008, the EDA was actively marketing this broadband infrastructure alongside the park's federal designation as a Hub Zone (making it attractive to federal contractors and defense-related businesses). The Virginia Economic Development Partnership met with the EDA regarding potential Homeland Security-related activities, which could range from a Defense Department facility to a data processing center. The EDA redrew its master plan to accommodate an 800,000-square-foot building footprint in the lower portion of the park—a dramatic scaling-up from the original 40,000-60,000-square-foot shell building concept—and submitted the updated map to VEDP for marketing.

The Great Recession and Renewed Urgency (2007–2010)

The VEDP's formal assessment of Appomattox's strengths and weaknesses, presented to the EDA in January 2008, was candid. Regional strengths included: available open land, a community college, a cooperative attitude, higher education access, and existing industry centers. Appomattox-specific strengths included its Hub Zone status, its status as a Tobacco Commission county (eligible for grants), and its broadband and natural gas access. Weaknesses were equally direct: insufficient water capacity and source, public controversies (a reference to the highly publicized local debates), and a lack of qualified employees for technical positions.

This assessment came at a critical inflection point. In September 2008, a Williams Transco natural gas pipeline running through central Appomattox County—along the same Route 26 corridor as the industrial park—ruptured and exploded, destroying two homes, damaging approximately 90 others, injuring five people, and triggering mandatory evacuations of some 800 residents including schoolchildren. The explosion, later attributed to external corrosion and inspection testing errors, registered as a small seismic event on Virginia Tech instruments. Williams Pipeline was ultimately fined $952,500 by the U.S. Department of Transportation. The disaster was a reminder of the county's infrastructure vulnerabilities and the complex interdependencies involved in industrial development—including the same natural gas infrastructure that was a selling point for industrial recruitment.

Three months after the pipeline explosion, Thomasville Furniture dealt Appomattox its most significant economic blow in decades. In December 2008, Furniture Brands International announced that the Appomattox plant would eliminate 29 percent of its workforce—approximately 140 employees—as part of a company-wide reduction of 1,400 workers, attributed explicitly to the "recessionary environment." The plant's payroll, which had peaked at over 1,000 workers and still stood at about 600 as recently as 2006, fell to approximately 350 workers. The third shift was eliminated entirely.

The timing was devastating in multiple ways. Nationally, the 2008-2009 financial crisis and Great Recession had produced the worst economic contraction since the 1930s. Consumer spending on discretionary goods—including furniture—collapsed. Credit markets froze, making it nearly impossible for small businesses to relocate or expand. The ACBC, with no utility-ready pads, no shell building, no reliable water supply, and no dedicated marketing professional, was simply not equipped to compete for the mobile capital that remained.

Former county supervisor Nina Beasley articulated the situation clearly in a January 2009 public address, calling for three interdependent actions: marketing the industrial park, securing a water source via the Campbell County waterline, and hiring a full-time Economic Development Director. She specifically cited the Thomasville layoffs as intensifying the urgency.

Also in early 2009, the county sought federal stimulus funds (under the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act) for the waterline project, and the EDA began formal planning for a $700,000 expandable shell building of 40,000-60,000 square feet, plus $208,000 in water and sewer upgrades, a $1.25 million Route 460 entrance road, and a natural gas tap at the park.

In October 2009, the ACBC welcomed its third tenant: Penelope Inc., a company that agreed to purchase two acres for $50,000 to construct facilities for its corporate offices, warehousing, and web store.

Post-Recession Reorganization and Hiring a Director (2009–2012)

The debate over hiring a dedicated Economic Development Director consumed county politics from 2008 through 2011 in a pattern that became almost circular: the EDA would endorse the hire; the Board of Supervisors would allocate funds, interview candidates, then pull back under state budget pressure; and the EDA would publicly criticize the reversal. In March 2010, the EDA delivered a pointed critique to the Board after the latter voted 4-1 to abandon funding for the position, even after having interviewed candidates and allocated $84,000 for the role, citing uncertainties in state budget numbers.

In June 2009, however, amid the nadir of the recession, the county had briefly succeeded in hiring a full-time Economic Development Director, marketing the county's broadband connectivity, the pending waterline, the community college campus, and the workforce as its selling points. This effort was short-lived due to budget pressures, and by March 2010, the position was again unfunded.

The November 2010 announcement that Thomasville Furniture would permanently close its Appomattox facility in March 2011—eliminating roughly 200 remaining jobs and ending a 37-year run as the county's largest employer—injected new urgency into the conversation. Town Council member Mary Lou Spiggle explicitly linked the need for a director to Thomasville's closure at a November 2010 meeting. County Supervisor Gary Tanner assured the EDA the position would be "prioritized in the budget."

Simultaneously, the EDA voted 6-0 in December 2010 to send a delegation to Wisconsin to court Ashley Furniture—whose corporate headquarters were there—hoping to attract the company to the now-vacant Thomasville plant. The visit was framed as a face-to-face appeal to Ashley's president about the company's goals and a possible Appomattox facility.

By late 2010 and into 2011, the county appropriated $105,000 for the Economic Development Director position and approved a job description. The EDA began advertising for the role in January 2011.

In 2012, the EDA completed a $5.7 million bond sale to generate revenue for park development over several subsequent years and commissioned two studies on industry-specific strengths and challenges for Appomattox County. The park also saw upgrades including new gates, concrete barriers, and surveillance cameras, overseen by Economic Development Director Jeff Taylor, who simultaneously worked with the Virginia Film group to promote Appomattox as a filming location. Consultants visited the park during this period exploring it for data center and light manufacturing potential—echoing the data center conversations that had emerged during the homeland security discussions of 2008.

By late 2012, the Town of Appomattox formally proposed extending its sewer system down Police Tower Road to the industrial park, at an estimated cost of $2.3 million, to be borne by the Town.

Maturation, Civil War Tourism, and Continued Marketing (2012–2015)

Through 2013 and 2014, the ACBC began to see modest gains in institutional recognition and strategic planning. In 2013, the county established an economic development strategic plan through the EDA, focused on expanding the tax base, creating economic stability, and growing jobs. The Town of Appomattox, for its part, created its own EDA in October 2013 (distinct from the county's EDA), signaling an increasing awareness that both jurisdictions needed dedicated economic development infrastructure. The town's EDA launched a performance agreement system tying tax incentives to job creation.

Goals set by county officials in late 2014 and early 2015 included: installing a gas line tap or substation at the ACBC, relocating the school maintenance shop and bus shop to the park, and completing waterline and sewer connections. These were modest but concrete objectives—indicative of a county that had learned, through hard experience, to set achievable incremental targets.

The park's role as a Civil War reenactment venue continued to generate public visibility and modest tourism revenue. The "Long Road Home" reenactment series, organized by the Appomattox County Historical Society, staged events at the ACBC in 2014 as a lead-up to the major sesquicentennial commemoration. In April 2015, as part of the 150th anniversary of the Civil War's end, the park hosted the final "Long Road Home" event, drawing approximately 5,500 attendees as part of a broader five-day sesquicentennial that brought roughly 31,000 visitors to the county. The park provided parking and was connected to other venues by a town-run shuttle loop.

Also in April 2015, the county began openly discussing the option of selling approximately 350 acres of the park land—valued at roughly $2.3 million—as a revenue-generation measure. This marked a quiet but significant shift in orientation: from treating all 580+ acres as a future industrial asset, to acknowledging that some portion might need to be monetized to relieve budget pressure on county taxpayers.

Xpress Natural Gas and Infrastructure Payoff (2015–2018)

The most significant business recruitment success in the park's first fifteen years arrived in October 2015, when Xpress Natural Gas (XNG) announced plans to establish a compressed natural gas distribution facility on 5.6 acres in the Appomattox Center for Business and Commerce. The company planned to invest $7.8 million and create up to 50 jobs within 18-24 months of construction beginning (targeted for January-February 2016). Governor Terry McAuliffe attended the announcement ceremony, joined by over 100 local dignitaries.

The XNG announcement was notable for what it validated. County and EDA officials explicitly cited the park's cumulative infrastructure investments as the decisive factor: the Route 460 waterline (finally completed after years of controversy), sewer service, three-phase electric power, and fiber optic broadband through the Mid-Atlantic Broadband Cooperative network. XNG had chosen the ACBC over a competing North Carolina site. Appomattox County contributed $100,000 toward the project using Virginia Tobacco Commission funds.

Citizen critics who had for years argued that the waterline and infrastructure investments were unjustifiable expenses were, in this instance, effectively answered—though the gap between the park's original ambitions (large-scale manufacturing) and the reality of its tenants (a screen printer, a concrete plant, a web-store business, and now a natural gas distribution facility) remained evident.

In February 2018, the Appomattox County Board of Supervisors approved a natural gas pipeline to be installed within the county, with a condition that a tap for industrial use be included—a recognition that natural gas access was now central to industrial recruitment strategy.

The mid-to-late 2010s also saw more modest EDA activity: the county and town discussed (and ultimately declined) merging their separate EDAs; the town's EDA focused on downtown revitalization grants and blight abatement; and the county EDA maintained its focus on the ACBC, fielding occasional interest from site consultants but not closing major deals.

Recent Developments: Rock Festivals, Green Energy, and the Data Center Era (2019–2024)

In February 2020, the ACBC appeared in a new context: as a planned venue for the inaugural Blue Ridge Rock Festival, scheduled for September 2020 at the Community Park / Industrial Park complex. The festival, a multi-day metal and hard rock event, represented the park's emerging role as a large-format outdoor event destination—a use pattern that had begun with Civil War reenactments but was now reaching a different demographic and commercial model entirely. (The 2020 event was affected by the COVID-19 pandemic.)

The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020 and subsequent years dramatically reshaped national demand for two types of facilities that were directly relevant to Appomattox County's assets: on one hand, pandemic-era supply chain disruptions accelerated interest in domestic manufacturing and distribution; on the other, the explosion in remote work, streaming, cloud computing, and digital commerce drove unprecedented demand for data center capacity—particularly in Virginia, which had become the world's largest data center market concentrated in Northern Virginia's "Data Center Alley." Land costs, power constraints, and community resistance in Northern Virginia and the Raleigh-Durham corridor had begun pushing data center developers to seek alternative locations in the mid-2010s, a trend that accelerated sharply after 2020.

This is the macro-economic context in which the park's most transformative announcement unfolded. In December 2024, AVAIO Digital Partners announced that it had signed an agreement with the Appomattox County Economic Development Authority to purchase 452 acres at the ACBC for a $3 billion data center campus, to be powered by substantial onsite green power including 300 MW secured from CVEC and Dominion Energy. The site was described as "zoned, cleared, and ready for construction." AVAIO planned to build nearly 600 MW of total data center capacity by 2027 as part of a broader 1.2 GW global portfolio.

Governor Glenn Youngkin attended the announcement, framing the project as evidence of Virginia's strategy to expand its technology sector beyond Northern Virginia into rural communities. EDA Chairman Garet Bosiger stated that the authority had spent nearly two years working to secure the deal, and credited Appomattox County's "cooperative regulatory environment" as a differentiating factor compared to other sites AVAIO had considered.

The AVAIO announcement represented an extraordinary historical full-circle: the land purchased for $1.4 million a quarter century earlier—criticized repeatedly as a "pink elephant," a waste of taxpayer money, and a "dead" piece of ground—was about to host a $3 billion investment. The very infrastructure investments that had been the subject of years of contentious public debate—the fiber backbone courtesy of the Mid-Atlantic Broadband Cooperative (funded by Tobacco Commission dollars), the Route 460 waterline, the utility-ready site preparation—had positioned the ACBC to compete for the most capital-intensive class of investment in the 21st century economy.

Contextual Notes: Macroeconomic Headwinds and Regional Conditions

The history of the Appomattox Center for Business and Commerce cannot be understood apart from the macro-economic forces that shaped its development at nearly every turn.

Late 1990s / Land Purchase Era: The national economic boom of 1995-2000 created optimism about rural industrial recruitment but also inflated expectations. The ACBC was conceived during a period when industrial parks were widely viewed as reliable tools for rural economic development—a view that was already being challenged by research showing that many such parks sat underutilized.

2001-2003 Recession and Manufacturing Decline: The post-dot-com recession, combined with WTO-enabled Chinese manufacturing competition, devastated the furniture and textile industries that had been the backbone of Southside Virginia's economy. Appomattox was directly exposed through Thomasville Furniture. The ACBC's inability to recruit tenants in its early years was partly a product of conditions that no local authority could have controlled.

2002 Drought: Virginia's severe drought of 2002 was the precipitating event that turned the ACBC's water supply problem from a known constraint into an active crisis. It forced a decade-long public debate about the waterline from Campbell County, delayed business recruitment, and consumed enormous political energy. Former Governor Warner's executive order on water supply planning, issued in response, paradoxically helped Appomattox by making its regional waterline plan eligible for state grants and low-interest loans.

2005-2007 Expansion and Tobacco Commission Era: The mid-2000s recovery and the Virginia Tobacco Commission's substantial investments in rural infrastructure (including the $21 million for MBC broadband) gave the ACBC its first real competitive differentiator. The park's emergence as the Mid-Atlantic Broadband hub was not planned by local officials but was a strategic windfall that positioned the site for technology-oriented recruitment.

2008-2009 Great Recession: The financial crisis produced Thomasville Furniture's catastrophic layoffs—which reduced the plant from 600 to 350 workers in December 2008, then to closure by March 2011—and froze corporate investment that might otherwise have flowed to the park. The county was simultaneously dealing with a dramatic natural gas pipeline explosion and a stalled waterline project. The recession reinforced the county's fundamental economic vulnerability and made the case for the industrial park's infrastructure investment more urgent rather than less.

2010-2015 Slow Recovery: The slow, "jobless recovery" of 2010-2014 constrained county revenues at precisely the time when investment in the park's development was most needed. The on-again-off-again drama over the Economic Development Director position reflected genuine fiscal constraints. The completion of the waterline—whose history extended from the IDA's 2005 endorsement through years of town-county negotiations—finally provided the park with a credible water supply, enabling the 2015 XNG recruitment.

2015-2019 Data Center Demand Surge: Virginia's data center market, already the world's largest, began experiencing severe land and power constraints in Loudoun County ("Data Center Alley") by the mid-2010s. This scarcity drove site selectors to explore alternative locations with lower land costs, available power, and fiber connectivity—criteria the ACBC, with its MBC fiber trunk, multi-phase electric service, and large available acreage, increasingly met.

2020-2024 Post-COVID Data Center Explosion: The COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent digital transformation of the economy drove a step-change increase in global data center demand. Hyperscale operators, co-location providers, and independent developers scrambled for sites. Appomattox's combination of large-scale cleared acreage (rare in the East), existing fiber trunk infrastructure, state tax incentives for data centers, and cooperative local government made it a viable candidate for transformative investment. The AVAIO Digital agreement of December 2024, representing a $3 billion commitment for 452 acres, was the culmination of this convergence of local preparation and national demand.
