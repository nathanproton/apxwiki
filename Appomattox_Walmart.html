    The Appomattox Walmart is a Walmart Supercenter located at 505 Oakville Road in the Town of Appomattox, Virginia. The store's arrival was one of the most debated topics in Appomattox County between 2006 and 2010, generating years of public controversy over its potential impact on small businesses, the town's rural character, taxpayer-funded incentives, Civil War battlefield preservation, and the store's architectural design. Walmart announced its plans for the 99,581-square-foot store in April 2007, purchased the 27-acre site for $2.2 million in November 2008, and officially opened on May 19, 2010, employing approximately 210 workers. The Supercenter remains the largest retail establishment in Appomattox County.

    Background

    By the mid-2000s, Appomattox County had no major big-box retail presence. Residents who wanted discount shopping drove roughly twenty minutes to Walmart stores in Lynchburg, Madison Heights, or Farmville. The nearest Walmarts served as a regular draw for Appomattox shoppers, who combined the trips with doctor visits, car purchases, and mall shopping.[1] At the same time, the county's economy was under pressure: Thomasville Furniture, the town's largest private employer with approximately 600 workers, had been cutting jobs and restructuring since at least 2004.[2] The combination of limited local retail, rising gas prices, and economic anxiety created strong demand among many residents for a closer Walmart.

    Behind the scenes, the Town of Appomattox had been in discussions with Walmart as early as mid-2006. Town Council minutes from May 2007 reference a closed session held to discuss "the investment of public funds as incentives for the prospective location of Business A to Appomattox," a coded reference widely understood to mean Walmart.[3] Mayor John Wilson and the Town Council conducted these discussions confidentially, a decision that would later draw sharp criticism from residents who felt excluded from a consequential land-use decision.

    Early rumors and community reaction (2006–2007)

    Rumors of a potential Walmart began circulating in the fall of 2006, prompting a spirited exchange in the letters section of the Times-Virginian. In October 2006, resident Rick Thomas wrote that a Walmart would harm existing merchants, arguing that while low prices seemed attractive on the surface, the true cost would include closed businesses and the loss of Appomattox's rural appeal.[4] Another letter that month raised concerns about Walmart's employment practices, citing poverty-level wages and the deliberate use of part-time scheduling to avoid providing benefits.[5]

    In November 2006, Ms. Walton of Appomattox countered that Walmart would bring needed business and jobs to the area, drawing a comparison with the economic decline of Brookneal after major employers left that community.[6] Thomas responded that Appomattox lacked a comprehensive development plan, warning that uncontrolled growth from big-box chains would permanently alter the town.[7] A third writer argued that locally owned businesses were preferable because they reinvested profits in the community, while Walmart sent its earnings to corporate headquarters in Bentonville, Arkansas.[8]

    In December 2006, a resident linked the proposed Walmart directly to the county's contentious waterline project, asking whether the waterline along U.S. Route 460 was being built primarily to accommodate commercial development such as Walmart.[9] This suspicion—that public infrastructure was being laid to benefit a single corporate retailer—would resurface repeatedly over the following years.

    Official announcement (April 2007)

    On April 10, 2007, Walmart officially announced that it would locate a Supercenter in the Town of Appomattox, ending months of speculation. The Times-Virginian was the first media outlet to break the story. The 99,000-square-foot facility would be constructed on a 27-acre parcel between the Shoppes of Appomattox and the U.S. 460 West entry ramp, with a single entrance on State Route 26 (Oakville Road). Walmart projected the store would open in the fall of 2008 and create approximately 200 jobs.[10]

    Mayor John Wilson was effusive in his welcome. "On behalf of the Town of Appomattox, I am excited to welcome Wal-Mart to our community," Wilson said. "This new Wal-Mart Supercenter will create hundreds of new jobs, provide new services and serve as a significant tax revenue generator for our town."[10] Wilson also predicted the store would benefit local businesses by drawing new customers from surrounding areas, specifically citing Wilbun's Supermarket as a business that would gain from the increased foot traffic.[11]

    Walmart Senior Public Affairs Manager Kelly Hobbs told the newspaper the Supercenter was designed to fit the community. "We wanted to make sure it fitted in the community. But it still is a Supercenter, so they all get the same conveniences of a larger format store," Hobbs said.[12] The building was to have a brick front and cost between $11 and $12 million to construct. Approximately 70 percent of the 200 jobs would be full-time, with an average wage of $10.28 per hour and benefits including health insurance and a 401(k) plan.[10]

    George Joyner, Walmart's Market Manager, expressed optimism that the store could coexist with local businesses. "I think that there is enough business for everyone. We can't be everything to everyone," Joyner said.[12] Beckie Nix, the town's Director of Tourism, added that the Supercenter would draw shoppers from outside the area who would also patronize restaurants and buy gasoline locally.[11]

    Site plan and community briefings

    On April 19, 2007, Walmart held two informational meetings at the Appomattox Town Office on Linden Street, attended by more than 30 citizens. Crystal Hill, a project manager with the Richmond engineering firm Vanasse Hangen Brustlin, presented the site plan. The plan revealed that in addition to the Supercenter, approximately four acres had been set aside for two outparcels intended for restaurants or smaller retail businesses—sparking immediate speculation that chains like Applebee's or Ruby Tuesday might follow.[12]

    The site plan called for 500 parking spaces, energy-saving skylights that would automatically adjust interior lighting based on ambient light levels, and 50 percent green or open space. A connector road would link the Walmart complex to the adjacent Shoppes of Appomattox. The Virginia Department of Transportation approved a traffic study recommending a new signal on the eastbound ramp of U.S. 460 to manage the expected increase in traffic volume.[12]

    Town Manager David Garrett commended Walmart for its environmental efforts, specifically noting the installation of paper and plastic recycling bins. Hobbs assured citizens that the store would feature a design sensitive to the historical nature of Appomattox.[13]

    Criticism of secrecy

    The announcement immediately drew criticism for how it had been handled. In a pointed letter to the Times-Virginian, a resident demanded to know why Mayor Wilson and the Town Council had kept the Walmart negotiations from the public, arguing that Wilson had run on a platform of government transparency.

    "What deals have been made behind closed doors to welcome and court Wal-Mart to build here? Don't be fooled, this is what Wal-Mart does, convincing the local government to pay for expenses, achieving any zoning and land use changes quickly and easily, and contracting certain exclusions from taxes is all part of their routine when moving into an American town."[14]

    The letter also criticized the Times-Virginian for withholding what it knew about the Walmart negotiations, accusing the newspaper of failing to do independent journalistic reporting on a story of clear public interest. The writer urged citizens to educate themselves about the Planning Commission and attend public meetings, describing the deal as "nearly a done deal" by the time the public was informed.[14]

    Community debate and letters to the editor

    The Walmart question generated one of the most sustained public debates in Appomattox County's modern history, playing out primarily in the letters-to-the-editor pages of the Times-Virginian between 2006 and 2010. The debate divided along several fault lines: small-business concerns versus consumer convenience, rural preservation versus economic development, and the proper role of local government in courting or constraining corporate retailers.

    Opposition: small business and rural character

    Barbara Worley Shattuck of Spout Spring (and Middlesboro, Kentucky) emerged as one of the most persistent critics, writing multiple letters over several years. After the announcement, Shattuck argued that Appomattox should capitalize on its historical character and natural beauty rather than inviting big-box retail. "What Appomattox does not need is a Walmart. It does not need a lot of farmland turned into shopping centers along 460," she wrote in April 2007.[15] In later letters, Shattuck pledged to continue supporting Wilbun's Supermarket, Appomattox True Value Hardware, and Appomattox Drugstore, predicting that some of these stores would not survive Walmart's arrival.[16]

    Rick Thomas of Appomattox was another prominent critic. In an April 2007 letter, he directly challenged Mayor Wilson's claim that Wilbun's would benefit from Walmart, writing sarcastically that he could not find the economic theory to support it. "That's like saying cyanide will cure the common cold," Thomas wrote. He questioned whether the town had factored proposed tax increases into its Walmart enthusiasm and warned that every available space between Appomattox and Concord would eventually be commercially developed.[17]

    Other opponents raised concerns about the type of employment Walmart would provide. Critics noted that Walmart's definition of "full-time" might surprise residents, and questioned whether employees could afford the health insurance that was technically available on a wage of $10.28 per hour.[17] Letters predicted increased traffic, higher crime rates, and the permanent loss of Appomattox's distinctive small-town atmosphere.[18]

    Support: jobs and convenience

    Supporters countered that Walmart would bring sorely needed jobs during a period of economic hardship. In February 2009, as the national recession deepened and Thomasville Furniture continued to shed workers, one letter writer pointed to local layoffs and factory closures, asking opponents whether they could see the sunrise while their neighbors struggled to afford food and medicine.

    "I can assure you, from what I have found, people here would rather have a job than see the sunrise and set. I wonder how many of those who oppose a Wal-Mart have lost their jobs, been laid off, had their hours cut, or can't afford to buy food or medicine for their families?"[19]

    The same letter directly compared prices at local stores to Walmart's, noting that some items cost significantly more in Appomattox. A pro-Walmart resident in March 2009 wrote of waiting eagerly for the store's arrival, citing the convenience of not having to drive to Lynchburg for routine purchases.[20]

    Mayor Wilson and mayoral candidate Paul Harvey both voiced support for the project. Wilson argued the store would grow Appomattox as a retail center and tourist destination, while Harvey—who succeeded Wilson as mayor in mid-2008—told voters that Walmart would be an asset by increasing the tax base and saving elderly and retired citizens the cost of traveling to distant stores.[21]

    Historic battlefield concerns

    A distinctive thread in the opposition involved historic preservation. In May 2007, a letter to the Times-Virginian identified the Walmart site as ground fought over in the final days of the Civil War. The writer noted that the land had seen action between Federal cavalry under General Henry Davies and Confederate troopers under General Thomas Munford, including Company H of the 2nd Virginia Cavalry—the "Appomattox Rangers." The site was where Robert E. Lee's Army of Northern Virginia fired its last shots and suffered its last casualties, and Confederate dead had been buried on the ground slated for development.[22]

    The letter urged the community to work with Walmart to preserve at least a portion of the historic ground, noting that Walmart stores were easily accessible within thirty minutes in either direction from Appomattox. "Is this what they gave their lives for?" the writer asked.[22] Barbara Shattuck echoed the concern in a subsequent letter, expressing dismay at the "destruction of local history for cheap merchandise" and suggesting alternative sites.[23]

    The issue of historic graves resurfaced during the December 2009 design meeting, when Councilwoman Jennifer Jamerson-Scruggs asked about gravesite markers and their preservation. Town Manager Bart Van Nieuwenhuise reported that one grave had been relocated and another had not been found.[24]

    Delays and the fee waiver controversy (2008)

    Opening delayed to 2009

    In January 2008, Walmart announced that the originally projected Fall 2008 opening had been pushed back due to planning delays. The company was still seeking site plan approvals from VDOT and the Department of Conservation and Recreation, as well as final approval from the Town of Appomattox. Stream assessments for a tributary to Phelps Branch required restoration measures as part of joint permits from the Army Corps of Engineers and the Virginia Department of Environmental Quality.[25]

    By April 2008, rumors circulated that Walmart might abandon the project altogether. Town Manager David Garrett contacted Kelly Hobbs directly and received assurance that construction would proceed as planned, dispelling the cancellation rumors.[26]

    Water and sewer availability waiver

    The most contentious government action related to the Walmart project came on September 30, 2008, when the Appomattox Town Council voted 5–1 to grant Walmart's developer, Thalhimer, an $89,145 waiver on water and sewer availability fees. The vote took place at a workshop meeting after the item was added to the agenda as a last-minute amendment.[27]

    Town Manager David Garrett told the Council that the project was "in jeopardy of going away." The underlying issue was a series of unexpected infrastructure costs: a 10-inch water main near the Shoppes of Appomattox needed to be relocated, and a drainage pipe required enlargement. Because the Town did not own the sewer easement it believed it held, an adjacent property owner had to be approached, and that owner demanded the relocation of an access road. Garrett explained that Walmart's budget had already been exceeded twice.[28]

    Three citizens spoke against the waiver during a public comment session. Peter Zdgiebloski of Church Street told the Council he did not think it was appropriate to waive the fees, arguing that Walmart should be required to pay the same hookup charges as any other customer. Marshall Goin encouraged the Council not to give concessions to a corporation of Walmart's size.[27]

    Councilman Jimmy Mayberry made the motion, seconded by Karl Carter, to approve the waiver. The motion cited the facts that the Town had granted similar waivers before through a Memorandum of Understanding with Appomattox County for businesses in the Industrial Park, that Walmart was an asset to the community, and that the waiver would offset expenses the Town would otherwise have incurred. The vote was: Jamerson-Scruggs—aye, Carter—aye, Williams—aye, Mayberry—aye, McDearmon—aye. Baine cast the sole dissenting vote.[28]

    Councilman Bryan Baine argued that the waiver represented lost income that could have been used for water and sewer system repairs. He cited the Wall Street Journal's reporting that Walmart's global revenues were $374 billion during the 2008 fiscal year and contrasted this with the Town's budget. "They are asking for special treatment to absorb unexpected cost," he told the Council.[29]

    The decision sparked immediate backlash in letters to the editor. Rick Thomas accused the Council of caving to Walmart's demands, writing that the waiver was evidence of backroom deals. "Our citizens need to be aware of what the real cost of bringing Wal-Mart to Appomattox will be," Thomas wrote. "Only one Councilman, (Thank you, Mr. Baine) voted against issuing the waiver."[30] Another letter writer noted that Walmart had been stalling on the land purchase as a negotiation tactic.[31] Barbara Shattuck questioned how the town's water system—which had experienced shortages in prior years—could support the added demand of a Supercenter.[32]

    In June 2008, the state health department had in fact required assurance that the town's water system was adequate for fire protection before Walmart could proceed, specifically a guarantee that the system could sustain a four-hour fire flow.[33] This requirement fed into the broader narrative that the town's aging infrastructure was being strained to accommodate a single corporate retailer.

    Land purchase and permits (2008–2009)

    After three and a half months of on-again, off-again negotiations, Walmart Real Estate Business Trust finalized the purchase of 27.727 acres from Courtland Realty Associates in November 2008 for $2.2 million. Courtland Realty was an LLC of investors originally including the late John R. Lawson, the late Harry G. Lawson, the late Samuel R. Lawson, Joseph D. Ferguson, Frederick H. Lawson, and Samuel L. Ferguson III, who acted as Managing Partner. The land transaction was recorded in the Appomattox County Clerk of Circuit Court's office.[34]

    In May 2009, Walmart paid $12,000 for a building permit specifying a 99,581-square-foot, one-story facility with an occupancy load of 2,793 persons. The permit was issued by Appomattox County Building Official Lonny Hamlett. County Administrator Aileen Ferguson told the Board of Supervisors they should "see something happening in a couple of weeks."[35]

    Construction (2009–2010)

    Construction workers from Johnson Excavating, a subcontractor of C.L. Lewis, began site preparation on Oakville Road on June 28, 2009, starting with erosion control measures at the proposed entrance. C.L. Lewis served as the general contractor. Keith Morris, Walmart's Public Affairs Director, told the Times-Virginian that the store was targeted for a Spring 2010 opening, with hiring details expected to emerge 60 to 180 days before the opening date.[36]

    The reaction was again divided. A letter writer questioned the cost to taxpayers and asked what concessions the town had promised. Supporters pointed to the construction activity as evidence of progress during an economically difficult period, arguing that the store would stimulate the local economy when it was most needed.[37]

    Storefront design dispute (December 2009–January 2010)

    In late 2009, a new controversy erupted when it became apparent that Walmart had changed the Supercenter's exterior design from the original plans without informing the Town. During early discussions in 2007, Walmart had presented drawings showing a brick facade with full-height columns and a vestibule—details intended to give the store a historical appearance befitting Appomattox. The Town had a "wish list" of architectural features based on those presentations. However, no legally binding agreement specifying these materials had been signed.[38]

    In December 2009, Town Council members discovered that the building under construction featured painted split-face concrete masonry units rather than atlas brick, three-quarter-height columns rather than full-height ones, and no vestibule. Walmart had undergone a nationwide architectural review and adopted a standardized design for all stores under construction and future builds.[39]

    On December 14, 2009, the Town Council convened in closed session to consult with legal counsel regarding "the legal options available to the Town to address unapproved changes to the Wal-Mart building design."[40] A week later, on December 22, Council held a special meeting attended by Walmart's corporate attorney Tom Kline (Troutman & Sanders), architect Teresa Murphy (BRR Architects), Public Affairs representative Keith Morris, and Market Manager George Joyner.

    Councilwoman Jennifer Jamerson-Scruggs opened the discussion by noting that the original plans presented to Council had called for columns and a vestibule. "Although it is attractive it is not the store we were promised," she said. Councilman John "Plicky" Williams, referring to the concrete facade, said the appearance gave him "heartburn." Councilman Bryan Baine said he felt "a small bill of goods have been sold to them."[39]

    Kline acknowledged a "lapse in communication," explaining that Walmart had contacted the County about the design changes rather than the Town, owing to a jurisdictional confusion where the County handled building inspections while the Town handled zoning and site plan review. Architect Murphy explained that the full-height columns had been abandoned due to water-tightness issues, and that bricking over the smooth block sides would be cost-prohibitive.[24]

    The dispute generated yet another round of letters to the editor. One writer criticized the three dissenting council members—Jamerson-Scruggs, Williams, and Baine—for making "trivial, inconsequential complaints" that could drive away future businesses. "Think about it … because of a totally uncalled for hassle with Wal-Mart, our town could be left with a huge mud-hole, as well as an unfinished building," wrote June Goodrich of Appomattox.[41]

    Nina Beasley of Appomattox called on voters to defeat the three critical council members in the upcoming May 2010 town elections, writing: "Instead of being seen as a business friendly community, there is a good chance that these comments will cause Appomattox to be seen as a business nuisance community."[42] Another resident pointed out that Councilman Williams was adding to his own home without brick and columns, and expressed bemusement at Councilwoman Jamerson-Scruggs's vision of "an elegant looking Wal-Mart."[38]

    Barbara Shattuck, meanwhile, maintained her stance: "I may, one day, have to eat my words and shop at Wally World when all the other stores have closed. In the meantime, there is nothing at Wally World that I want or need that I can't find elsewhere."[16]

    The dispute was resolved in early January 2010 when Walmart agreed to install full-length columns, garden center fencing, and a screen for the loading dock. Following a January 8, 2010 follow-up meeting attended by Councilmembers Jamerson-Scruggs and Mayberry along with Walmart representatives and architect Brad Moore, Town Manager Van Nieuwenhuise confirmed the revised drawings met the Town's intent. "At no time was an actual stop work order or litigation considered," the Town stated in a press release, looking forward to the grand opening in May.[43]

    The design saga would echo years later. At an August 2016 joint public hearing, a council member referenced the Walmart experience as a cautionary tale about informal agreements: "Our dear friends at Walmart, we had that gentleman's agreement that we are going to have painted red brick—now guess what it is not. The point I am trying to make is that all of this needs to be documented."[44]

    Infrastructure upgrades

    The Walmart project prompted several infrastructure improvements in the immediate area. The most visible was a new traffic signal at the intersection of Business 460 and the entrance ramp from U.S. 460 East. Don Austin, the VDOT Residency Administrator, confirmed that Walmart conducted a traffic impact analysis indicating the signal was necessary due to projected traffic increases. Walmart covered all associated costs, estimated between $150,000 and $200,000. The signal was to operate continuously, 24 hours a day, without reverting to a blinking yellow during low-traffic periods. Austin said no other traffic signals were expected to be needed for the Walmart opening.[45]

    A bridge was installed to provide access via a connector road linking the Supercenter complex with the Shoppes of Appomattox, allowing motorists to reach the store from U.S. 460 without using the Oakville Road entrance.[45]

    Beyond the immediate site, Supervisor Tom Conrad argued during the summer of 2008 that the pending Walmart underscored the urgency of the county's waterline project from Concord. Conrad stressed that delays in the waterline could jeopardize Walmart's interest and that the waterline would also benefit the county's industrial park.[46] The state health department's requirement that the town demonstrate adequate fire-flow capacity before Walmart could begin construction further linked the store to the county's broader water infrastructure challenges.[33]

    The Walmart site was also integrated into recreational planning. Phase I of the Appomattox Heritage and Recreational Trail was designed to connect the Community Park on Police Tower Road with the area behind Walmart on Oakville Road. By late 2009, the county had secured approximately $273,000 in grants toward the $677,000 project, though the Board of Supervisors ultimately postponed funding due to the economic downturn.[47]

    Grand opening (May 2010)

    The Walmart Supercenter at 505 Oakville Road opened to the public on May 19, 2010. The Appomattox County Chamber of Commerce hosted an official ribbon-cutting ceremony at 7:30 a.m., attended by Walmart staff, citizens, and local dignitaries. Prior to the ribbon-cutting, the crowd performed a Walmart cheer.[48]

    Store manager Denzil VanSwearingen, an Appomattox native who had worked for Walmart for seven years, called the opening a dream come true. "I have a feeling that they are happy that we are here and we are happy to be here," VanSwearingen said. Market Manager George Joyner acknowledged the personal significance: "This is his dream come true. It is not every day that you get a chance to place a resident in a store."[48]

    Shoppers flocked to the store on opening day. Shirley Lovelace, one of the first customers, said she had previously driven to the Wards Road Walmart in Lynchburg. Sue Robinson purchased a pack of chewing gum just to be among the first checkout customers. "Now I do not have to go to Lynchburg or Farmville. I've been waiting for this for a long time," Robinson said.[48]

    Three days before the grand opening, the Walmart Foundation presented $16,000 in grants to seventeen local organizations during a VIP walk-through on May 16. Recipients included Appomattox County Public Schools ($4,500), Appomattox Community Park and Trail ($1,000), and the Appomattox County High School memorial garden ($500).[49]

    The store employed approximately 210 workers and operated seven days a week from 6 a.m. to midnight. Full-time employees worked 34 to 40 hours per week.[48]

    Aftermath and legacy

    The arrival of Walmart had the mixed effects both supporters and critics had predicted. At the June 4, 2007 meeting of the Board of Supervisors, Supervisor Sam Carter observed that the introduction of Walmart would "have a domino effect on our lives one way or another," noting it would increase the tax base but also generate more solid waste—a factor he cited in supporting the county's entry into the Region 2000 Regional Solid Waste Authority.[50]

    Wilbun's Supermarket, whose survival Mayor Wilson had assured residents of, closed in 2010, not long after the Walmart opening—a result that critics had foretold and that supporters attributed to broader economic forces.[51] The Triangle Plaza shopping center, already described as "almost empty" before Walmart's arrival, continued to decline. However, Walmart became the county's largest retail employer and a significant contributor to the local tax base through meals tax, lodging tax, and property taxes.

    Over the following years, Walmart became an integrated part of county life. The Appomattox County Sheriff's Department regularly provided security officers at the store, with Walmart reimbursing the county for overtime costs—a recurring item in the Board of Supervisors' supplemental appropriations.[52] The Walmart Foundation's "Shop With a Cop" program provided annual grants for holiday gift-giving through the Sheriff's Department.[53]

    The hoped-for restaurant outparcels adjacent to the Walmart site did not materialize as quickly as speculated. The two four-acre outparcels remained graded but undeveloped for years after the store opening.

    In a broader sense, the Walmart debate served as a proxy for deeper questions about Appomattox County's identity and future: whether a rural community defined by its Civil War heritage and small-town atmosphere could or should embrace big-box retail development, and who should have a voice in those decisions. The letters to the editor from this period remain among the most extensive public dialogues in the newspaper's archives.
